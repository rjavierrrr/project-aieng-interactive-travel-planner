<a href="/wiki/Braid" title="Braid">braiding</a> and twisting lengths of <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">thread</a>, which are wound on <a href="#Bobbins">bobbins</a> to manage them. As the work progresses, the weaving is held in place with pins set in a <a href="/wiki/Lace_pillow" class="mw-redirect" title="Lace pillow">lace pillow</a>, the placement of the pins usually determined by a pattern or pricking pinned on the pillow.\n</p><p>Bobbin lace is also known as <b>pillow lace</b>, because it was worked on a pillow, and <b>bone lace</b>, because early bobbins were made of <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>.\n</p><p>Bobbin lace is one of the two major categories of handmade laces, the other being <a href="/wiki/Needle_lace" title="Needle lace">needle lace</a>, derived from earlier <a href="/wiki/Cutwork" title="Cutwork">cutwork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reticella" title="Reticella">reticella</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levey_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levey-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span Isabella Parasole</figcaption></figure>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg/220px-BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg/330px-BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg/440px-BLW_Bobbin_lace_-_detail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a><figcaption>BLW Bobbin lace - detail</figcaption></figure>\n<p>A will of 1493 by the Milanese <a href="/wiki/House_of_Sforza" title="House of Sforza">Sforza</a> family mentions lace created with twelve bobbins.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There are two books that represent the early known pattern descriptions for bobbin lace, <i>Le Pompe</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and <i>N\xc3\xbcw Modelbuch</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" class="mw-redirect" title="Z\xc3\xbcrich">Z\xc3\xbcrich</a>. Other popular lace pattern books were produced by <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Parasole" title="Isabella Parasole">Isabella Parasole</a>, which included patterns for reticella, needle lace and bobbin lace designs. Other pattern books of this period include Cesare Vecellio<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and Bartolomeo Danieli.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Bobbin lace evolved from <a href="/wiki/Passementerie" title="Passementerie">passementerie</a> or <a href="/wiki/Braid" title="Braid">braid</a>-making in 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levey_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levey-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> was famous for its braids, hence it is not surprising to find bobbin lace developed in the city. It traveled along with the Spanish troops through Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-reigate_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reigate-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Coarse <i>passements</i> of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>-wrapped threads or colored <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silks</a> gradually became finer, and later bleached <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> yarn was used to make both braids and edgings.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The making of bobbin lace was easier to learn than the elaborate cutwork of the 16th century, and the tools and materials for making linen bobbin lace were inexpensive.  There was a ready market for bobbin lace of all qualities, and women throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> soon took up the craft which earned a better income than <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">spinning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewing" title="Sewing">sewing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> or other home-based <a href="/wiki/Textile_arts" title="Textile arts">textile arts</a>.  Bobbin lace-making was established in <a href="/wiki/Charity_school" title="Charity school">charity schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almshouse" title="Almshouse">almshouses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Levey_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levey-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 17th century, the textile centers of <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> eclipsed Italy as the premiere sources for fine bobbin lace, but until the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> and the coming of mechanization hand-lacemaking continued to be practiced throughout Europe, suffering only in those periods of simplicity when lace itself fell out of fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-Levey_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levey-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Some skilled lace makers work to re-create older lace patterns based on the period portraiture and extant lace samples.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On paintings that have sufficient detail, entire pieces can be reconstructed by lacemakers who understand the early structural techniques and details.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Materials">Materials</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobbin_lace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Materials"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Bobbin lace may be made with coarse or fine threads. Traditionally it was made with <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, or, later, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> threads, or with precious metals. <a href="/wiki/Bess_of_Hardwick" title="Bess of Hardwick">Bess of Hardwick</a> bought red silk, gold, and silver thread for making "bone lace" in 1549, the earliest English reference to this kind of work.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Today bobbin lace is made with a variety of <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">natural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">synthetic fibers</a> and with wire and other filaments.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Even bobbin lace made from human hair, <span title="French-language text"><i lang="fr">point tresse</i></span>, was once popular as a personal memento.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Structure">Structure</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobbin_lace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Elements of bobbin lace may include <a href="/wiki/Toile" title="Toile">toile</a> or <i>toil\xc3\xa9</i> (clothwork), <i>r\xc3\xa9seau</i> (the net-like <a href="/wiki/Bobbin_lace_ground" title="Bobbin lace ground">ground</a> of continuous lace), fillings of <a href="/wiki/Part_lace" title="Part lace">part laces</a>, tapes, <a href="/wiki/Gimp_(thread)" title="Gimp (thread)">gimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Picot" title="Picot">picots</a>, tallies, ribs and rolls. Lacemaking groups still meet in regions as varied as <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devonshire</a>, England and <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_California" title="Orange County, California">Orange (Farminh\xc3\xa3o),<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> being known as <i>Renda de to make bobbin lace are a pillow, bobbins, pins and prickings.  The part laces also require a crochet hook, very fine types of lace require very fine hooks. There are different types of pillows and bobbins linked to areas, eras and type of lace.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Bobbins">Bobbins</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobbin_lace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bobbins"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Bobbins, which are traditionally made of wood or bone, are used to hold the thread. They come in different shapes, often associated with certain types of lace. The parts of a bobbin are the neck, which is where the thread is wound, a head, where thread is hitched to keep it from coming unwound, and the shank, which is used as a handle. Bobbins from England may also have a beaded spangle at the end of the shank, which makes the bobbin heavier and helps with tensioning the thread.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Bobbins are usually 3 1/2 - 4 inches long, though they may be shorter or longer.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Bobbins are wound and used in pairs. Bobbin collection is a common aspect of the hobby for many lace makers. Within the lace community, commemorative bobbins designating annual meetings, special anniversaries, or historic events are frequently offered which become collector\'s items.\n</p><p>There are many types of bobbins, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Belgian bobbins: They have a single head and a bulbous rounding near the end of the shank that helps with tensioning threads.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li>Binche bobbins: The round bulb near the end of the shank is small, making these bobbins good for fine, straight laces.</li>\n<li>East Midlands bobbins: These double-headed bobbins are slender and spangled. They are also called Bucks or Midlands bobbins.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li>Honiton bobbins: Honiton bobbins are straight below the single head, and the end of the shank comes to a blunt point, which helps with sewing. They may be called a lace stick.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li>Square bobbins: Square bobbins have a shank with flattened sides, which makes it easier to keep them from rolling on the pillow.</li>\n<li>Portuguese bobbins: The bobbin is an elongated pear-shaped wooden artefact where the thread is wrapped.</li>\n<li>Bone bobbins are sometimes decorated with names, commemorative text, or other messages and patterns</li></ul>\n<div style="clear:right;" class=""></div>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">\n\t\t\t<div class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobbin_lace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types of pillow"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg/220px-Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg/330px-Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg/440px-Sweden_1775_lace_roller_pillow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2566" /></a><figcaption>"A lady sits and reads, the chambermaid comes with tea" 1775 roller pillow</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The pillows must be firm, or otherwise the pins will wobble. The pillows were traditionally stuffed with straw, but nowadays polystyrene (styrofoam) is generally used.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>An early type of pillow can be seen in <a href="/wiki/The_Lace_Maker" title="The Lace Maker">The Lace Maker</a> by <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Netscher" title="Caspar Netscher">Caspar Netscher</a>. The pillow has a wooden frame, and is slightly sloping. The lace-maker rests it on her lap. Another representation of the similar style of pillow is found in the painting <a href="/wiki/The_Lacemaker_(Vermeer)" title="The Lacemaker (Vermeer)">The Lacemaker</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Vermeer" title="Johannes Vermeer">Johannes Vermeer</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Lace-Maker_(Metsu)" title="The Lace-Maker (Metsu)">The Lace-Maker</a> portrait by <a href="/wiki/Gabri%C3%ABl_Metsu" title="Gabri\xc3\xabl Metsu">Gabri\xc3\xabl Metsu</a> was memorialized in a <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bolster" title="Bolster">bolster</a> or cylindrical pillow was much cheaper to make as it is just a fabric bag stuffed with straw. It was used in <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire_lace" title="Bedfordshire lace">Bedfordshire lace</a>. It needs a stand as it does not have a flat bottom.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Usually the bolster had the pattern pinned round the cylinder, so by turning the pillow, the lace could be as long as was needed. However, <a href="/wiki/Maltese_lace" title="Maltese lace">Maltese lacemakers</a> used the pillow the other way. They had a long thin pillow, which they rested against something. Then they worked the lace down the length of the pillow.<sup id="cite_ref-crafts_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crafts-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>This problem (of the lace needing to be longer than the pillow) is solved in a different way by the roller pillow, which has a small roller, for working the lace, set into a larger area, where the bobbins are laid. This means that the pattern can be pinned round the roller, but the pillow has a flat bottom.\n</p><p>The cheapest modern pillow is domed and made of polystyrene (styrofoam). It is often called a cookie pillow, because of its shape. Another modern pillow is a block pillow, with a frame which holds covered polystyrene blocks. The blocks can be moved around as the lace progresses, to keep the lace being worked on at the centre of the pillow.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">\n\t\t\t<div class="thumb" style="width: 150px; Most lacemakers belong to <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">regional guilds</a> within their country of origin. Guilds can be devoted to one kind of lace, often that which developed locally, or may include makers of all kinds. In the United States, most guilds are organized within chapters of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://internationalorganizationoflace.org/">International Organization of Lace</a>, which also includes Canadian lace guilds. Quarterly publications of <i>"The Bulletin"</i> journal provide articles about current projects and events, historical research, annual meeting details, patterns, and more. Internationally, the <i>Organisation Internationale de la Dentelle au Fuseau et \xc3\xa0 l\'Aiguille</i> (OIDFA, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.oidfa.com/">International Bobbin and Needle Lace Organization</a>) is the primary governing and networking body for lacemakers. OIDFA organizes annual global congresses, regional fairs, and local gatherings to promote the appreciation and knowledge of lacemaking.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2
