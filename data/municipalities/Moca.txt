.IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">Spanish pronunciation:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="es-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">&#91;\xcb\x88moka&#93;</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Moca_barrio-pueblo" title="Moca barrio-pueblo">town</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Municipalities of Puerto Rico">municipality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, located in the north-western region of the island, north of <a href="/wiki/A%C3%B1asco,_Puerto_Rico" title="A\xc3\xb1asco, Puerto Rico">A\xc3\xb1asco</a>; southeast of <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguadilla, Puerto Rico">Aguadilla</a>; east of <a href="/wiki/Aguada,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguada, Puerto Rico">Aguada</a>; and west of <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Sebasti\xc3\xa1n, Puerto Rico">San Sebasti\xc3\xa1n</a>. Moca is spread over 12 barrios and <a href="/wiki/Moca_barrio-pueblo" title="Moca barrio-pueblo">Moca Pueblo</a> (the downtown area and the administrative center). It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla-Isabela-San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_Metropolitan_Statistical_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Aguadilla-Isabela-San Sebasti\xc3\xa1n Metropolitan Statistical Area">Aguadilla-Isabela-San Sebasti\xc3\xa1n Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>.\n</p><p>The name comes from the Moca tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Andira_inermis" title="Andira inermis">Andira inermis</a>)</i> which are very common in this region.\n</p><p>The Moca tree was officially adopted as the representative tree of the town on February 19, 1972. Moca is famous for its <a href="/wiki/Mundillo" title="Mundillo">Mundillo</a> lace. Mundillo is a Puerto Rican-style of handmade <a href="/wiki/Bobbin_lace" title="Bobbin lace">bobbin lace</a>. <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Mundillo</i></span> almost synonymous with the small town of Moca.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moca,_Puerto_Rico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Visittomoca.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Visittomoca.jpg/200px-Visittomoca.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Visittomoca.jpg/300px-Visittomoca.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Visittomoca.jpg/400px-Visittomoca.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a><figcaption>The Labadie Mansion inspired <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Laguerre" title="Enrique Laguerre">Enrique Laguerre</a> to write La Llamarada. The property was restored as a museum and renamed the <a href="/wiki/Palacete_Los_Moreau" title="Palacete Los Moreau">Palacete Los Moreau</a>, in honor of Laguerre\'s fictional characters. </figcaption></figure>\n<p>Moca, known as <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">La Capital del Mundillo</i></span> (The Mundillo Capital), is famous for its lace or <i><a href="/wiki/Mundillo" title="Mundillo">Mundillo</a></i>. It was founded by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_de_Qui%C3%B1ones&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 de Qui\xc3\xb1ones (page does not exist)">Don Jos\xc3\xa9 de Qui\xc3\xb1ones</a> on June 22, 1772. Diverse versions exist on the date of its foundation. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_de_Ubeda_and_Delgado&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Manuel de Ubeda and Delgado (page does not exist)">Manuel de Ubeda and Delgado</a>, in his "<i>Isla de Puerto Rico. "Estudio hist\xc3\xb3rico, geogr\xc3\xa1fico y estad\xc3\xadstico"</i>, published in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> in 1878, says Moca was founded in 1774. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Cayetano_Coll_y_Toste" title="Cayetano Coll y Toste">Cayetano Coll y Toste</a>, in the "<i>Bolet\xc3\xadn hist\xc3\xb3rico de Puerto Rico</i>", maintains it was founded on June 22, 1772.\n</p><p>Puerto Rico was ceded by <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a> under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_of_1898" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Paris of 1898">Treaty of Paris of 1898</a> and became an unincorporated territory of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.In 1899, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">United States Department of War</a> conducted a <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of Puerto Rico finding that the population of Moca was 12,410.<sup id="cite_ref-OfficeSanger1900_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OfficeSanger1900-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 16, 2010, Moca was the epicenter of a strong 5.8 <a href="/wiki/2010_Moca_earthquake" title="2010 Moca earthquake">earthquake</a>. The earthquake was felt in the entire island and also in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>. Damage was reported in various towns.\n</p><p>On September 20, 2017 <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> struck Puerto Rico. In Moca, landslides and the flooding of the R\xc3\xado Culebrinas caused major destruction to bridges, roads and homes.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USGS_Maria_Landslides_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_Maria_Landslides-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USGS_Maria_Landslides_map_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_Maria_Landslides_map-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> About 1300 homes were impacted by landslides and flooding, bridges collapsed, and residents were left without access to electrical power, telecommunication services and basic necessities. Close to a month and a half later, 25% of the 31,117 residents of Moca had electrical power and access to drinking water and 75% did not.<sup id="cite_ref-END_Moca_6-0" class="reference"><a Although there have been reports of vampire-like attacks on farm animals from as early as the 1930s, <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">El vampiro de Moca</i></span> legend officially began on February 25, 1975, when newspapers reported that fifteen cows, three goats, two geese and a pig were found with puncture marks on their blood-drained bodies in <a href="/wiki/Rocha,_Moca,_Puerto_Rico" title="Rocha, Moca, Puerto Rico">Rocha, Moca</a>. These events were also connected to <a href="/wiki/Unidentified_flying_object" title="Unidentified flying object">UFO sightings</a> and other supernatural reports by residents of Moca. Similar events were reported throughout 1975 in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Corozal,_Puerto_Rico" title="Corozal, Puerto Rico">Corozal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fajardo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Fajardo, Puerto Rico">Fajardo</a> and even rural parts of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The legend resurfaced in the 1990s after similar reports came from <a href="/wiki/Can%C3%B3vanas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Can\xc3\xb3vanas, Puerto Rico">Can\xc3\xb3vanas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Government">Government</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moca,_Puerto_Rico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Government"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mayoralty_in_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayoralty in Puerto Rico">Mayoralty in Puerto Rico</a></div>\n<p>Like all municipalities in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, Moca is administered by a mayor. The rectangular flag consists of a magenta equilateral triangular field, the color of the <a href="/wiki/Andira_inermis" title="Andira inermis">Moca tree</a> flower. In this field appear five-point stars, silver-plated, surrounding a greater gold star, also with five points.<sup id="cite_ref-LexJuris_(Leyes_y_Jurisprudencia)_de_Puerto_Rico_2020_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LexJuris_(Leyes_y_Jurisprudencia)_de_Puerto_Rico_2020-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moca,_Puerto_Rico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Coat of arms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>It has oblong form. Divided in a silver-plated field and blue sky united by a purple rhombus (diamond shape), the color of the Moca flower. The rhombus has religious symbolisms. The rhombus is surrounded, in its inferior part, by two branches of the Moca tree; in its superior part, an arc of eleven silver-plated five-point stars. Within the rhombus is a gold monogram (of the <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Virgin Mary</a>) topped by a Christian crown of the same metal. A silver-lined crown in form of a three-tower castle crowns the shield. On the frontal portion of the crown, carved in gold, the word <i>Moca</i>. The stones of the castle are lined in blue.
