The area where the refinery is located has been used for the growth and processing of sugarcane since the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Cortada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-Central_Cortada_in_Santa_Isabel%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="853" data-file-height="1280" /></a><figcaption>Ruins of the chimney and refinery of the plantation.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The area where Central Cortada is located was originally called <i>Estancia <a href="/wiki/Descalabrado" title="Descalabrado">Descalabrado</a>,</i> owned by <a href="/wiki/Catalans" title="Catalans">Catalan</a> settlers named Juan de Quintana (from 1737 to 1789) and later Juan Cortada Manzo (from 1800 to 1865), who build the <i><a href="/wiki/Trapiche" title="Trapiche">trapiche</a></i>. The Cortada family kept operating the farm as part of their crop financing business, the <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce</a>-based <i>Cortada &amp; Cia</i>. This company kept growing and acquiring new <i>haciendas</i> in the area, such as Hacienda Palmarito in 1868. The cholera epidemic of 1855-1856, which killed many <i>estancia</i> slaves, and the <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism" title="Abolitionism">abolition of slavery</a> in 1873 drastically transformed Puerto Rico\'s economy and impacted the sugarcane industry at the time. This period lasted through the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a> until the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Santa Isabel Sugar Company</i> was founded in 1918 to build the infrastructure necessary to modernize and develop the sugarcane industry in the former <i>estancia</i>. This company was founded in 1918 and was owned by the Cortada family and shareholders J. C. Mc Cormick Hartman, Hugh Guill\xc3\xa9n, Isidro Abarca, Antonio \xc3\x81lvarez, Francisco Verges, George T. Parker, Leopoldo Cabassa, Antonio Alcaide, and Rafael Fabi\xc3\xa1n. The director was Juan Cortada Tirado. Most of the sugarcane workers at this time were poor peasants who would come from the mountainous areas in search of work and alternative employment. These workers would often be called <i>colonos</i> (<a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">Spanish</a> for "colonists") as the former slave-operated <i>estancias</i> and the communities that grew around them were now referred to as <i>colonias</i> or "colonies".\n</p><p>In the 1930s, the refinery was sold to the <i>Central Aguirre Sugar Company</i>, which operated the <a href="/wiki/Central_Aguirre_Historic_District" title="Central Aguirre Historic District">Central Aguirre</a> refinery in <a href="/wiki/Guayama,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayama, Puerto Rico">Guayama</a>. The refinery ceased operations in 1940 and due to grinding restrictions it remained closed throughout the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. It opened again in 1944, and continued to operate until the 1970s. In 1970 it produced 16,968 tons of sugar, and it remained operational until 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span
