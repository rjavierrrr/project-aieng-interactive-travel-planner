Puerto Rico-Guajataca Tunnel-Construction circa 1904\xe2\x80\x931905</figcaption></figure>\n<p><b>Guajataca Tunnel</b> is a railroad tunnel that connected the town of <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebradillas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Quebradillas, Puerto Rico">Quebradillas, Puerto Rico</a>. The tunnel is one of the most significant work of the remnants of the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Puerto_Rico#National_railroad_system" title="Rail transport in Puerto Rico">national railway system</a> that connected the island during the first half of the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Lex_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lex-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  In 2000, the Government of Puerto Rico declared it a historical monument.<sup id="cite_ref-Lex_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lex-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guajataca_Tunnel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The <i>American Railroad Co. of Puerto Rico</i> began construction of the tunnel around 1904 during the expansion of the northern line towards the west of the island. The tunnel connected the towns of <a href="/wiki/Quebradillas" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebradillas">Quebradillas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Guajataca_River" title="Guajataca River">Guajataca River</a> canyon. The construction was completed with the excavation of two tunnels on each side of the canyon. They were joined by a steel viaduct 250 feet (76.2&#160;m) long at an elevation of 131 feet (40&#160;m) above the river level.<sup id="cite_ref-Lex_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lex-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The tunnel is open to the public but it is fairly isolated.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The tunnel leads to "Guajataca beach" known for its white sand, raging surf, and dangerous turbulent waters.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Concerts are sometimes held at the Guajataca Tunnel.<sup id="cite_ref-Discovering_Puerto_Rico_2019_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discovering_Puerto_Rico_2019-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nIn 2019, virtual tours of Guajataca Tunnel, were organized by Discover Puerto Rico, the marketing arm of the Puerto Rico Department of Tourism.<sup
