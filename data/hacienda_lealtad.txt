</span>es.pe\xcb\x88\xc9\xbe\xc3\xa3n.sa&#93;</a></span>) is a historic <a href="/wiki/Coffee_plantation" class="mw-redirect" title="Coffee plantation">coffee plantation</a> in barrio <a href="/wiki/La_Torre,_Lares,_Puerto_Rico" title="La Torre, Lares, Puerto Rico">La Torre, Lares, Puerto Rico</a>. A large <a href="/wiki/Hacienda" title="Hacienda">hacienda</a>, it was founded in 1830, by Juan Bautista Plumey, a French immigrant, who arrived in Puerto Rico with enslaved people. (Juan Bautista Plumey was born in France on September 8, 1797, and named Jean Baptiste Henri Plumey.)<sup id="cite_ref-Plumey_on_Ancestry_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plumey_on_Ancestry-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It would become the largest coffee plantation in Lares, with over thirty slaves and hundreds of day laborers working the 69 <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">cuerdas</i></span> of coffee farm. For many years the plantation was a large producer and exporter of coffee. <a href="/wiki/Day_laborers" class="mw-redirect" title="Day laborers">Day laborers</a>, <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">jornaleros</i></span> or <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">braceros</i></span> from Lares worked in the coffee fields of the <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">hacienda</i></span>. In 1880, it was owned by  Miguel Marquez Ense\xc3\xb1at.\n</p><p>It is now owned by Edwin Soto and his family, who invested millions into its restoration and operate the hacienda as a hotel, coffee shop and a <a href="/wiki/Living_museum" title="Living museum">living museum</a> recreating the historical setting of the height of coffee production in Puerto Rico. Coffee growing in Puerto Rico has seen a resurgence and Hacienda Lealtad produces coffee under the brand Caf\xc3\xa9 Lealtad. The <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Caf\xc3\xa9 Bistro Hacienda Lealtad</i></span> on <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Highway_128" title="Puerto Rico Highway 128">Puerto Rico Highway 128</a> kilometer 55.8, is where groups meet for the start of their tour of the 19th century coffee plantation.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Brief_history">Brief history</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hacienda_Lealtad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Brief history"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad,_Lares,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-Gl%C3%A1cil_o_secadero_de_caf%C3%A9_en_Hacienda_Lealtad%2C_Lares%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1050" /></a><figcaption>The "Gl\xc3\xa1cil" is the area where 21 female slaves worked, setting the coffee out under the sun to dry.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Lares was founded on April 26, 1827, and became an important location of coffee production in Puerto Rico. In 1830, Juan Bautista Plumey established Hacienda Lealtad, the coffee producing farm. Plumey, a French immigrant<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> had come to Lares with 32 slaves. <i>(Pepino)</i> and they had twelve children.<sup id="cite_ref-KonetzkeKellenbenz1992_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KonetzkeKellenbenz1992-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span Soto, a Puerto Rican businessman from Lares, who had picked ripe coffee beans there as a child. Soto decided he would buy the hacienda as a second home.<sup id="cite_ref-YalixRiveraCruz2012_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YalixRiveraCruz2012-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While Soto\'s initial intentions for the property, when he purchased it in 2007, were to keep it as a second home he later decided to restore it for its <a href="/wiki/Historic" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic">historic</a> importance. Soto invested millions of dollars into its restoration and <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Hacienda Lealtad</i></span> is now a living museum, a hotel, coffee shop, offering educational tours to local and international tourists. It also serves as a place for people in the agriculture and coffee industry to meet and hold workshops.\n</p><p>In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Agriculture" title="Puerto Rico Department of Agriculture">Puerto Rico Department of Agriculture</a> began an initiative to develop <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_tourism" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural tourism">agricultural tourism</a> and in 2016, <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Hacienda Lealtad</i></span> was endorsed by the Puerto Rico Tourism Company (<span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Compa\xc3\xb1ia de Turismo de Puerto Rico</i></span>). Since the start of its restoration Caf\xc3\xa9 Lealtad, Inc., as the company is called, has purchased coffee seeds and other equipment and services from the Puerto Rico Department of Agriculture.<sup Clean-up, restoration and planting began anew soon afterwards and tours and functions at the hacienda resumed.\n</p><p>A few months after Hurricane Maria struck, a meeting was held by the Secretary of
