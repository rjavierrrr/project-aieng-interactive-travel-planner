Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>El Toro Wilderness</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es"><b>Selva El Toro</b></i>) is a 10,254-acre (41.5&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) federally designated <a href="/wiki/National_Wilderness_Preservation_System" title="National Wilderness Preservation System">National Wilderness Preservation System</a> unit located within <a href="/wiki/El_Yunque_National_Forest" title="El Yunque National Forest">El Yunque National Forest</a> (formerly known as the Caribbean National Forest) on the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Luquillo" title="Sierra de Luquillo">Sierra de Luquillo</a> in eastern <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.  El Toro, named after the <a href="/wiki/El_Toro_(Sierra_de_Luquillo)" title="El Toro (Sierra de Luquillo)">highest peak</a> in the forest at 3,524 feet (1,074&#160;m), is the only tropical wilderness in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Forest">United States National Forest</a> System.<sup id="cite_ref-usfs_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usfs-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was created in 2005 by the Caribbean National Forest Act of 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In descending order of land area, the wilderness is located in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Municipalities of Puerto Rico">municipalities</a> of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Grande,_Puerto_Rico" title="R\xc3\xado Grande, Puerto Rico">R\xc3\xado Grande</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naguabo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Naguabo, Puerto Rico">Naguabo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Las_Piedras,_Puerto_Rico" title="Las Piedras, Puerto Rico">Las Piedras</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Can%C3%B3vanas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Can\xc3\xb3vanas, Puerto Rico">Can\xc3\xb3vanas</a>. El Toro Wilderness, along with the <a href="/wiki/Luquillo_Experimental_Forest" title="Luquillo Experimental Forest">Luquillo Experimental Forest</a> (LEF), is part of the <b>Luquillo Biosphere Reserve</b>, a designated <a href="/wiki/Man_and_the_Biosphere_Programme" title="Man and the Biosphere Programme">UNESCO biosphere reserve</a>, which emphasizes the site\xe2\x80\x99s importance in the global protection of biodiversity through conservation action.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Features">Features</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span /></a><figcaption></figcaption></figure><p>El Toro Wilderness has dense vegetation with mixed evergreen plants ranging from 3 meters to 30 meters in height at the highest and lowest mountain elevations, respectively. The forest protects all four major forest types found throughout the national forest: the <a href="/wiki/Dacryodes_excelsa" title="Dacryodes excelsa">tabonuco</a> forest, the <i>palo colorado</i> forest, the <a href="/wiki/Prestoea_montana" class="mw-redirect" title="Prestoea montana">Sierra palm tree</a> forest and the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_forest" title="Dwarf forest">dwarf forest</a> (also known as the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_forest" title="Dwarf forest">elfin forest</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forest</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The wilderness is home to a number of plant and animal species found nowhere else in the world, such as the endangered <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_amazon" title="Puerto Rican amazon">Puerto Rican parrot</a> and the rare <a href="/wiki/Elfin_woods_warbler" title="Elfin woods warbler">elfin woods warbler</a>. The forest is home to 42 native and 35 migratory bird species, a number of bat species, and high numbers of amphibian and reptile species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_boa" title="Puerto Rican boa">Puerto Rican boa</a>. Some of the rare plant species found in the area include the endangered miniature orchid <i><a href="/wiki/Lepanthes_eltoroensis" title="Lepanthes eltoroensis">Lepanthes eltoroensis</a></i> and the critically endangered <i>palo de jazm\xc3\xadn</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Styrax_portoricensis" title="Styrax portoricensis">Styrax portoricensis</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The area also contains important <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological sites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">Taino</a> <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Icacos_Petroglyph_Group" title="Icacos Petroglyph Group">Icacos Petroglyph Group</a> (also known as the Rio Blanco Petroglyphs), listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> since 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Recreation">Recreation</h2><span Tradewinds Trail - the cloud forest, before <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a>. Nikon image.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Recreational opportunities in the wilderness include <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">birdwatching</a> and primitive or <a href="/wiki/Dispersed_camping" title="Dispersed camping">dispersed camping</a>. Two hiking trails cross the wilderness area: <a href="/wiki/El_Toro_(Sierra_de_Luquillo)" title="El Toro (Sierra de Luquillo)">El Toro Trail</a>, which starts from <a href="/wiki/Cubuy,_Can%C3%B3vanas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cubuy, Can\xc3\xb3vanas, Puerto Rico">Cubuy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Can%C3%B3vanas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Can\xc3\xb3vanas, Puerto Rico">Can\xc3\xb3vanas</a> and leads to the summit of <a href="/wiki/El_Toro_(Sierra_de_Luquillo)" title="El Toro (Sierra de Luquillo)">El Toro</a>, and the Sabana Trail which begins at the Sabana Recreation Area in <a href="/wiki/Naguabo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Naguabo, Puerto Rico">Naguabo</a> and loops through the dense jungle. Additionally, the Tradewinds Trail traverses El Toro Wilderness and connects these two trails with the rest of the national forest.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The entrance to the El Toro Trail is at Road 186 km 10.8 which is near an entrance to the park in Road 186. This is a steep about 3-hour hike. First you will go by next to some private property, then see the trail signs, then a steep gravel trail for 1 hr, then a very muddy trail, then a series of boulders like a staircase for 1 hr and then a flat path to the top. From El Toro peak it connects to the Tradewinds Trail which starts in El Yunque PR 191 - the main access road to El Yunque. \n</p><p>From El Toro peak it connects to the Tradewinds Trail which starts in El Yunque PR 191 - the main access road to El Yunque. Advise to return from here since the other trail is over 4 hours long and you will end way off your parked car, unless you have someone pick up up in the end gate of PR 191.\n</p><p>El Toro peak is the tallest and has a view of the east coast of Puerto Rico - the Naguabo and Humacao towns and the ocean. This peak has less rain and fog compared to El Yunque and is undeveloped. Very few do come around here unlike El Yunque trail.\n</p><p>Like most deep forest trails there is little if any cell phone reception and one must be very careful since you are very isolated. Try to come early to avoid being there at night, carry water, some food and good shoes. Avoid this forest when hurricanes are close or during storms. After major storms or hurricanes, the trails may be damaged, and the park closed.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="See_also">See also</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span
