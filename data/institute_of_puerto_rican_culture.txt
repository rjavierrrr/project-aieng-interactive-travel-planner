Puerto Rico"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p><p>The <i><b>Instituto de Cultura Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a</b></i> (English: <span lang="en">Institute of Puerto Rican Culture</span>), or <b>ICP</b> for short, is an institution of the Government of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> responsible for the establishment of the cultural policies required in order to study, preserve, promote, enrich, and diffuse the cultural values of Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-gobierno1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gobierno1-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since October 1992, its headquarters have been located at the site of the old colonial Spanish Welfare House in <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n</p><p>The ICP was created by order of Law Number 89, signed June 21, 1955, and it started operating in November of that year. Its first Executive Director was sociologist and archeology PhD <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Alegr%C3%ADa" title="Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada">Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada</a>, who felt that "There was a need to counteract decades of harmful influences, which at times were openly contradictory to our cultural values, with an effort to promote those values. There was an urgent need to struggle against a psychological conditioning which had become deeply rooted in our colonial society, and which led many Puerto Ricans to systematically diminish anything <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">autochthonous</a> or anything that seemed autochthonous, while disproportionately valuing everything that was foreign, or that seemed foreign."<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1955_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1955-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was in this social and sociological environment that the Instituto de Cultura Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a was born. The bill provoked fierce debate as, for some whose political views were in favor of the direction Puerto Rican politics had been taking in the several years prior to the debate, the bill touched on the very essence of the <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Political status of Puerto Rico">political status of Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1955_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1955-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Once the bill was signed into law, the controversy created by the new government institution did not end.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mission">Mission</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In general terms, the organizational structure of the Instituto responds to the functions assigned to it by Law.  Various programs address to the following aspects of the Puerto Rican culture: promote the arts, archeology, museums, parks, monuments, historic zones, music, theater, dance, and the Archives and the National Library of Puerto Rico. It extends its promotion of these throughout all the municipalities of Puerto Rico through local Cultural Centers. These Cultural Centers are autonomous organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-gobierno1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gobierno1-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Functions">Functions</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a arts, the popular arts and handcrafts, the theatrical arts, and the musical arts, has a branch that publishes books and periodicals, manages a number of museums and parks, and has restored many historic buildings throughout Puerto Rico as part of its historic zones and monuments program. Many of these buildings have been turned into museums.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As an example, as part of a project of restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Ponce_Historic_Zone" title="Ponce Historic Zone">Ponce Historic Zone</a>, the Instituto de Cultura Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a assembled a 13-member team to research and survey what changes would need to be implemented for the restoration as that zone.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>High-resolution images of works of art from Puerto Rico\'s museums are being digitized and made available online with the help of the Instituto, <a href="/wiki/Google_Arts_%26_Culture" title="Google Arts &amp; Culture">Google Arts &amp; Culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lin_Manuel_Miranda" class="mw-redirect" title="Lin Manuel Miranda">Lin Manuel Miranda</a> and other stakeholders. 350 such works were available online by November 7, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-Katz_2019_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz_2019-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Google_Arts_&amp;_Culture_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Google_Arts_&amp;_Culture-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-El_Nuevo_Dia_2019_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-El_Nuevo_Dia_2019-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Proposal">Proposal</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proposal"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Trias_Monge" class="mw-redirect" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Trias Monge">Jos\xc3\xa9 Trias Monge</a> the concern of <a href="/wiki/Luis_Mu%C3%B1oz_Mar%C3%ADn" title="Luis Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn">Luis Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn</a> (then president of the Senate of Puerto Rico) about the role of local culture within the territorial framework of the political status of Puerto Rico dated as far back as 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez155_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez155-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After being elected governor, the functionary discussed the issue again in 1953, trying to separate it from the political issue, and arguing about being selective about what should be adopted from American culture. In the days that followed the Spanish-American War, there had been a decline in the condition of historic buildings as well as an increase in consumerism. On November 18, 1954, Arnold Miles presented a "program" (formally named The Preservation of the Cultural Heritage of Puerto Rico) to preserve "the cultural heritage of Puerto Rico", asserting that it had "been neglected" under the American flag.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez156_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez156-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He proposed the creation of the Commonwealth Trust for Historical Preservation. Mu\xc3\xb1oz responded by congratulating Miles for the report, which was then discussed in anticipation of a legislative project.\n</p><p>In January 1955, the governor announced before the legislative assembly that he intended to create a public corporation to preserve and promote Puerto Rican heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez157_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez157-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Archeologist and anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Alegr%C3%ADa" title="Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada">Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada</a>, historian Arturo Morales Carri\xc3\xb3n, architect Bara\xc3\xb1o de la Junta Planes and others held reunions with Mu\xc3\xb1oz, who was also interested in acquiring the Junghanns Collection. A law allowing for the expropriation of private collections/historic documents for public display and/or preservation was assigned to then Secretary of Justice Tr\xc3\xadas Monge, with the intention of keeping it in Puerto Rico and prevent it from falling into the hands of foreign collectors. Alegr\xc3\xada analyzed foreign laws to this effect, including countries from throughout the hemisphere. Carri\xc3\xb3n proposed following the example of the Casa de la Cultura Ecuatorian in terms of scope, but with autonomy from the central government.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez158_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez158-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He collaborated with representative Jorge Font Salda\xc3\xb1a in the first legislative draft. The  new institution was going to be solely focused in the investigation, preservation and promotion of culture, distancing it from being a university.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez159_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez159-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Legislation">Legislation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legislation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>On May 3, 1955, Project 1381 was presented before the Puerto Rico House of Representatives by its president, Ernesto Ramos Antonini.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez159_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez159-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During the ensuing debate, there was a clash between cultural nationalism and universalism, as well as the opposition arguing that it could serve as a tool for the PPD to politicize culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Carri\xc3\xb3n, Alegr\xc3\xada (in representation of the UPR), Rafael A. Gonz\xc3\xa1lez and Luis M. Rodr\xc3\xadguez Morales (the latter two conservationists from San Juan) and others attended the public hearings.  Within the PPD, universalist Jos\xc3\xa9 Arsenio Torres opposed it calling it "of totalitarian tendencies", argued that it was only "going to support a political ideology [the Commonwealth]" and dismissed this type of culture as not "a real problem for the people".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez161_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez161-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Independentist Marcos A. Ram\xc3\xadrez Irizarry proposed an amendment, citing concern that "no official interpretation of [Puerto Rican] history or cultural values" would be "imposed" by it.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Statehooder Leopoldo Figueroa argued against "culture viewed from [the PPD\'s] perspective", while his partner Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9 argued against "static culture", before employing a universalist approach by arguing for the name to be "Puerto Rican Institute of Culture", de-emphasizing  local customs.\nTwo days later, the project was sent to the Treasury Commission for the study of its scope.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On May 17, 1955, an amended version defined its functions, how the Board of Directors would elect the executive director (who would work for $9,600) and a $35,000 budget.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez160-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The project passed with 35 votes in favor and 13 against. The Senate of Puerto Rico approved it on May 25, 1955.\nDuring the summer, Mu\xc3\xb1oz discussed his idea for "Operation Serenity", which he intended to serve as a counterpart to the industrialization process known as "Operation Bootstrap", at Harvard University.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez154_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez154-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On June 21, he signed the creation of the Institute of Puerto Rican Culture into Public Law 89.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="First_board">First board</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First board"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The ICP\'s first board included Tr\xc3\xadas Monge, Enrique Laguerre, Salvador Ti\xc3\xb3, Jos\xc3\xa9 A. at La Fortaleza.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez164_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez164-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez164_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez164-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Ti\xc3\xb3 and Carri\xc3\xb3n proposed Nilita Vient\xc3\xb3s Gast\xc3\xb3n. Alegr\xc3\xada won 5-1, and Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn approved it despite having reserves with Alegr\xc3\xada\'s nationalist political ideology, when Tom\xc3\xa1s Blanco and the governor\'s own wife, Ines (who had been a teacher for the young archeologist) supported it. On October 23, 1955, the Board of Directors requested a work plan, which was presented the following week covering the first year and a half of operations. Restoration of buildings and objects with cultural, historic and artistic value; preservation of historic and cultural heritage at large, gathering of valuable material; research; promotion and divulgation were the key aspects of the document. For this, the scope was to include anything following the European arrival during the 15th century up to modern times, any cultural manifestation including folklore was relevant.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez168_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez168-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Board of Directors held frequent reunions during this stage, often bi-weekly. In the fifth, Alegr\xc3\xada demanded a cut in his salary, but the board at large declined. Instead, he began investing it back into the ICP. Inter-agencias coordination was discussed, in particular how the Planning Board could be involved in the restoration of Old San Juan, but additional clashes and difficulties were experienced with the Treasury Department, Budget, Personnel and Supply among others.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez169_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez169-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The UPR was also defensive, believing that it was going to lose functions that had so far been controlled by it.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez165_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez165-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nAlegr\xc3\xada brought members from across the political spectrum to the ICP, but it was the contract of Roberto Beascoechea Lota and Isabel Gut\xc3\xaderrez del Arroyo the ones that caused more trouble, being nationalists while the Gag Law was in full effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez169_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez169-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Francisco Espinoza of the Department of Justice interfered, but desisted from pursuing the matter after Alegr\xc3\xada proposed that the matter was settled in a court, knowing well that Tr\xc3\xadas Monge (who had authored the Gag Law as requested, but actually opposed its implementation) was the Secretary of Justice. Marisa Rosado was later brought to work as Alegr\xc3\xada\'s secretary.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez171_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez171-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nAlegr\xc3\xada began pursuing the acquisition of the Caguana site, which he had failed to accomplish under the UPR.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez166_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez166-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Shortly after he was confirmed in October 1955, Eliseo Combas of <i>El Mundo</i> sardonically criticized his interest in Talla de Santos and other similar practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez165_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez165-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Operational_organization_and_reception">Operational organization and reception</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operational organization and reception"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>With its establishment, the ICP created a series of precedents in areas such as government-sponsored cultural institutions and building restoration that preceded the cultural efforts of the federal government during the Kennedy administration (which later led to the National Foundation of Arts and Humanities during the Johnson administration and the National Historic Preservation Act of 1966 (Robert McNutty, who advised Congress, spent time in Puerto Rico to study the restoration of San Juan and the ICP\'s protocol, crediting Alegr\xc3\xada for providing a definition of "cultural heritage".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez272_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez272-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Kennedy himself had discussed the functioning of the ICP with Alegr\xc3\xada during his visit to Puerto Rico, as part of activism held at <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a> on December 15, 1961. During the research phase that led to the law that created the National Foundation of Arts and Humanities, <a href="/wiki/Livingston_L._Biddle" class="mw-redirect" title="Livingston L. Biddle">Livingston L. Biddle</a> traveled to Puerto Rico and learned of the ICP\'s restoration efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Afterwards, Henry Allen Moe]] recruited Alegr\xc3\xada as an advisor on museums for the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Humanities" title="National Endowment for the Humanities">National Endowment for the Humanities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1959, <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Rockefeller" title="Nelson Rockefeller">Nelson Rockefeller</a> attended a reunion at La Fortaleza, and during the travel came in contact with the ICP\'s work, later loaning his collection for exhibit.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez273-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A year later, he sent a commission to study the institute\'s work in an effort that led to the creation of the New York State Arts Council. His then-assistant <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Hanks" class="mw-redirect" title="Nancy Hanks">Nancy Hanks</a> described Alegr\xc3\xada as "an international leader [...] in the field of arts" and credited him with inspiring the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Arts" title="National Endowment for the Arts">National Endowment for the Arts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez274_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez274-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both would later coincide at the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_on_the_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council on the Arts">National Council on the Arts</a>, where he exchanged notes on his work with English-language plays at the Civic Theater and noted a similarly with the Expansion Arts initiative for minorities in the states. \n</p><p>Both the pro-statehood PNP and the pro-Independence PIP internally opposed the creation of the ICP, citing through their leaders <a href="/wiki/Luis_A._Ferr%C3%A9" title="Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9">Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gilberto_Concepci%C3%B3n_de_Gracia" title="Gilberto Concepci\xc3\xb3n de Gracia">Gilberto Concepci\xc3\xb3n de Gracia</a> their concerns that the PPD would politicize/monopolize what constituted "culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After an extended debate, Marcos A. Ram\xc3\xadrez Irizarry (who had been approached by PPD Jorge Font Salda\xc3\xb1a) defeated Concepci\xc3\xb3n\'s posture among legislators, but the Executive Commission voted in support of the president and Ram\xc3\xadrez fell in line.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez206_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez206-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Ferr\xc3\xa9 continued his opposition, wanting to name it "Puerto Rican Institute of Culture", de-emphasizing the local culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez162-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Figures like Jos\xc3\xa9 Arsenio Torres also expressed skepticism about the possibility of it serving to promote nationalism.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez266_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez266-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nHistorically, the PNP opposed the ICP, which they considered a "breeding ground for radicals".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez288_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez288-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A belief that Alegr\xc3\xada countered by noting how artists tended to believe in independence even before joining and that figures like Jos\xc3\xa9 Oliver, Walter Murray Chiesa and Aurelio Ti\xc3\xb3 were statehooders. In private correspondence, Antonio Colorado argued in favor of Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada for the office of executive secretary, feeling that he didn\'t care for compliments and would perform an efficient job, besides possessing the track record.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Alegr\xc3\xada argued in favor of Tom\xc3\xa1s Blanco, but the latter declined due to previous differences with Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the process, Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn consulted figures like Arturo Morales Carri\xc3\xb3n to create a "general plan" to pursue several cultural proposals (among them, the restoration of Old San Juan).<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez205_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez205-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The ICP\'s model took inspiration from the Ecuadorian House of Culture, the Uruguayan Service of Cultural Diffusion, the American <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_International_Education" title="Institute of International Education">Institute of International Education</a> and the British Council.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez205_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez205-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nAlegr\xc3\xada created the ICP\'s spectrum emphasizing his interests as an anthropologist, requiring advisers in other topics.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez259_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez259-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He established initiatives with the intention of becoming a broader "tradition", so it would outlive his tenure.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez260_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez260-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He held an open doors policy, welcoming whoever wanted to met with him and continued working after hours at his house. In order to keep the ICP independent of politics, orders were given not to ask affiliation, donate or even cooperate with legal investigations if the motives were deemed political.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez261_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez261-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Regardless of political influence, any individual would only be contracted if there was an opening in the pre-existing structure. Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn\'s wife, In\xc3\xa9s Mendoza, had been a teacher to a young Alegr\xc3\xada and supported several of his initiatives, also allowing a relaxed access for him to La Fortaleza.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez281_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez281-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nUnder Alegr\xc3\xada, work activities would also include a cultural element, such as being held at Caguana.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez262_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez262-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nAlegr\xc3\xada would initially help employees in debt, leading to the creation of the Urayo\xc3\xa1n Credit and Savings Cooperative.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez263_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez263-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Following its establishment, the ICP faced critics from political figures such as Eliseo Combas Guerra of <i>El Mundo</i>, who either opposed promoting Puerto Rican culture as defined by "the PPD" or considered it an extension of the Commonwealth initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez267_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez267-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nOther groups, such as artists Rafael Rivera Garc\xc3\xada and Rafael Ferrer, resented the standards used to define art by the ICP for diverse reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez268_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez268-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The traditionalist preferences of both Alegr\xc3\xada and director Jos\xc3\xa9 Oliver placed the ICP at odds with abstract painters, despite the collaboration of Luis Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Cruz with the institution. However, the curator of the Engraving Department of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Metropolitan_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Metropolitan Museum">New York Metropolitan Museum</a> A. Hyatt Mayor considered that the ICP\'s work was fostering an "artistic explosion" product of a changing socioeconomic landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez269_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez269-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The ICP received control of several cultural initiatives that up to that point had been assigned to the UPR and superseded the Fine Arts Department as the main hub of development for local artists.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez207-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The further acquisition of the General Archive and its funding, which angered Ben\xc3\xadtez and his Occidentalist clique.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez208_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez208-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Arturo Morales Carri\xc3\xb3n would lead the General Archive. The ICP\'s approach also represented a contrast to the universalist approach promoted during this time at the UPR.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez265_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez265-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Figures like Thomas S. Hayes used the press to diminish the accomplishments of the ICP and label it as unoriginal in the wake of the transfer of the archives. Despite this, others like Jos\xc3\xa9 Arsenio Torres were moved to favor the ICP based on its political neutrality.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez266_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez266-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Historical,_archeology_and_art_programs"><span id="Historical.2C_archeology_and_art_programs"></span>Historical, archeology and art programs</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical, archeology and art programs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Isabel Guti\xc3\xa9rrez del Arroyo and Arturo D\xc3\xa1vila were placed in charge of matters related to local history.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez491_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez491-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The archeological investigation program received the collaboration of Irvin Rouse (Yale) and initially focused at Caguana, also studying sites at San Juan and Aguada. The conference program began in 1956 in collaboration with the UPR, incorporating both local and foreign experts, being later compiled in 1960.\nIn 1956, H\xc3\xa9ctor Campos Parsi was placed in charge of the Music Advisory Commission and the Musical Recording Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez492_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez492-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The latter gathered a variety of 19-20 century, contemporary and folkloric recordings and documents, which was used as basis for an archive. Another initiative, the Archive of the Word, gathered speeches and other such expressions.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez495_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez495-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>When the UPR invited Rufino Tamayo to inaugurate a mural named <i>Prometeo</i> in its library, he visited the ICP and Alegr\xc3\xada (with whom he was acquainted), a move that angered Ben\xc3\xadtez.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez265_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez265-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>The ICP\'s board would include figures like Salvador Ti\xc3\xb3, Enrique Laguerre and Eugenio Fern\xc3\xa1ndez M\xc3\xa9ndez.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez258_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez258-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Roberto Beascoechea aided Alegr\xc3\xada.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez261_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez261-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1950, the Center of Puerto Rican Art was created.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez216_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez216-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As the transfer of the ancient Spanish forts became impending, the legislature assigned $53,000 to the ICP for their conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez213_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez213-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1956, the cultural center program integrated pre-existing institutions, which received funds exchange for cultural work.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez493_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez493-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Established in 1957, the cultural diffusion program created a method for the cultural centers to act autonomously in regards to initiatives that originated in their vicinity rather than depend on directions from San Juan.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez494_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez494-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other programs, like the Ballets of San Juan, were also sponsored.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez494_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez494-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>The 1955 budget was $35,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez263_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez263-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During the early years, the budget was limited to the point that Alegr\xc3\xada would occasionally work without being paid and had to repurpose the kitchen as his office and request funds for air conditioning.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez209_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez209-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During his stay, he didn\'t ask for a raise and declined offers. Likewise, he used the same office for advisers Tom\xc3\xa1s Blanco and Lidio Cruz Monclova, both of whom lashed against each other upon learning of this.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez258_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez258-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Artists also clashed among themselves.A grants program was established for investigation and studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez501_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez501-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Interaction_with_other_agencies">Interaction with other agencies</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instituto_de_Cultura_Puertorrique%C3%B1a&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Interaction with other agencies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>From the beginning, the ICP and Fomento were immersed in a competition, further fueled by diverging ideas about culture among their leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez277_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez277-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Alegr\xc3\xada himself was noted for opposing the universalist concept of culture, for which he clashed with figures like Teodoro Moscoso and Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9, due to his belief that anything good enough to gather universal appraisal was born from within a national context. From within Fomento, the former held a belief that there was no such thing as national culture and as such introduced Festival Casals as an event were artist from abroad converged at San Juan for days and left, contrasting Alegr\xc3\xada\'s methodological approach.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez278_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez278-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The latter would once claim that Fomento viewed the ICP as "trying to cling to roots that were related to the black man, to the Indian, with creole things. Which to some was mediocre, third rate." The rivalry between organization became so evident that C\xc3\xa9sar Andreu Iglesias wrote a satirical "what-if" piece postulating what would have if they changed seats.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez280_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez280-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ICP mingled within the fringes of Fomento\'s jurisdiction, fostering and promoting typical restaurants and other establishments as part of Alegr\xc3\xada\'s vision of a "Puerto Rican-flavored tourism".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez294_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez294-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span A "rolling museum" also served to carry exhibitions elsewhere.\n</p><p>On October 7, 1957, the board declined a proposal that Alegr\xc3\xada presented with the support of Ti\xc3\xb3 to turn the Hermanos Behn house at Condado into a hotel as part of an initiative that would continue for years to "Puerto Ricanize [the local] tourism", then in hands of several foreign interests.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez313_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez313-63"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>63<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1958, when Fomento requested the Arsenal la Puntilla building to establish a museum, Alegr\xc3\xada received a copy of the request and objected, due to it infringing the limits of cultural affairs assigned to the ICP.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez279_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez279-64"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> That same year, restoration efforts at the Caparra settlement began. As part of his interest in Puerto Rican folklore, Alegr\xc3\xada published <i>Los renegados; Cuentos Folkl\xc3\xb3ricos Puertorrique\xc3\xb1os</i> and <i>The Three Wishes: A Collection of Puerto Rican Folktales</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez296_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez296-65"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>65<span for the initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez214_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez214-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span to the ICP, where it would house the Conservatorio de M\xc3\xbasica and Casals Festival.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez214_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez214-74"><span Ferr\xc3\xa9">Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9</a> won the elections, there were calls from within that party to have Alegr\xc3\xada removed from the ICP due to ideological differences and a campaign against him was launched by Pedro V\xc3\xa1zquez in <i>El D\xc3\xada</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez289_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez289-75"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>75<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He did not attend when the new governor was sworn in and on January 13, 1969, presented his resignation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez290_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez290-83"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>83<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the board refused to accept it and figures like Nilita Vient\xc3\xb3s, Noel Col\xc3\xb3n Mart\xc3\xadnez, Gilberto Concepci\xc3\xb3n and In\xc3\xa9s Mendoza rallied behind him, along a number of mayors and cultural centers. Within the statehood movement, Genar\xc3\xadn Cauti\xc3\xb1o, Aurelio Ti\xc3\xb3 and Carlos A. Pesquera Reyes supported him. Carlos E. Chard\xc3\xb3n wrote to Jos\xc3\xa9 M. L\xc3\xa1zaro considered its work "extraordinary" and within its intended purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez506_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez506-108"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>108<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Writer Vicente G\xc3\xa9igel Polanco considered that the promotion of cultural fields was "of the upmost quality", also noting Alegr\xc3\xada as "hardworking". Painter Rafael Tufi\xc3\xb1o considered that the ICP\'s programs were "well intended" and that its artistic grants had been "well oriented". Architect Jos\xc3\xa9 Firpi said that its restoration of Old San Juan had been "an important contribution that worked as guide for the cultural life of [the Puerto Rican] people" and credited it for rescuing "long forgotten values". Writer Emilio S. Belaval assessed that the ICP "met [the standards] of the humanistic program of institutions of its [kind]", crediting Alegr\xc3\xada with creating a balance that was "noble" and did not go as far as "deify culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez507_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez507-109"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>109<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Composer Amaury Veray discussed the extent of the cultural centers in promoting music, something that he considered had "transcendental importance in the preservation of the Puerto Rican root[s]." Poet Manuel Joglar Cacho credited Alegr\xc3\xada\'s work for the success of the Congresos de Poes\xc3\xada, calling the ICP itself a "very necessary institution".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez508_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez508-110"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>110<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Writer Mar\xc3\xada Teresa Bab\xc3\xadn States that the ICP was involved in a "crusade in pro of Puerto Rican nationality [in the process] securing the future of Puerto Rico within the hispanic orbit of America" and urged the readers to be proud of the Board of Directors. Painter Rafael R\xc3\xados Rey noted "appreciated the [artistic creation] task" of the ICP. Composer H\xc3\xa9ctor Campos Parsi called it a reflection "of the inherent greatness of [the Puerto Rican people]" and gauged its work as "prodigious".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez509_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez509-111"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>111<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Poet Francisco Matos Paoli argued that the "Puerto Rican homeland was in debt" with the ICP and Alegr\xc3\xada, closing by saying that "any grateful man should clap". \n</p><p>Wayne Grover of the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a> said that "the Government and people of Puerto Rico have reasons to be profoundly proud of what [had] been accomplished in such a short time". Spanish linguist Tom\xc3\xa1s Navarro Tom\xc3\xa1s called its first years "astounding", calling it an "example" for other places.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez510_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez510-112"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>112<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Writer Monelisa P\xc3\xa9rez Marchand called the ICP the "unwavering watchtower of [Puerto Rican] cultural heritage", which she claimed was achieved without "false patriotism or outrageous chauvinism". Historian Luis M. D\xc3\xadaz responsibilities it for "liberating [the public] from its indifference for all things cultural". Playwright Gerard P. Mar\xc3\xadn noted that he was initially skeptical of the ICP, but that what had taken place -despite being insufficient for a full assessment- had been positive.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez511_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez511-113"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>113<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Painter Miguel Pou considered that it had filled a void in the education of cultural values. Writer Ren\xc3\xa9 Marqu\xc3\xa9s discussed the difficulties of engaging in "direct and uninterrupted cultural activity" within the government agency with limited budget and personnel, arguing that it was worthy of praise and also assessing that its work was "valuable, of permanent character". Linguist Rub\xc3\xa9n del Rosario called the work of the ICP unprecedented in Puerto Rico and evaluated that it had so far avoided politicization or chauvinism, something that he considered worthy of felicitations.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez512_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez512-114"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>114<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Cuban writer Eugenio Florit commented on the ICP\'s literature, calling it "praise worthy" and stated that work of this kind would "honor any country". Writer Gilberto Concepci\xc3\xb3n de Gracia discussed its "extraordinary work" and the "competence" of Alegr\xc3\xada as its director. Architect Santiago Iglesias Jr. assessed that the ICP had coordinated the fine arts  and that it "deserve[d] the recognition of all Puerto Rico". Poet Diana Ram\xc3\xadrez de Arellano called the promotion of Puerto Rican culture an "unreplacable patriotic  work".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez513_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez513-115"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>115<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\nHistorian Adolfo de Hostos credited it with being effective in the diffusion of local history through a number of venues, including the arts and the restoration of Old San Juan. Writer Gustavo Agra\xc3\xadt credited Alegr\xc3\xada with being able to quell the concerns that emerged when the ICP was created by the government. Architect Carlos R. Sanz called the restoration "gigantic and highly valuable".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez514_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez514-116"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>116<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Writer Emilio J. Pasarell called it a "beginning worthy of praise and promising of a complete task". Writer Juan Mart\xc3\xadnez Cap\xc3\xb3 considered that the ICP\'s publishing division was undertaking a work that was overdue in republishing prominent authors. Writer Ces\xc3\xa1reo Rosa Nieves emphasized the "deep island roots" of the "nativist efforts" of the ICP, calleing it award-worthy.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez515_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez515-117"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>117<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Wroter Figueroa Barrios considered that the institution was carrying out "services that any government owes to its people". Poet Carmelina Vizcarrondo expressed he faith that the ICP would continue its work within the arts and culture. Spanish linguist Samuel Gili Gaya said that usually cultural institutions took time to produce results, but that somehow the ICP had "surprises with a brilliant harvest" within five years, a sign of the maturity of its collaborators. Writer Luis Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Aquino noted that all fields of arts had "been favored" as part of a "wonderful cultural work".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez516_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez516-118"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>118<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1973, several figures gauged the ICP\'s work as it neared two decades of existence. Spanish musician Pablo Casals called what had been achieved "notable". Historian Arturo Morales Carri\xc3\xb3n noted that he felt "profound spiritual satisfaction" at what had been achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez517_119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez517-119"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>119<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Architect MARIO Buschiazzo noted that the ICP had fulfilled initiatives that had been unsuccessfully proposed to the Junta de Planificaci\xc3\xb3n concerning the restoration of Old San Juan. Politician Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9 congratulated Alegr\xc3\xada for a "magnificent work". Actress Mona Marti emphasized the theater festival as an example of how that field had been advanced by the institution. Writer Jos\xc3\xa9 Agust\xc3\xadn Balseiro noted the struggles that took place in order to restore Old San Juan and praises the work done with popular art.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez518_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez518-120"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>120<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Argentinian professor Aurelio Tanodi said that he was "impressed" with the work done at Old San Juan, which he called a rare sight in the Americas. Poet Juan Antonio Corretjer argued that Alegr\xc3\xada\'s formation was responsible for the work accomplished so far, all the while criticizing the management of the historic buildings prior to the creation of the ICP and expressing concern that as long as sovereignty rested in foreign hands it could be undone.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez519_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez519-121"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>121<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The curator of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> Hyatt Mayor called it "a radiant center of culture on the Island", one "of all arts, whose light will radiate all around", further stating that he "admire[d Alegr\xc3\xada\'s] intelligence". Writer Tom\xc3\xa1s Blanco argued that it deserved "the congratulations and cooperation of any good Puerto Rican". Restoration expert Charles E. Peterson said that he "believed [the restoration of Old San Juan] an outstanding success" product of a "comprehensive and well-thought-out program".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez520_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez520-122"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>122<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Catholic cardinal Luis Aponte Mart\xc3\xadnez provided "a testimony of admiration and gratitude" to the ICP and Alegr\xc3\xada for the work done on historic religious sites at San Juan and San Germ\xc3\xa1n. In\xc3\xa9s Mendoza, former teacher of Alegr\xc3\xada and wife to governor Mu\xc3\xb1oz Mar\xc3\xadn, claimed that the ICP had salvaged the national and cultural heritage of Puerto Rico and brought forth an artistic and cultural renaissance, crediting the director for this success.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez521_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez521-123"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>123<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Archeologist Irving Rouse wrote that the ICP had "achieved more than any other country that [he had] visited and that its work is of the highest quality". Edward P. Alexander of the Colonial Williamsburg site noted that while the work was based on "its authenticity based on historical and archeological research", it also kept San Juan a living city with great cultural relevance", also crediting the "broad programs of your Institute of Puerto Rican Culture [and this] kind of cultural entrepreneurship" with making "Puerto Rico a delightful place in which to live of visit".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez522_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez522-124"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>124<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Henry Allen Moe of the New York State Historical Association credited Alegr\xc3\xada with "pre-eminence in the field of cultural activities, including museums".\n</p><p>In 1965, Ursula von Eckardt of the UPR and universalit wrote criticizing the ICP, claiming that it was "fostering a museum-like institution which preserves monuments of \'Puerto Rican\' native culture under glass for the self-glorification of a small establishment of "intellectuals" who are worried about their own "identity".<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez267_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez267-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In his own words, <a href="/wiki/Mario_Vargas_Llosa" title="Mario Vargas Llosa">Mario Vargas Llosa</a> noted that he was impressed by the ICP\'s restoration of San Juan, hosting Alegr\xc3\xada in his <i>La Torre de Babel</i> show in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez275_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hern\xc3\xa1ndez275-81"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>81<span
