lang="es">Isla de Mona</i>) is the third-largest island of the <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Geography of Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican archipelago</a>, after the main island of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vieques" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieques">Vieques</a>. It is the largest of three islands in the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Passage" title="Mona Passage">Mona Passage</a>, the strait between <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> and Puerto Rico, with the others being <a href="/wiki/Monito_Island" title="Monito Island">Monito Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desecheo_Island" title="Desecheo Island">Desecheo Island</a>. It measures about 7 miles by 4 miles (11&#160;km by 7&#160;km), and lies 41&#160;mi (66&#160;km) west of Puerto Rico, of which it is administratively a part. It is one of two islands that make up the <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_Mona_e_Islote_Monito" class="mw-redirect" title="Isla de Mona e Islote Monito">Isla de Mona e Islote Monito</a> barrio in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Mayag\xc3\xbcez, Puerto Rico">Mayag\xc3\xbcez</a>.\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-STS007-4-137_-_View_of_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4393" data-file-height="4600" /></a><figcaption>Satellite image of Mona Island between the Dominican Republic (left) and the Puerto Rico mainland (right)</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The island is managed under the <a href="/wiki/Mona_and_Monito_Islands_Nature_Reserve" title="Mona and Monito Islands Nature Reserve">Mona and Monito Islands Nature Reserve</a> (<i>Reserva Natural Islas de Mona y Monito</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-drna_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drna-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There are no permanent inhabitants; only rangers and biologists from the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Natural_and_Environmental_Resources" title="Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources">Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources</a> reside on the island, to manage visitors and take part in research projects. The island, along with Monito, form part of the <b>Mona and Monito Islands</b> <a href="/wiki/National_Natural_Landmark" title="National Natural Landmark">National Natural Landmark</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which recognizes and preserves the islands\' ecosystem as an outstanding example of diversity and natural history.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The island is home to a number of endemic species such as the <a href="/wiki/Mona_ground_iguana" title="Mona ground iguana">Mona ground iguana</a> and it is an important seabird area.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg/220px-Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg/330px-Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg/440px-Mona_Ta%C3%ADno_cave_art_no.2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">Ta\xc3\xadno</a> cave art in caves of Mona island</figcaption></figure>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Pre-Columbian_history">Pre-Columbian history</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-Columbian history"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Mona Island is believed to have been originally settled by the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">Ta\xc3\xadno</a> since the 12th century or sooner.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_1941_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_1941-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An archeological excavation during the 1980s discovered many <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">Pre-Columbian</a> objects on the island that helped support historians\' theories of the island\'s first inhabitants. Stone tools found in a rock shelter have been dated to around <a href="/wiki/3000_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="3000 BC">3000 BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Much later the island was settled by the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADnos" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta\xc3\xadnos">Ta\xc3\xadnos</a> and remained so until the arrival of the Spanish in the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_Dating_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian_Dating-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Colonial_period">Colonial period</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colonial period"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>On November 19, 1493, during his second voyage to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> encountered the island now known as Puerto Rico, which the natives called <i>Borinquen</i> (or <i>Borik\xc3\xa9n</i>), and which Columbus named <i>San Juan Bautista</i> (Saint John the Baptist). Within hours of setting foot in Puerto Rico, Columbus and his ships headed west to <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, where he expected to meet several crewmembers who had remained behind from his first voyage. As he left Puerto Rico, he reputedly became the first European to sight the island on September 24, 1494, which was claimed for Spain. The name <i>Mona</i> derives from the Ta\xc3\xadno name <i>\xc3\x81mona</i>, bestowed by the natives in honor of the ruling <i><a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">Cacique</a></i> or chief of the island.\n</p><p>In 1502, Fray <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando">Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando</a> was sent to Isla de la Mona to keep an eye, from a safe distance, on the native revolts occurring in Hispaniola. With a group of 2,000 Spanish settlers, Ovando was left in charge of creating a permanent settlement on the island. Due to its small size and location, the island proved inadequate to accommodate such a large settlement, and food became scarce as shipments from Hispaniola and Puerto Rico were received infrequently.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a>, who accompanied Columbus on his first two voyages, became the first ruling <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">governor of Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1515, after some wrangling, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II</a> was able to reclaim the island from <a href="/wiki/Diego_Col%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Diego Col\xc3\xb3n">Diego Col\xc3\xb3n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_the_Indies" class="mw-redirect" title="Viceroy of the Indies">Viceroy of the Indies</a>. By then, Isla de la Mona was an important point of trade between Spain and the rest of Latin America, as well as a rest stop for the crews of boats carrying slaves. With his possession of the island, King Ferdinand II gave the resident Ta\xc3\xadnos two options if they wished to continue living on the island: they could work by fishing, making <a href="/wiki/Hammock" title="Hammock">hammocks</a> and cultivating plants, or they could become miners and help in the mining of <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> and other minerals. Realizing that mining would require intense labor, the majority of inhabitants chose to work as fishermen and farmers. By accepting this option, they also were exempted from paying imposed taxes, and were able to avoid the hard labor many other natives endured in mines. In time, natives from other neighboring islands were brought to Mona Island to assist with labor.\n</p><p>After the death of Ferdinand II in 1516, ownership of the island was transferred to Cardenal Cisneros. The island changed ownership again in 1520, when Francisco de Barrionuevo became the island\'s new landlord. By 1524, <a href="/wiki/Alonso_Manso" title="Alonso Manso">Alonso Manso</a>, bishop of Puerto Rico, had become interested in gaining personal wealth, and he accused Barrionuevo, among others, of various crimes under the Spanish justice system of the time. Because of this situation, Barrionuevo exiled himself to one of Spain\'s colonies in South America, taking many Ta\xc3\xadnos along with him, and leaving the island practically deserted.\n</p><p>By 1522, ships from other major sea powers such as <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="French colonization of the Americas">France</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Dutch colonization of the Americas">Netherlands</a> began to arrive at Isla de la Mona to replenish supplies for their transatlantic voyages. The island also provided a refuge from which they, as well as pirates, could attack and plunder <a href="/wiki/Galleon" title="Galleon">Spanish galleons</a>.\n</p><p>In 1561, during an audience held in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>, it was recommended that Isla de la Mona should become a part of that colony (which at the time occupied the entirety of Hispaniola). The reasons offered were simply that the island was closer to Santo Domingo (presently the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>) than to Puerto Rico, and that it had a small population which could help the colony\'s economy in overall agricultural production. However, the petition was turned down and the island continued to remain politically part of Puerto Rico.\n</p><p>In 1583, the Spanish archbishop of Puerto Rico received royal permission to bring Christianity to Mona Island. However, by this time most Ta\xc3\xadnos remaining on the island had either died or fled to mainland Puerto Rico due to repeated raiding by European (especially French) ships. From the end of the 16th century up until the mid-19th century the island was largely abandoned by the colonial authorities. It seems to have been sporadically inhabited, although records from this period are somewhat sketchy. It continued to be used as a refuge by pirates and privateers, including the notorious <a href="/wiki/Captain_Kidd" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain Kidd">Captain Kidd</a> who hid out there in 1699.<sup id="cite_ref-herald_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The island\'s circumstances changed in the mid-19th century when it became the site of commercial guano mining operations. Various companies were granted licenses to extract the bat and seagull guano (a valuable fertilizer and key strategic commodity for the production of gunpowder) from the island\'s caves. Mining continued until 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="20th_century">20th century</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Monaisland.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Monaisland.JPG/220px-Monaisland.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="325" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Monaisland.JPG/330px-Monaisland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Monaisland.JPG 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="591" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Mona_Island_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Mona Island Lighthouse">Mona Island Lighthouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island_Tramway" title="Mona Island Tramway">Mona Island Tramway</a>, 1913</figcaption></figure>\n<p>With the 1898 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1898)" title="Treaty of Paris (1898)">Treaty of Paris</a>, Isla de la Mona, along with the rest of Puerto Rico, was handed to the United States by Spain. The population of Mona Isla was six at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-OfficeSanger1900_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OfficeSanger1900-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Within two years of occupation, the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island_Light" title="Mona Island Light">Mona Island Light</a>, left in an unfinished state since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a>, was completed and began operation. The lighthouse was not, as commonly believed, designed by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Eiffel" title="Gustave Eiffel">Gustave Eiffel</a>, but by the Spanish engineer Rafael Ravena in 1886. It was accessible by the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island_Tramway" title="Mona Island Tramway">Mona Island Tramway</a> from the beach and remained in continuous operation until 1976 when it was replaced by a newer automated light near the center of the island.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>On December 22, 1919, the island was declared an <i>Insular Forest of Puerto Rico</i>, under the auspices of the U.S. Forest Law No. 22.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> the island had a history of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_smuggling" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcohol smuggling">smuggling</a>, with its geographic location making it a prime location for <a href="/wiki/Rum_runners" class="mw-redirect" title="Rum runners">rum runners</a> to smuggle rum, <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_whiskey" title="Bourbon whiskey">bourbon</a>, and other liquor. In 1923, a stash of liquor, drugs, and perfumes, reportedly from the French islands of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collectivity_of_Saint_Martin" title="Collectivity of Saint Martin">Saint Martin</a> and worth US$75,000, was found in a cave by customs officials.\n</p><p>In 1942, at the height of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-156_(1941)" title="German submarine U-156 (1941)">German U-156 submarine</a> fired 30 shells from its deck gun at the southern coast of the island. The crew of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> vessel suspected that Camp Cofres\xc3\xad, a camp that the <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">Civilian Conservation Corps</a> were using as the base of a reforestation effort, could be an unmarked military facility. There were no significant losses, but the incident was one of the few military strikes of the war in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, which was also the focus of a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Caribbean" title="Battle of the Caribbean">German/Italian campaign</a> against <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> resource shipping. Prior to this, the Nazi <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> had sunk nineteen ships in the region including one named <i>Del Plata</i> in Puerto Rican waters. On June 4, 1942, the <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tanker</a> <a href="/wiki/MV_C.O._Stillman" class="mw-redirect" title="MV C.O. Stillman">MV <i>C.O. Stillman</i></a> was sunk by the German submarine <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-68_(1940)" title="German submarine U-68 (1940)"><i>U-68</i></a> 41 nautical miles (76&#160;km) southwest of Isla de Mona.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> From 1945 to 1955 Mona Island was leased to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> as a military exercise area.\n</p><p>Since 1941 the island has also been used for camping and hunting goats and wild boar. In 1960 a small ranger post was established to monitor the island, operated by the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Natural_and_Environmental_Resources" title="Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources">Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources</a>.\n</p><p>In July 1972 the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Environmental_Quality_Board" title="Puerto Rico Environmental Quality Board">Puerto Rico Environmental Quality Board</a>, because of growing interest in the development of the islands, made a full scientific assessment of Mona and Monito using a local team of volunteer scientists. A two-volume report with maps of natural and historic features was produced.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It evaluated the climate, geology and mineral resources, soils, water resources, archaeology, vegetation, animals and insects, and <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic</a> life around the island. Shortly thereafter geotechnical and <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">bathymetric</a> studies were conducted by engineering firms to determine the feasibility of using Mona as a deep-water terminal for transferring oil from <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">supertankers</a> to smaller tankers which would continue to the mainland US; this plan was never implemented.\n</p><p>In 1981, the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island_Light" title="Mona Island Light">Mona Island Light</a> was listed on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> as <i>Faro de la Isla de la Mona</i>.\n</p><p>On February 15, 1985, the passenger-cargo ferry <a href="/wiki/MV_A_Regina" title="MV A Regina">MV&#160;<i>A Regina</i></a> ran aground on a reef on the southeastern side of Mona Island. While there were no deaths nor serious injuries, 143 passengers and 72 crew members landed on Mona Island to await rescue. The wreck of the ship was removed from the reef in 1990.\n</p><p>In 1993, the island, inscribed as <i>Isla de la Mona</i>, was listed as a historic district on the National Register of Historic Places.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The historic district consists of eight contributing buildings (including the lighthouse), nine prehistoric and historic archaeological sites (including several <a href="/wiki/Ball_court/plaza_sites_of_Puerto_Rico_and_the_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Ball court/plaza sites of Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands">ceremonial ball courts</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Batey_(game)" title="Batey (game)">bateyes</a></i>), two guano industrial ruins, a roadway and a cistern.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Geography">Geography</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Isla_de_Mona,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-Isla_de_Mona%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a><figcaption>Mona is a mainly flat plateau surrounded by sea cliffs.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Mona has an area of about 22&#160;sq&#160;mi (57&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) and lies 41&#160;mi (66&#160;km) west of the main island of Puerto Rico, 38&#160;mi (61&#160;km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and 30&#160;mi (48&#160;km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Desecheo_Island" title="Desecheo Island">Desecheo Island</a>, another island in the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Passage" title="Mona Passage">Mona Passage</a>.\n</p><p>Mona has been designated an ecological reserve by the Puerto Rican government and is not permanently inhabited. The US census of 2000 reported six housing units, but a population of zero.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The island is a <a href="/wiki/Barrios_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Barrios of Puerto Rico">barrio</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Municipalities of Puerto Rico">municipality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Mayag\xc3\xbcez, Puerto Rico">Mayag\xc3\xbcez</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Monito_Island" title="Monito Island">Monito Island</a> 3.1&#160;mi (5&#160;km) northwest (<i><a href="/wiki/Isla_de_Mona_e_Islote_Monito,_Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isla de Mona e Islote Monito, Mayag\xc3\xbcez, Puerto Rico">Isla de Mona e Islote Monito</a> barrio</i>). This is the largest ward of <a href="/wiki/Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Mayag\xc3\xbcez, Puerto Rico">Mayag\xc3\xbcez</a> by area, and the only one without permanent population. The total land area of both islands in the barrio is about 21.98&#160;square miles (56.93&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) (Mona Island 21.924&#160;square miles [56.783&#160;km<sup>2</sup>] and nearby Monito Island 0.057&#160;square miles [0.147&#160;km<sup>2</sup>]), and it comprises 28.3 percent of the total land area of the municipality of Mayag\xc3\xbcez. <a href="/wiki/Desecheo_Island" title="Desecheo Island">Desecheo Island</a>, 30&#160;mi (49&#160;km) to the northeast, is part of Sabanetas barrio.\n</p><p>Mona is a mainly flat plateau surrounded by sea cliffs. It is composed of <a href="/wiki/Dolomite_(rock)" title="Dolomite (rock)">dolomite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> with many <a href="/wiki/Caves" class="mw-redirect" title="Caves">caves</a> found throughout.  With an arid <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and untouched by human development, many <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> species inhabit the island, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mona_ground_iguana" title="Mona ground iguana">Mona ground iguana</a> (<i>Cyclura cornuta stejnegeri</i>). Its topography, ecology, and modern history are similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Navassa_Island" title="Navassa Island">Navassa Island</a>, a small limestone island located in the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Channel" title="Jamaica Channel">Jamaica Channel</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Climate">Climate</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isla_de_Mona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The Isla de Mona has a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna_climate" title="Tropical savanna climate">tropical savanna climate</a> (<i>Aw</i> according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification</a>), with a dry season and wet season and the temperature being warm year-round. The average annual mean is 26.6&#160;\xc2\xb0C (79.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F), an average annual high is 31.7&#160;\xc2\xb0C (89.1&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and an average annual low is 21.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (70.7&#160;\xc2\xb0F). August, the warmest month, has a mean of 28.3&#160;\xc2\xb0C (82.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and an average high of 33.8&#160;\xc2\xb0C (92.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F), while the coolest month, January, has a mean of 24.8&#160;\xc2\xb0C (76.6&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and an average low of 20.3&#160;\xc2\xb0C (68.5&#160;\xc2\xb0F), although February has a lower average low at 20.0&#160;\xc2\xb0C (68.0&#160;\xc2\xb0F).\n</p><p>On July 2, 1996, the Isla de Mona recorded a temperature of 40.0&#160;\xc2\xb0C (104.0&#160;\xc2\xb0F), which is the highest temperature to have ever been recorded in Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-NWSpuertorico_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NWSpuertorico-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Isla de Mona receives 986 millimetres (38.8&#160;in) of precipitation annually over 126.5 precipitation days. Due to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, autumn is the wettest season, while winter is the driest. The wettest month is May, which receives 125 millimetres (4.9&#160;in) of precipitation on average. October has 12.7 precipitation days on average, which is the highest of any month. January is the driest month, receiving 41 millimetres (1.6&#160;in), while April has the least precipitation days (8.3).\n</p>\n<div>\n<table class="wikitable mw-collapsible" style="width:auto; /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Mona_ground_iguana" title="Mona ground iguana">Mona ground iguana</a>, endemic to the island</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The island presently serves as a retreat for Puerto Ricans and nature enthusiasts from all around the world, and has also become a popular destination for Puerto Rican <a href="/wiki/Scouting_in_Puerto_Rico" title="Scouting in Puerto Rico">Boy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Girl_Scouts_of_the_USA" title="Girl Scouts of the USA">Girl Scouts</a>. Due to the islands\' unique topography, ecology and location, <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_Mona_e_Islote_Monito,_Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isla de Mona e Islote Monito, Mayag\xc3\xbcez, Puerto Rico">Mona, Desecheo and Monito</a> have been nicknamed "The <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Gal\xc3\xa1pagos Islands">Gal\xc3\xa1pagos Islands</a> of the Caribbean". Scientists, ecologists, and students have visited Mona Island to explore its distinct ecosystem, which includes the endemic <a href="/wiki/Mona_Ground_Iguana" class="mw-redirect" title="Mona Ground Iguana">Mona Ground Iguana</a>. The island is also home to many <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave drawings</a> that were left behind by the island\'s original inhabitants. Remains of the <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> mining industry can also be seen.\n</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a>-certified <a href="/wiki/Mona_Airport" title="Mona Airport">airport</a> that can handle small aircraft was built by the Puerto Rican government. This airport has no <a href="/wiki/ICAO" class="mw-redirect" title="ICAO">ICAO</a> or <a href="/wiki/IATA" class="mw-redirect" title="IATA">IATA</a> code. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> is able to provide transportation with helicopter flights from <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Airport" title="Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport">Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguadilla, Puerto Rico">Aguadilla</a>, to help with medicines and first aid equipment; they also fly whenever an emergency requiring hospitalization occurs. Private and commercial planes require a special permit issued by the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Natural_and_Environmental_Resources" title="Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources">Puerto Rico Department of Natural Resources</a> to use the airport\'s facilities.\n</p><p>The most common form of transportation is by private yacht, though commercial excursions are available from <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Rojo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cabo Rojo, Puerto Rico">Cabo Rojo</a> for small groups of up to twelve people traveling together.\n</p><p>Hunting is permitted in season to control the population growth of non-indigenous species (goats and <a href="/wiki/Wild_boar" title="Wild boar">wild boars</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> because they can represent a threat to various endangered species. The hunting season usually commences in December and ends in April. Camping is allowed from May through November.\n</p><p>In recent years, the island has become a major drop-off point especially for <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominican_people_(Dominican_Republic)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dominican people (Dominican Republic) (page does not exist)">Dominicans</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Haitian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Haitian people">Haitians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuban_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuban people">Cubans</a> trying to reach Puerto Rico illegally. As a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._insular_area)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. insular area)">U.S. Commonwealth</a>, Puerto Rico is seen by many undocumented migrants as a stepping stone to the United States, but they are usually deported immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the island\'s natural and cultural importance there has been interest in seeking world heritage status for Mona as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by the Puerto Rican government.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a
