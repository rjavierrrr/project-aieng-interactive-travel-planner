It was deemed notable as "one of Puerto Rico\'s finest examples of Spanish Revival apartment houses from the early 20th century."<sup id="cite_ref-nrhpdoc_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpdoc-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The building was designed by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Adolfo_de_Castro_y_Besosa" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedro Adolfo de Castro y Besosa">Pedro Adolfo de Castro y Besosa</a> (January 5, 1895-October, 1936), the first United States university-trained Puerto Rican architect.<sup id="cite_ref-nrhpdoc_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpdoc-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 16">&#58;&#8202;16&#8202;</span></sup>  He designed 33 or more apartment houses during 1929\xe2\x80\x931936.  <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Carmoega" title="Rafael Carmoega">Rafael Carmoega</a> emerged to higher prominence only after de Castro\'s early death, at age 41.<sup id="cite_ref-nrhpdoc_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpdoc-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 17">&#58;&#8202;17&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p><p>Although it appears massive, the building\'s footprint only uses up 30 percent of the lot\'s area.  It is U-shaped with a courtyard not visible from the corner of the streets.<sup id="cite_ref-nrhpdoc_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpdoc-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span
