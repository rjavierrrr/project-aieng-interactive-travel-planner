<b>Gu\xc3\xa1nica State Forest</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Bosque Estatal de Gu\xc3\xa1nica</i>), popularly known as the <b>Gu\xc3\xa1nica Dry Forest</b> (Spanish: <i>Bosque seco de Gu\xc3\xa1nica</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_dry_forests" title="Puerto Rican dry forests">subtropical dry forest</a> located in southwest <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. The area was designated as a forest reserve in 1919 and a United Nations <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Biosphere Reserve">Biosphere Reserve</a> in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is considered the best preserved subtropical <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_dry_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical dry broadleaf forests">dry forest</a> and the best example of dry forest in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>With approximately 10,000 acres, it is the largest of the 20 <a href="/wiki/List_of_state_forests_in_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="List of state forests in Puerto Rico">state forests of Puerto Rico</a>, and it extends over 5 municipalities: <a href="/wiki/Gu%C3%A1nica,_Puerto_Rico" title="Gu\xc3\xa1nica, Puerto Rico">Gu\xc3\xa1nica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guayanilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayanilla, Puerto Rico">Guayanilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1uelas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Pe\xc3\xb1uelas, Puerto Rico">Pe\xc3\xb1uelas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yauco,_Puerto_Rico" title="Yauco, Puerto Rico">Yauco</a> (although the forest units located in the municipalities of Pe\xc3\xb1uelas and Ponce only consist of keys and small islands).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The dry forest area of southwestern Puerto Rico protected under the jurisdiction of the <b>Gu\xc3\xa1nica Dry Forest</b> was first established in 1919 as a forest reserve. The United Nations recognized the ecological value of the forest in 1981 when it was designated a <a href="/wiki/Man_and_the_Biosphere_Programme" title="Man and the Biosphere Programme">Biosphere Reserve</a>, the second in Puerto Rico after <a href="/wiki/El_Yunque_National_Forest" title="El Yunque National Forest">El Yunque National Forest</a> (then called the Caribbean National Forest). The forest was given further protections as a nature reserve on October 16, 1985, by the Puerto Rico Planning Board (Spanish: <i>Junta De Planificaci\xc3\xb3n</i>), and all provisions from Law No. 133 from 1975 were subsequently applied to the nature reserve.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Geography">Geography</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Located in the dry <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">orographic rain shadow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Cordillera Central, Puerto Rico">Cordillera Central</a>, Puerto Rico\'s driest area, temperatures in the forest are, on average, around 80&#160;\xc2\xb0F (27&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in shaded areas and 100&#160;\xc2\xb0F (38&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in exposed areas.<sup id="cite_ref-DRNA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DRNA-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The average temperature is 25.3&#160;\xc2\xb0C (77.5&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and the average annual rainfall is 791 millimetres (31.1&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Geology">Geology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The dry forest is located on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Karst_(Puerto_Rico)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Southern Karst (Puerto Rico) (page does not exist)">Southern karst</a> region of Puerto Rico. The soils in the state forest include limestone, fine and soft lime soil which include areas of sand and clay where deciduous plants grow, and sand and clay soils where evergreen plants thrive.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Ecology">Ecology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario,_Gu%C3%A1nica,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-Guayac%C3%A1n_Centenario%2C_Gu%C3%A1nica%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3456" /></a><figcaption>The famous <i>Guayac\xc3\xa1n Centenario</i>.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The Gu\xc3\xa1nica State Forest is of great natural complexity and scientific importance as it serves as a critical habitat to numerous species of animals and plants that are unique to the region. Its ecosystems include sandy beaches, rocky shores, mangrove forests, seagrass prairies, coral reefs, limestone caverns, saltwater lagoons, salt flats and different types of dry forests (both deciduous and evergreen).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n</p><p>The last remaining natural population of Puerto Rican crested toads (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_crested_toad" title="Puerto Rican crested toad">Peltophryne lemur</a></i>) can be found in the intermittent freshwater pools which are created by seasonal rains. This species proliferates in the natural conditions created by the karstic geology of the forest, and current efforts by the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Zoos_and_Aquariums" title="Association of Zoos and Aquariums">Association of Zoos and Aquariums</a> (AZA) include breeding programs and projects to release individuals of this species in similar ecosystems in <a href="/wiki/Coamo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Coamo, Puerto Rico">Coamo</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Karst_(Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Karst (Puerto Rico)">Northern karst</a> of Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Fauna">Fauna</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg/220px-Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg/330px-Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg/440px-Puerto_Rican_crested_toad.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2400" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_crested_toad" title="Puerto Rican crested toad">Puerto Rican crested toad</a></figcaption></figure>\n<p>Approximately half of Puerto Rico\'s birds and nine of sixteen the <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> bird species occur in the Gu\xc3\xa1nica State Forest. In addition to the Puerto Rican crested toad, other protected animal species in the forest include the Puerto Rican vireo (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_vireo" title="Puerto Rican vireo">Vireo latimeri</a></i>), the Puerto Rican nightjar (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_nightjar" title="Puerto Rican nightjar">Antrostomus noctitherus</a></i>), the red land crab (<i><a href="/wiki/Gecarcinus_ruricola" title="Gecarcinus ruricola">Gecarcinus ruricola</a></i>), and the Mona basket shrimp (<i><a href="/wiki/Typhlatya_monae" title="Typhlatya monae">Typhlatya monae</a></i>). The dry forest is also one of the few habitats where the Cook\'s pallid anole (<i><a href="/wiki/Anolis_cooki" title="Anolis cooki">Anolis cooki</a></i>) can be found. The coral reefs and maritime areas of the forest reserve are also home to numerous coral, animal and plant species, such as the <a href="/wiki/West_Indian_manatee" title="West Indian manatee">West Indian manatee</a> which feeds on seaweeds, found in underwater prairies in the reserve.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Flora">Flora</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The vegetation in the forest is divided into three main groups: upland <a href="/wiki/Deciduous_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Deciduous forest">deciduous forest</a> (which occupies 23.5 square kilometres or 9.1 square miles), semi-<a href="/wiki/Evergreen_forest" title="Evergreen forest">evergreen forest</a> (7.2 square kilometres or 2.8 square miles), and scrub forest (5.8 square kilometres or 2.2 square miles).<sup id="cite_ref-Lugo_et_al._7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo_et_al.-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Similar to other insular dry forests species diversity is low; between 30 and 50 tree species are found per hectare.<sup id="cite_ref-Murphy_&amp;_Lugo1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy_&amp;_Lugo1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> More than 700 plant species, of which 48 are endangered and 16 are endemic to the forest, occur within the forest. Some of the endangered plant species in the forest include the sebucan cactus (<i><a href="/wiki/Leptocereus_quadricostatus" title="Leptocereus quadricostatus">Leptocereus quadricostatus</a></i>), the palo de rosa (<i><a href="/wiki/Ottoschulzia_rhodoxylon" title="Ottoschulzia rhodoxylon">Ottoschulzia rhodoxylon</a></i>), the violeta tree (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polygala_cowellii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Polygala cowellii (page does not exist)">Polygala cowellii</a></i>), and the critically endangered bariaco (<i><a href="/wiki/Trichilia_triacantha" title="Trichilia triacantha">Trichilia triacantha</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One of the most famous plant inhabitants of the forest is an old-growth guaiacwood tree or guayac\xc3\xa1n (<i><a href="/wiki/Guaiacum_officinale" title="Guaiacum officinale">Guaiacum officinale</a></i>) often called the <i>Guayac\xc3\xa1n Centenario</i> ("the centenary guaiacwood") which could be as old as 1,000 years-old.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Recreation">Recreation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gu%C3%A1nica_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recreation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The dry forest is open to visitors and some of its beaches are popular with tourists and locals alike. Fishing and biking are allowed in special designated areas, while scuba diving is also popular in the coral reefs and in the beaches. There are 12 hiking trails with varying difficulties open to visitors and the reserve is also popular for <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">birdwatching</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-7"
