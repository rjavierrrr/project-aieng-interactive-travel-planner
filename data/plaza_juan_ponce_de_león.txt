Deco">Art Deco</a> architecture structure is located in <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce, Puerto Rico</a>, and dates from 1926.  It was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Plaza was rebuilt in 1992, under the administration of Mayor <a href="/wiki/Churumba" class="mw-redirect" title="Churumba">Churumba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  It is located in the alley connecting Mayor and Leon streets, in the block between Estrella and Guadalupe streets. The Plaza and the alley are one and the same.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Ponce\'s <i>Plaza de los Perros</i> (English: Plaza of the Dogs), receives its name from the packs of stray dogs that gathered there to feed on the discarded meat scraps. A product of the rapid urban growth experienced in the city during the early 20th century, the Plaza was built in 1926 to relieve the overcrowded farmer\'s market ("Plaza del Mercado") and provide an adequate market place for perishable meat products.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Significance">Significance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Significance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Located across the street from the historic <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Mercado_de_Ponce" class="mw-redirect" title="Plaza de Mercado de Ponce">Plaza del Mercado</a> (Farmer\'s Market) the Plaza de los Perros serves as an urbanistic and commercial complement to the market place. Occupying a long, narrow lot, previously belonging to two back-to-back structures, the meat market connects two of Ponce\'s busiest commercial streets, Mayor and Leon, providing mid-block circulation to the main market by means of the <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_mall" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedestrian mall">pedestrian mall</a> concept so popular today. Of significance is the adaptation of such innovative concepts to traditional social and architectural elements (the farmers\' market and Moorish detailing), representing the contemporary modernistic trends in architecture. The double-function of such a traditional space was an innovation in Ponce and in Puerto Rico as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Of great importance is that the building\'s design bears the signature of <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Carmoega" title="Rafael Carmoega">Rafael Carmoega</a>, one of the most established Puerto Rican architects of the 20th century. A graduate of the <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University_College_of_Architecture,_Art,_and_Planning" title="Cornell University College of Architecture, Art, and Planning">Cornell University School of Architecture</a> and subsequent director of the Architectural division of Puerto Rico\'s Department of the Interior, Carmoega also designed the <a href="/wiki/Capitol_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Capitol of Puerto Rico">Capitol Building</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico,_Rio_Piedras_Campus" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Puerto Rico, Rio Piedras Campus">University of Puerto Rico Main Campus at Rio Piedras</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico,_Medical_Sciences_Campus" title="University of Puerto Rico, Medical Sciences Campus">School of Tropical Medicine</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mayag%C3%BCez_City_Hall" title="Mayag\xc3\xbcez City Hall">Mayaguez City Hall</a>, all listed on the <a href="/wiki/NRHP" class="mw-redirect" title="NRHP">National Register</a>, in addition to other significant buildings. In his interest to preserve Hispanic traditions in the wake of the recent change of sovereignty from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, Carmoega utilized the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Baroque_architecture" title="Spanish Baroque architecture">Spanish Baroque</a> and Neo-<a href="/wiki/Mudejar" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudejar">Mudejar</a> vocabularies in his designs, emphasizing the use of glazed, <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> tiles in many buildings. The Plaza de los Perros is a fine example of this latter style, incorporating glazed mosaics, horseshoe arches, and <a href="/wiki/Balcony" title="Balcony">galleries</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a>-like space for commercial usage.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_3-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Physical_appearance">Physical appearance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical appearance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Overall">Overall</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Overall"><span>edit</span></a><span Rico</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The old "Mercado de las Carnes", or Meat Market, occupies a narrow through-lot connecting Mayor and Leon streets at the center of the block between Estrella and Guadalupe streets.  In plan, the concrete, wood, and sheet-metal structure consists of an elongated quadrangle created by galleries opening onto an attenuated interior court. The neo-<a href="/wiki/Mudejar" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudejar">mudejar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> decorative facades appear to be independent of the actual utilitarian structure inside, as they shift slightly in opposite direction off the central court axis to accommodate the mall.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Fa\xc3\xa7ade"><span id="Fa.C3.A7ade"></span>Fa\xc3\xa7ade</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fa\xc3\xa7ade"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The east and west facades are identical, each consisting of a rectangular mass of approximately 20 feet (6.1&#160;m) in height and 30 in width, which defines the major <a href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" title="Fa\xc3\xa7ade">fa\xc3\xa7ade</a> element. This rectangle is divided into five ground-storey bays: a series of three central bays flanked by slightly narrower wall areas. The bays are defined by a series of four vertical buttress-like piers which extend slightly above the ridge of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Entryways">Entryways</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Entryways"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The three central bays form the <a href="/wiki/Frontispiece_(architecture)" title="Frontispiece (architecture)">frontispiece</a> of the facade, each of similar dimensions and created as an access to the interior court. Each bay contains a horseshoe arch approximately one-half the height of the facade and almost the full width of the bay. Above each arch, a decorative <a href="/wiki/Spandrel" title="Spandrel">spandrel</a> panel contains glazed mosaic tiles in intricate Moorish patterns. Within each bay, a horizontal band stretching between the concrete piers separates the spandrel area from the parapet above. The <a href="/wiki/Parapet" title="Parapet">parapet</a> is completely solid above the central bay, whereas the parapets of the flanking bays are articulated with open, semi-circular concrete forms. At this level, the concrete piers are decorated with vertical rectangular panels of glazed multi-colored mosaic tiles.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The bays flanking the <a href="/wiki/Frontispiece_(architecture)" title="Frontispiece (architecture)">frontispiece</a> are solid and without articulation, although the southern bay has been punctured at ground level by wide, wooden doors as access to a street-side refreshments stand. A concrete <a href="/wiki/Cantilever" title="Cantilever">cantilever</a> shades the entrance and a vertical sign above announces the name of each business.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Interior">Interior</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interior"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>At the interior court, a series of simplified, square, <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_order" title="Tuscan order">Tuscan</a> columns creates a gallery enclosing individual market areas within each bay. The stoa-like galleries are sheltered by a wood and sheet-metal roof pitched inward toward the court. Additional continuous wood and sheet-metal <a href="/wiki/Eave" class="mw-redirect" title="Eave">eaves</a>, stemming from the <a href="/wiki/Colonnade" title="Colonnade">colonnade</a> and suspended by link-chains from the <a href="/wiki/Architrave" title="Architrave">architrave</a> provide shelter within the court for those doing their shopping.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Alterations">Alterations</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Alterations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>This property has remained basically unaltered all elements described contribute to the structure\'s value. The only alteration is the refreshments stands\' sign and the covering of the upper part of the stand\'s arch which are non-contributing elements.<sup id="cite_ref-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joaquin_Acevedo_Cruz_1986._Page_2-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Effect_of_2020_Puerto_Rico_earthquakes">Effect of 2020 Puerto Rico earthquakes</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mercado_de_las_Carnes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect of 2020 Puerto Rico earthquakes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The roof of the structure was damaged due to the <a href="/wiki/2020_Puerto_Rico_earthquakes" class="mw-redirect" title="2020 Puerto Rico earthquakes">2020 Puerto Rico earthquakes</a> and Plaza Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n had to be closed for repairs. As a result, vendors had to vacate their shops and remove their merchandise. Provisional repairs were announced to take approximately two weeks, after which merchants could then move back in. Municipal government plans were to prepare plans for a more permanent restoration of the roof at a later date.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span
