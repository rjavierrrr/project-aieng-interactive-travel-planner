2000</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Hermitage of San Antonio de Padua de la Tuna</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Ermita de San Antonio de Padua de la Tuna</i>) is an archaeological site located near the <a href="/wiki/Guajataca_River" title="Guajataca River">Guajataca River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Coto,_Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Coto, Isabela, Puerto Rico">Coto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela, Puerto Rico</a>, dating from 1730. It comprises the ruins of a village church that was abandoned in the early 19th century when the community, with the permission of Governor <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvador_Mel%C3%A9ndez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Salvador Mel\xc3\xa9ndez (page does not exist)">Salvador Mel\xc3\xa9ndez</a>, moved to a more favorable location nearer the coast, which became the modern <a href="/wiki/Isabela_barrio-pueblo" title="Isabela barrio-pueblo">town of Isabela</a>, founded in 1819.<sup id="cite_ref-AntonioTunaNom_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AntonioTunaNom-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The hermitage was listed on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1983,<sup id="cite_ref-WkList_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WkList-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and on the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Register_of_Historic_Sites_and_Zones" title="Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones">Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones</a> in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ruins are easily accessible from the main <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Highway_2" title="Puerto Rico Highway 2">PR-2</a> road.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermitage_of_San_Antonio_de_Padua_de_la_Tuna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Around 1725, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Spanish Governor of Puerto Rico</a> Jos\xc3\xa9 Antonio de Mendiz\xc3\xa1bal y Azares, granted authorization to base a population on the existing hermitage and village. Its given name, San Antonio de La Tuna, derives from the avocation of the Spanish settlers to the saint <a href="/wiki/Anthony_of_Padua" title="Anthony of Padua">Anthony of Padua</a> and after the abundance of prickly pears growing in the region (<i><a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">Opuntia</a></i> or <i>tuna</i>, in Spanish). At the end of the 18th century San Antonio de la Tuna had a church, more than sixty houses, and almost 1,200 inhabitants, which was a considerable population for those times.\n</p>\n<div
