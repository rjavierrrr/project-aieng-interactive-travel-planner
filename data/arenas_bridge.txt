2000</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Arenas Bridge</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Truss_bridge" title="Truss bridge">truss bridge</a> built in 1894 between the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Cayey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cayey, Puerto Rico">Cayey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cidra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cidra, Puerto Rico">Cidra</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.  Also known as <b>Puente Rio La Plata</b>, it was the longest bridge constructed in Puerto Rico during the Spanish colonial period.  It crosses the <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_la_Plata_(Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio de la Plata (Puerto Rico)">Rio de la Plata</a>, the longest river in Puerto Rico.  According to the U.S. National Park Service, "[t]his is the most important metal bridge in Puerto Rico from the period."<sup id="cite_ref-npsArenasBridge_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npsArenasBridge-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The bridge is still standing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arenas_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The bridge was built as "part of the <a href="/wiki/Carretera_Central_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Carretera Central (Puerto Rico)">Carretera Central</a>, the first highway across the central mountains of Puerto Rico."<sup id="cite_ref-npsArenasBridge_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npsArenasBridge-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The Carretera Central, a 134 kilometer highway between <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> and the port of <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce</a> was largely completed by 1886, with temporary bridges in place at some crossings and 13 permanent bridges completed.<sup id="cite_ref-mpsPRbridges_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpsPRbridges-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: E-7">&#58;&#8202;E-7&#8202;</span></sup>  The installation of the permanent Arenas Bridge in 1894 was near the completion of the Carretera in 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-mpsPRbridges_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpsPRbridges-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: E-7">&#58;&#8202;E-7&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p><p>Because of scarcity of iron in Puerto Rico, there were no metal fabricators in the country.  Instead, bridges like this were fabricated overseas and shipped in parts to Puerto Rico.  A Spanish government agent in Paris secured international bids, largely from French and Belgian firms, for bridges to be built in the overseas colonies of Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-mpsPRbridges_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpsPRbridges-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: E-10">&#58;&#8202;E-10&#8202;</span></sup>  The Arenas Bridge itself was fabricated by <a href="/wiki/Nicaise_et_Delcuve" title="Nicaise et Delcuve">Nicaise et Delcuve</a>, a Belgian firm, and was shipped on the steamship <a href="/wiki/Teutonia_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonia (ship)"><i>Teutonia</i></a> to Puerto Rico for assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-npsArenasBridge_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npsArenasBridge-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>An alternative to a <a href="/wiki/Truss_bridge" title="Truss bridge">truss bridge</a> would have been a <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> design, which was a well-developed technology for long spans before the Arenas Bridge was built.  However, there were no truly long spans in Puerto Rico that would have absolutely required a suspension bridge in colonial times, as there are no particularly wide rivers or canyons, and chief engineer Manuel Sanchez-Nunez assessed that suspension bridges were unsuited to the tropical climate, even removing, a few years later, one that had been constructed in <a href="/wiki/Caguas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Caguas, Puerto Rico">Caguas</a> in 1857.<sup id="cite_ref-mpsPRbridges_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpsPRbridges-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: E-5">&#58;&#8202;E-5&#8202;</span></sup>  As a result, suspension bridge technology was not widely employed in Puerto Rico.  Even today it is believed that there are only a small number of suspension bridges in the commonwealth, one being a modern pedestrian bridge in <a href="/wiki/Bayamon,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayamon, Puerto Rico">Bayamon</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Another one is located in <a href="/wiki/Caguas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Caguas, Puerto Rico">Caguas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and yet <a href="/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_Izcoa_Moure_Bridge" title="Jes\xc3\xbas Izcoa Moure Bridge">another one</a>, a vehicular one, is on the <a href="/wiki/Bayamon,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayamon, Puerto Rico">Bayamon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Naranjito,_Puerto_Rico" title="Naranjito, Puerto Rico">Naranjito</a> border.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The bridge was designed by Mariano Sichar.  A steel <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">truss</a> bridge, it weighed 23,623 pounds and was contracted to be built for 56,221 pesos.  Stone <a href="/wiki/Abutments" class="mw-redirect" title="Abutments">abutments</a> to support the bridge were constructed under supervision of Jos\xc3\xa9 Roque-Paniagua; engineer Julio Larrinaga oversaw assembly of the bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-npsArenasBridge_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npsArenasBridge-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It brings what is now <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Highway_735" title="Puerto Rico Highway 735">Puerto Rico Highway 735</a> over Rio de la Plata between Barrio Monte Llano and Barrio Arenas.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  It is between the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Cayey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cayey, Puerto Rico">Cayey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cidra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cidra, Puerto Rico">Cidra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-npsArenasBridge_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npsArenasBridge-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War, American forces moved from south to north over the Carretara Central.  Another Carretera bridge was demolished by the Spanish to delay the American advance, but not Arenas Bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-mpsPRbridges_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpsPRbridges-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: E-12">&#58;&#8202;E-12&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="References">References</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
