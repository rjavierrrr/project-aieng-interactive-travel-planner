language">Spanish</a> \xe2\x80\x94 is a historic <a href="/wiki/Metre_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Metre gauge"><span class="nowrap">1,000&#160;mm</span></a> (<style .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);clip-path:polygon(0px 0px,0px 0px,0px 0px);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="nowrap">3&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">3<span class="sr-only">+</span><span href="/wiki/Heritage_railway" title="Heritage railway">heritage railroad</a> operating within the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arroyo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Arroyo, Puerto Rico">Arroyo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PR-railroad_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR-railroad-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-travelsports_2-0" class="reference"><a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugar cane</a> plantation line still in existence on the entire island, which was part of a large railroad system that operated around Puerto Rico prior to the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-PR.com-article_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR.com-article-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup href="#cite_note-Railroads_of_Puerto_Rico_/_Ferrocarriles_de_Puerto_Rico_by_Roger_Aponte-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Train_of_the_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The railroad took passengers and sightseers across <a href="/wiki/Arroyo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Arroyo, Puerto Rico">Arroyo\'s</a> old <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugar cane</a> fields on a fifty-minute long guided tour that explained the industry and other historic aspects of it.<sup id="cite_ref-PR-railroad_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR-railroad-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PR.com-article_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR.com-article-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Most of the original railroad right-of-way is former <a href="/wiki/Ponce_and_Guayama_Railroad" title="Ponce and Guayama Railroad">Ponce &amp; Guayama</a> trackage, which was in regular use up until 1990;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> a short 4-mile (6.4&#160;km) segment became the Tren del Sur in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-prfrogui_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prfrogui-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Much of the original equipment abandoned on site also served as historic props along the route.\n</p><p>After a temporary shutdown in 1988, former Arroyo\'s mayor Reinaldo Pirela obtained funds from Puerto Rico\'s government to refit tracks and the transfer of eight <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Locomotive_Works" title="Plymouth Locomotive Works">Plymouth locomotives</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ponce_and_Guayama_Railroad" title="Ponce and Guayama Railroad">Central Aguirre\'s</a> stock to restart successful operations in 1996. In 2005, the railroad was temporarily shutdown pending a major restoration project; although all work has been halted in recent years afterwards for unknown reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-travelsports_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-travelsports-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-prfrogui_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prfrogui-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the Department of the Interior has plans to re-commission the railroad and extend the system further sometime in the near future.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> To enforce legal protection of the Tren del Sur, the Puerto Rico Legislative Branch first approved the Law 118-1995<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and later the Law 212-2014 that superseded the 1995 law, but both laws failed to keep the train running.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Reinaldo Pirela\'s successor, former Arroyo\'s mayor <a href="/wiki/Basilio_Figueroa" title="Basilio Figueroa">Basilio Figueroa</a> (term: 2003-2013), found the Tren del Sur operations too expensive for the Arroyo\'s municipal budget. As a result of past unaccomplished promises of financial assistance from Puerto Rican Legislative and Executive Branches, mayor Figueroa signed a contract with Progressive Southern Railway Inc. (a local company just incorporated in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>) to refurbish and manage the Tren del Sur. When Progressive Southern Railway took control of the Tren del Sur assets in November 2005 under the premise that the "rails needed to be refitted", they dismantled nearly all of the tracks along the 4-mile (6.4&#160;km) route to "send to the United States for refurbishment"; after this, the historical train never resumed operations. Progressive Southern Railway Inc. sued the Arroyo Municipality for remaking a complement of accords, because the Land Authority of Puerto Rico never transferred property of right-of-way to the Municipal Government of Arroyo, asking for $2.5 million in compensation. In 2011, the Arroyo Municipality realized that the lawsuit costs were too expensive, and settled paying $100,000 to Progressive Southern Railway Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Rolling_stock">Rolling stock</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Train_of_the_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rolling stock"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>A number of vintage and historic <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock" title="Rolling stock">rolling stock</a> equipment exists around the grounds of the Train of the South. Locomotives that once operated the tourist trains until 2005 consist of original plantation-era <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Locomotive_Works" title="Plymouth Locomotive Works">Plymouth Locomotive Works</a> diesel switcher engines which range from five 18-ton engines to three 40-ton locomotives.<sup id="cite_ref-critters_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critters-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The 40-ton Plymouth\'s are all painted in a direct mirror of the old <a href="/wiki/Atchison,_Topeka_and_Santa_Fe_Railway" title="Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway">Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway</a> red and silver <i>Warbonnet</i> scheme, including the yellow Santa Fe "cigar band" logo with the railroad\'s name painted within it. Engine #18 was the main locomotive used on all tourist runs, it pulled several converted <a href="/wiki/Flatcar" title="Flatcar">flatcars</a> with custom built interiors to allow for seating of passengers. Along the route, many of the sidings are filled with abandoned sugar cane related freight cars.<sup id="cite_ref-PR-railroad_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PR-railroad-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Engine_Roster">Engine Roster</h3><span
