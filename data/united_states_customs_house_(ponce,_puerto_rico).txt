border: 0;">Added to NRHP</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">10 February 1988</td></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="font-weight:bold; border: 0;">Designated&#160;RNSZH</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">May 16, 2001</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>U.S. Customs House</b> (Spanish: <i>Aduana de Ponce</i>), located at Bonaire and Aduana streets in <a href="/wiki/Barrio" title="Barrio">barrio</a> <a href="/wiki/La_Playa_(de_Ponce)" class="mw-redirect" title="La Playa (de Ponce)">La Playa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce, Puerto Rico</a>, is the oldest <a href="/wiki/Customs_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Customs house">customs house</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_8,_Page_3_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_8,_Page_3-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the only one of its type under the U.S. flag. As of 10 February 1988, the building was owned by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Customs_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Customs Service">U.S. Customs Service</a>, Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The building is listed on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> as "U.S. Custom House".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was listed on 10 February 1988 and later on the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Register_of_Historic_Sites_and_Zones" title="Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones">Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones</a> in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Historical_background">Historical background</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Customs_House_(Ponce,_Puerto_Rico)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Customs_House,_Ponce,_Photo_2.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg/220px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg/330px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg/440px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="658" data-file-height="445" /></a><figcaption>U.S Customs House in Ponce, Puerto Rico. General view, looking southeast.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The Ponce Customs House opened in 1842.  At that time, customs administrative offices and warehouses were located on the first floor, while the second floor was devoted to dwelling units for the director of the customhouse and for the <a href="/wiki/Captain_(nautical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain (nautical)">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Ponce" class="mw-redirect" title="Port of Ponce">Port of Ponce</a>. In 1845 the customhouse survived a terrible fire that destroyed most of the Ponce port vicinity.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During the Spanish-American occupation, In 1898 General <a href="/wiki/Nelson_A._Miles" title="Nelson A. Miles">Nelson A. Miles</a> established the headquarters of the American army in the customhouse during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a>, from which he directed the invasion of the southern part of Puerto Rico. Salvador Brau, noted Puerto Rican historian, served as the head appraiser for the Spanish customs service in Ponce; after the American occupation, he was appointed by General Miles as Customs Administrator of Puerto Rico, a post he held for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossides_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossides-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>During the period of significance, Ponce was the second most populous city in Puerto Rico, with a population of about 70,000, and for most of the period, the second busiest port, after San Juan.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Following a major earthquake in <a href="/wiki/1918_San_Ferm%C3%ADn_earthquake" title="1918 San Ferm\xc3\xadn earthquake">1918</a>, the U.S. Customs Service undertook a comprehensive program of rebuilding and new construction. The remodeling may have been under the supervision of Albert B. Nichols, architect and inspector of buildings under the Supervising Architect of the Treasury\'s San Juan field office. A sheet of drawings, unsigned and undated, for the remodeling are in the San Juan Custom House files, and the Puerto Rico Historic Preservation Office believes that they may be Nichols\'  work. Three other sheets signed by <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Carmoega" title="Rafael Carmoega">Rafael Carmoega</a>, Chief architect of the insular government and presumed to date from August 1930, exist in the Public Building Service records.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The Ponce Custom House was designated a historic customhouse by the Secretary of the Treasury in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossides_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossides-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Despite its 1924 remodeling, it remains an important example of Spanish Colonial architecture outside <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Building_Description">Building Description</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Customs_House_(Ponce,_Puerto_Rico)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Building Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The building is a Spanish-period building which was remodeled by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in 1924, with small changes since then. The two-story building has massive <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> walls and large arched entrances to a central courtyard that reflects its Spanish origins, while the false partial <a href="/wiki/Gable" title="Gable">gables</a> with Spanish barrel tiles are typical of the United States <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Revival">Spanish Revival</a> architectural work of the 1920s. The building is situated on the waterfront facing the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and next to the port of Ponce, two miles from the center of this second-largest city in Puerto Rico. The customs house fully occupies a small city block. Its principal facade faces Bonaire Street and looks toward the ocean across a grassed area containing a Coast Guard beacon. It is located in an area of traditional warehouses, commercial buildings, mostly one-story and many from the Spanish period. There is a sidewalk around the four sides of the building, with the one on Bonaire Street wider and providing three mature trees.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Customs_House,_Ponce,_Photo_4.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg/220px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg/330px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg/440px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="646" data-file-height="440" /></a><figcaption>U.S Customs House in Ponce, Puerto Rico. Passage and courtyard, looking north.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The building is a simple rectangle measuring 97 feet wide x 83 feet deep. The plan is typical of Spanish construction, with interconnected rooms, around the outside perimeter that also open to a central <a href="/wiki/Courtyard" title="Courtyard">courtyard</a>. There is a wide arched vehicular passage leading from the front through the courtyard to the rear exit from the building. The original courtyard has been partially closed in on the first floor only on each side of the passageway to create two additional rooms. Between the front rooms and the courtyard, a fine masonry staircase rises on the left side off the central passage. The second floor repeats the first-floor layout of perimeter rooms with later modifications, but includes the full extent of the original courtyard or patio in the center. Originally there may have been a roofed courtyard gallery for circulation around the courtyard. At present, there are two short modern gallery sections bridging between the quarry-tile-paved roofs of the infilled first-floor courtyard areas. At the head of the stairs is a large public lobby that opens to the courtyard and to the front of the building. There is also a small circular iron stair to the roof from the southeast corner of the second-floor courtyard.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Customs_House,_Ponce,_Photo_3.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_3.jpg/170px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="252" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_3.jpg/255px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="293" data-file-height="435" /></a><figcaption>U.S Customs House in Ponce, Puerto Rico. Main entrance, looking north.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The facades are similar with regular door and window openings and with small <a href="/wiki/Balcony" title="Balcony">balconies</a> to the second-floor openings. There is a small partial false gable roof of Spanish barrel red-clay tiles around the four sides and around the courtyard. On the front (south) facade, facing the ocean, there are five bays with a large elliptical arch-headed passageway large enough for vehicular traffic. It is architecturally marked only by a plain, flat frame painted differently from the body of the building. There are plain board, double doors with a smaller pedestrian port in the right door. In the elliptical arch there is a simple fanlight window. Flanking the arched entrance are two large high windows on each side. The windows are two-leaf casements with exterior solid and Jouvered combination paneled shutters. Metal grilles are at each window. The walls have a simple low projecting base course, and the first floor walls are marked by horizontal incised coursing, all in <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>. There is a large molded string course between stories and on the second floor, five door openings, each with double-leaf doors that are half sash, which replaced the original <a href="/wiki/Louvered" class="mw-redirect" title="Louvered">louvered</a> and paneled shutter combinations. Over the doors are sashed transoms, although the right group has been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioner" class="mw-redirect" title="Air conditioner">air conditioners</a>. Each opening has a decorative <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Balcony" title="Balcony">balcony</a> with scroll support dating from the U.S. remodeling of the building. Previous to these wrought-iron balconies, the building had <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> railings. "U.S. Customs" is lettered over the central window. At the top is a large double masonry molded cornice, surmounted by the small false partial gable in Spanish tiles, both from 1924. On the front only, there are three flag poles on the roof, and a large antenna system. On each side of the first floor passage entry is an alarm box and a plain light fixture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7,_Page_2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The rear facade is identical to the front, except that the right first-floor opening is a plain board door rather than a window and that some of the second-floor doors still retain their original louvered and paneled shutter systems and have not had glass inserted.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The side facades are similar to the front and rear, except for the absence of the arched passageway. Both sides are in five bays, all windows, on the first floor, with the exception of a door on the east side. The second-floor openings and balconies match the front with air conditioners generally mounted in the transoms. On the west side one second-floor door has a modern boxed-out bar grille around it.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Construction of the original 1841-42 building is in traditional heavy masonry walls two feet thick.  The ceilings are high: 15\' on the first floor, 14\' on the second. There are several large structural <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arch</a> openings in the interior masonry walls. The exposed floor joists are heavy 4x10 beaded edge <a href="/wiki/Ausubo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ausubo">ausubo</a> wood members laid 24" on center. These old joists support modern concrete slab second floor laid over wire mesh and composition board forms laid during the 20th century remodeling. Also added at this point was reinforced concrete framing adjoining the old walls on the first floor. It is now in deteriorated condition, while the original walls appear generally sound.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.1,_Page_5-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the interior, the central passage extends from front to rear through the open courtyard, paved in <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarry</a> tiles. On the left side at the front of the building, are two <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="Immigration and Naturalization Service">Immigration and Naturalization Service</a> offices. On the right front are two <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a> offices. Behind the offices on the left side of the passage is a fine enclosed stair with two runs and a landing. The remaining first-floor rooms are storage rooms and a <a href="/wiki/Retailing" class="mw-redirect" title="Retailing">shop</a> with concrete floors, several 8\' wide masonry openings with slat fanlights (sol truncos). Storage room windows are solid vertical board shutters without sash and with outside metal grilles. Two rooms flanking the passage in the center fill in an original part of the courtyard in a manner similar to the older construction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Customs_House,_Ponce,_Photo_6.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_6.jpg/170px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_6.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="246" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_6.jpg/255px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="304" data-file-height="440" /></a><figcaption>U.S Customs House in Ponce, Puerto Rico. Second floor stair hall and stair arcade, looking northwest.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>On the second floor, the staircase from the entry passage opens on a large lobby from a two-arched staircase arcade. The lobby extends from the front back to the courtyard and is floored with diagonally laid gray and white marble tiles. The walls, as are old walls in the building, are plastered, and the ceiling has exposed 4x10 beaded ausubo wood beams. Old double four-paneled doors and a transom open to the courtyard. The window in the stairhall has been filled in with glass brick.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Customs_House,_Ponce,_Photo_5.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg/220px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg/330px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg/440px-US_Customs_House%2C_Ponce%2C_Photo_5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="658" data-file-height="443" /></a><figcaption>U.S Customs House in Ponce, Puerto Rico. First floor, east side, store room, looking southwest.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The offices on the second floor generally have been modernized with acoustical tile ceilings, fluorescent fixtures and vinyl floors. The old exterior doors remain, generally with glass replacing the shuttered sections, except for several windows on the rear <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">lavatories</a> and kitchen. Air conditioners are mounted in the <a href="/wiki/Transom_(architectural)" class="mw-redirect" title="Transom (architectural)">transoms</a> over the doors. On the southeast corner is a large inspector\'s office, with a counter for public business. Some old masonry partitions had been removed or altered to create this space. Along the east side, small rooms have been partitioned off and in the rear northeast corner is the large Port Director\'s office, in its original configuration. Across the north rear are a men\'s lavatory, a women\'s lavatory and a kitchen, all with black and white floor tiles. The kitchen appears to be original to the building and has traces of the original masonry cooking stove and <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">flue</a>. The left rear north corner room now used for training is also floored in old black and white tiles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the San Juan customs house files, there is one large sheet of remodeling plans for the Ponce Customs house, possibly by Albert B. Nichols, although not signed. In 1985 Customs Service commissioned a set of fifteen measured drawings by architect Rene Acosta, Hato Rey, P. R. which are reproduced in a report by the architect on the rehabilitation needs of the Ponce Custom House. (Feasibility Study for the Rehabilitation of the U.S. Ponce Custom House, Ponce, P. R.)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMaxwell1988Section_7.2,_Page_6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Significance">Significance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Customs_House_(Ponce,_Puerto_Rico)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>This United States Custom House in Ponce is the oldest extant customhouse in Puerto Rico. Built in 1841-42, the <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> building of the Spanish era was remodeled by the United States in 1924. It is historically and architecturally significant for its antiquity and its importance in the Spanish period of customs administration in Puerto Rico, as it is the only one of its type among U.S. Customs Service properties. It is also significant for the role it played in the transitional phase of the American customs service in Puerto Rico, from 1898 to 1930. This period is bracketed at one end by the cession on 10 December 1898 of the island of Puerto Rico to the United States by Spain and on the other end by the completion in 1930 of the major building and rehabilitation program undertaken by the U.S. Customs Service following World War I. The Ponce Custom House is an important example of the Spanish-Colonial architecture outside of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>.<sup
