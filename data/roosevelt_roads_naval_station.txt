The site operates today as <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Aponte_de_la_Torre_Airport" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte de la Torre Airport">Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte de la Torre Airport</a>, a public use airport.\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Maryland_(SSBN-738)_1997.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg/220px-USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg/330px-USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg/440px-USS_Maryland_%28SSBN-738%29_1997.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1000" /></a><figcaption> Ohio-class ballistic missile submarine USS Maryland, Roosevelt Roads Naval Station, 1997</figcaption></figure>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1919, future US President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_the_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Assistant Secretary of the Navy">Assistant Secretary of the Navy</a>, toured <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, visiting Ceiba. When he returned to the White House, he expressed a liking for the terrain where the base was to be located. This was during the World War&#160;I-era, and the US could benefit from an air field in Ceiba. While Puerto Rico is a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._insular_area)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. insular area)">Commonwealth</a>, its territorial rights belong to the US, which made it feasible for the US government to build an air base in Ceiba.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It took many years for the US to become convinced of the need for an air base in Ceiba. When <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> began to invade other European countries, the US, led by then President Roosevelt, considered the idea of a naval air station in Ceiba. With war in the <a href="/wiki/European_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="European theatre of World War II">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean_theater_of_World_War_II" title="Pacific Ocean theater of World War II">Pacific theatres</a>, they saw an airbase in the Caribbean as necessary. President Roosevelt ordered the creation of the base in 1940. In 1941, $50<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million (equivalent to $1036&#160;million in 2023) was appropriated to develop a protected anchorage in the sea area between Puerto Rico and <a href="/wiki/Vieques" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieques">Vieques</a>, an area later named Roosevelt Roads by <a href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_the_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Secretary of the Navy">Navy Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Knox" title="Frank Knox">Frank Knox</a> on 15 May 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On 22 August 1941, President Roosevelt signed a naval works among other things, authorized another $21.97<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million (equivalent to $455&#160;million in 2023) for a protected fleet anchorage at Roosevelt Roads.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The base had been inaugurated, but scaled down to maintenance status with a public works office in 1944. From then until 1957, the base endured many shifts, being opened seven times and closed eight times. Meanwhile, it continued as a source of employment for the citizens of Ceiba.\n</p><p>In 1957, it was upgraded to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval Station">Naval Station</a> status. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fort_Bundy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fort Bundy (page does not exist)">Fort Bundy</a> was located there, but it crossed over to parts of <a href="/wiki/Vieques,_Puerto_Rico" title="Vieques, Puerto Rico">Vieques</a>, a fact that became important in the future. A US military mission, the M3, was located there. It was part of the "Naval Computer and Telecommunications Station, Puerto Rico Base Communication Department". M3 had a fleet center, a technical control facility and a Tactical support communications department among other things. The M3 was designated to help Puerto Rico, the US and other Caribbean and Latin American countries to deal with drug trafficking, illegal immigration and other problems. The main purpose of the base was tactical support for land/sea/air maneuvers at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_in_Vieques,_Puerto_Rico" title="United States Navy in Vieques, Puerto Rico">Naval Training Range in Vieques</a>.\n</p><p>In 1969, the US Navy established Camp Moscrip which held a rotating US Navy Construction Battalion (<a href="/wiki/Seabee" title="Seabee">Seabee</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Seabee_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seabee-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>For the next 47 years the base was utilized for flight practice, as well as other missions and control of the area\'s air space. In August 2002, a <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_MC-130" title="Lockheed MC-130">MC-130H</a> airplane carrying seven airmen crashed in the town of Caguas, while en route from Roosevelt Roads to <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Airport" title="Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport">Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguadilla, Puerto Rico">Aguadilla</a>. All seven perished, in the largest air tragedy in Caguas\'s history.\n</p><p>Within the industrial area the drydock, a bombproof power plant, a sewage pumping station, and a machine shop were completed. The drydock, 1100 by 155 feet, and built in the dry, was first used  in July 1943. The power plant, a bombproof structure with 4-foot-thick concrete walls, was equipped with two 5,000-kw steam-driven generators.  The drydock was dedicated on 15 February 1944, and the Bolles Drydock, in memory of Captain Harry A. Bolles, (CEC) USN, who was killed in Alaska in World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2003, <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Natter" title="Robert J. Natter">Robert J. Natter</a> said that, with the upcoming closure of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_in_Vieques,_Puerto_Rico" title="United States Navy in Vieques, Puerto Rico">Naval Training Range in Vieques</a>, Roosevelt Roads was no longer needed by the Navy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Later that year, a military appropriations bill required the Secretary of the Navy to close within six months of the enactment of the act.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The base officially closed on 31 March 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-goodbye_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodbye-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At the time, there were nearly 1,200 active-duty officers and sailors at Roosevelt Roads.<sup id="cite_ref-goodbye_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodbye-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Special_Operations_Command" title="United States Special Operations Command">United States Special Operations Command South</a> moved from Roosevelt Roads to <a href="/wiki/Homestead_Air_Reserve_Base" title="Homestead Air Reserve Base">Homestead Air Reserve Base</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Forces_Southern_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command">U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command</a> moved from Roosevelt Roads to <a href="/wiki/Mayport_Naval_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayport Naval Station">Mayport Naval Station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Naval Mobile Construction Battalion (NMCB) 74 (<a href="/wiki/Seabee" title="Seabee">Seabee</a>) moved from Roosevelt Roads to <a href="/wiki/Little_Creek,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Creek, Virginia">Little Creek, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Seabee_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seabee-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> When Roosevelt Roads closed, the only U.S. naval base in the Caribbean was the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> From the time that Congress voted to close the base until its closure, Roosevelt Roads closed faster than any other military installation on US soil in several decades.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After its closure, 200 sailors and civilians remained to help in the transition from a naval base to a naval agency coordinating the closing process.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Of the former base\'s property, about 30% was transferred to the government of Puerto Rico and its municipalities, 40% became a wetlands preserve, and the remainder was offered for sale at public auction.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="US_Naval_Forces_Southern_Command">US Naval Forces Southern Command</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a data-file-height="2810" /></a><figcaption>US Naval Station Roosevelt Roads entrance sign 1986</figcaption></figure>\n<p><a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Forces_Southern_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command">U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command</a> (NAVSO) directs naval forces and interacts with partner nations to shape the maritime environment within United States Southern Command\'s Area of Focus (AOF). With a focus on Theater Security Cooperation, NAVSO works to strengthen and build effective alliance and friendships, develop partner nation capabilities, and maintain US operational access to defend the US.\n</p><p>U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command (COMUSNAVSO), headquartered at Naval Station Roosevelt Roads, Puerto Rico, was the Naval Component Commander to the U.S. Southern Command, based in Miami, Florida. It provides strategic and operational command and control for U.S. Naval Forces in South America, Central America and the Caribbean.\n</p><p>On 10 December 1999 the US Navy established Commander, U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command, also known as NAVSOUTH, in a ceremony aboard <a href="/wiki/USS_Vicksburg_(CG-69)" title="USS Vicksburg (CG-69)">USS&#160;<i>Vicksburg</i>&#160;(CG-69)</a> inport Roosevelt Roads. The new organization is responsible for Navy operational forces in the United States Southern Command\'s area of responsibility (AOR), including Naval Special Warfare Unit Four, a training detachment for SEAL teams from the SOCOM operational area. It oversees US naval forces participating in drug enforcement operations and interaction with South American naval forces, including the annual UNITAS operations around South America. As the Navy\'s senior representative, Commander, United States Naval Forces, Southern Command (COMUSNAVSO) serves as the principal liaison with the government of Puerto Rico. In January 2004 The Navy decided to relocate U.S. Naval Forces Southern Command (USNAVSO) from Naval Station Roosevelt Roads, Puerto Rico, to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Mayport" title="Naval Station Mayport">Naval Station Mayport</a>, Florida. Since the Navy had to close Naval Station Roosevelt Roads by 31 March 2004, relocation of USNAVSO was a high priority.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Life_on_Roosevelt_Roads">Life on Roosevelt Roads</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life on Roosevelt Roads"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Roosevelt Roads Naval Station, or Roosey (pronounced "Rosy") as it was frequently called, was home to hundreds of military personnel and dependents. Education for children of those based at the Station was initially provided by teachers who were DOD contractors from the US. Later, more local teachers were hired.\n</p><p>Radio and TV entertainment on the base during the mid-1970s was limited. News and information broadcasts were provided by Navy and Marine Corps journalists from the studios of The American Forces Caribbean Network (AFCN). Families could tune into the on-base AFRTS radio and TV station\'s family-oriented shows, or rig an antenna for signals from St. Thomas or San Juan. At one time, the AFCN operated repeater transmitters broadcasting radio and TV to San Juan, and <a href="/wiki/Ramey_Air_Force_Base" title="Ramey Air Force Base">Ramey Air Force Base</a>, located on the west coast of Puerto Rico.\n</p><p>At the El Coqui Theater, in the Bundy area of the base, movie goers watched films as swifts, a small batlike bird, flitted across the screen. Bats were abundant on the island, but the brightly-lit screen attracted these small birds whose stiff-winged flight was easily distinguishable from the flutter of a bat. Birds hunt using superior eyesight and the projector provided welcome light for them in the darkened theater while bats who depend on echo-location do not need it and were seen only incidentally. But both served as first-class controls on the persistent infestation of mosquitoes (and Mimis, an almost invisible biting midge with a most painful bite) encountered everywhere on the island.\n</p><p>The base equestrian <a href="/wiki/Stables" class="mw-redirect" title="Stables">stables</a> were near the golf course aka:Lake Bundy. Most of the \'horses\' were technically <a href="/wiki/Pony" title="Pony">ponies</a>, although several thoroughbreds, washouts from race tracks, occasionally found a home on base.\n</p><p>The base flying club had many members, and met regularly to plan trips and activities. Flight instruction was available to all members.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="The_Vieques_effect">The Vieques effect</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Vieques effect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Navy-Vieques_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy-Vieques protests">Navy-Vieques protests</a></div>\n<p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/David_Sanes" class="mw-redirect" title="David Sanes">David Sanes</a>, a civilian employed as a security guard by the US Navy, died from a stray bomb while observing a routine exercise. As a consequence of this many Vieques citizens and Puerto Rican activists from other towns (<a href="/wiki/Ruben_Berrios" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruben Berrios">Ruben Berrios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tito_Kayak" title="Tito Kayak">Tito Kayak</a>, etc.) began activism against the military presence in Vieques, which included illegally entering the live -fire areas at the military reservation. Other important activists included <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Jackson" title="Jesse Jackson">Jesse Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kennedy_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Kennedy Jr.">Robert Kennedy Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Sharpton" title="Al Sharpton">Al Sharpton</a>, US Representative <a href="/wiki/Luis_Guti%C3%A9rrez" title="Luis Guti\xc3\xa9rrez">Luis Guti\xc3\xa9rrez</a> D-IL, US Representative <a href="/wiki/Nydia_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Nydia Vel\xc3\xa1zquez">Nydia Vel\xc3\xa1zquez</a> D-NY, <a href="/wiki/Rigoberta_Mench%C3%BA" title="Rigoberta Mench\xc3\xba">Rigoberta Mench\xc3\xba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_James_Olmos" title="Edward James Olmos">Edward James Olmos</a> (the last was jailed in Puerto Rico for trespassing on federal property). An agreement between the Clinton Administration and the Government of Puerto Rico (then Gov. Rosello) agreed to cease all live bombing on Vieques by March 2003.\n</p><p>Fort Bundy was affected by the protests, because part of it was in Vieques. After Sanes\' death, there was a struggle between the (State equivalent) constitutional <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Government of Puerto Rico">Government of Puerto Rico</a> and the US Government. This was over after President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> granted Puerto Rico the rights to operate the former military possessions in Vieques, including Fort Bundy, a portion of which was aboard Roosevelt Roads Naval Station. As a consequence of President Bush\'s decision, the Roosevelt Roads Naval Station became NAPR (Naval Activity Puerto Rico), where DoD Police provides security, but is a military installation. On 31 March 2004, the station again became inoperative.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="The_future">The future</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The future"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>After the military left the station, Puerto Rican Governor <a href="/wiki/Sila_Mar%C3%ADa_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Sila Mar\xc3\xada Calder\xc3\xb3n">Sila Mar\xc3\xada Calder\xc3\xb3n</a> announced that her party, the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Democratic_Party_of_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular Democratic Party of Puerto Rico">PPD</a>, had political goals to turn the base into an <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a>. This was backed in 2005 by the then-new Governor, <a href="/wiki/An%C3%ADbal_Acevedo_Vil%C3%A1" title="An\xc3\xadbal Acevedo Vil\xc3\xa1">An\xc3\xadbal Acevedo Vil\xc3\xa1</a>, who asserted that the area might be converted to an airport. He also stated that Ceiba as a town would serve as an economic and tourist center for eastern Puerto Rico, and that converting the former military base into a civil airport would be part of a plan to open seven or eight large airports in Puerto Rico.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>As of January 2009, approximately 2,900 acres (12&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of the former Naval Station was being marketed to the public by the Los Angeles group of Colliers International,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> on behalf of the Navy\'s Base Realignment and Closure Program Management Office,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as a public auction to commence in the near future. The remaining portion is in the process of being conveyed to the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico and other Federal agencies in various stages. Since November 2008, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Ports_Authority" title="Puerto Rico Ports Authority">Puerto Rico Ports Authority</a> operates the <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Aponte_de_la_Torre_Airport" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte de la Torre Airport">Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte de la Torre Airport</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, the former Roosevelt Roads Naval Station was under consideration as a possible location for the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> private launch site, but was not selected.<sup id="cite_ref-faa201304v1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faa201304v1-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/CBP_Air_and_Marine_Operations" title="CBP Air and Marine Operations">U.S. Customs and Border Protection Air and Marine Operations</a> began marine operations at a new facility at Roosevelt Roads Naval Station on 30 August 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Marine Environmental Remediation (MER) Group signed an agreement with the LRA to rent Pier 3 as a ship recycling facility in April 2016.  MER Group is the first true GREEN recycling facility within the US.  There are currently three ships, Seven Polaris, Lone Star and the Atlantic VII, at the pier for recycling.  The company will bring over 700 jobs to the local area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>As of October 2018, <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Rossell%C3%B3" title="Ricardo Rossell\xc3\xb3">Ricardo Rossell\xc3\xb3</a>\'s administration made it the launching port to <a href="/wiki/Vieques" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieques">Vieques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culebra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Culebra, Puerto Rico">Culebra</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In mid-March 2020, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Aponte_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte Hern\xc3\xa1ndez">Jos\xc3\xa9 Aponte Hern\xc3\xa1ndez</a>, a former Speaker of the House of Representatives of Puerto Rico said he would request from <a href="/wiki/Jenniffer_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Col%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Jenniffer Gonz\xc3\xa1lez Col\xc3\xb3n">Jenniffer Gonz\xc3\xa1lez Col\xc3\xb3n</a>, (the resident commissioner of Puerto Rico) for funds to restore a former hospital to operational status. What was discussed was the possibility of using the former hospital located on the Roosevelt Roads Naval Station to treat persons affected by the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Puerto_Rico" title="COVID-19 pandemic in Puerto Rico">COVID-19 pandemic in Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hurricane_Maria_rescue">Hurricane Maria rescue</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hurricane Maria rescue"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 2017, Roosevelt Roads military personnel arrived at Roosevelt Roads after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> to assist in rescue efforts. Units such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> 821st Contingency Response Support Squadron, 821st Contingency Response Group, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division</a> Aviation Brigade and the <a href="/wiki/101st_Airborne_Division" title="101st Airborne Division">101st Airborne Division</a> "Dustoff" unit arrived at Roosevelt Roads. Marines and sailors set up a supply staging base receiving around-the-clock airlifts at Roosevelt Roads. This was the first major military activity at Roosevelt Roads since 2004.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2
