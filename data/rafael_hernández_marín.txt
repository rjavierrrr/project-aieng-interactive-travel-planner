Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Mar\xc3\xadn</b> (October 24, 1892 \xe2\x80\x93 December 11, 1965) was a Puerto Rican songwriter and the author of hundreds of popular songs in the Latin American repertoire.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He specialized in Cuban styles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Canci%C3%B3n" title="Canci\xc3\xb3n">canci\xc3\xb3n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolero" title="Bolero">bolero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guaracha" title="Guaracha">guaracha</a>. Among his most famous compositions are "<a href="/wiki/Lamento_Borincano" title="Lamento Borincano">Lamento Borincano</a>,\xe2\x80\x9d "Capullito de alhel\xc3\xad,\xe2\x80\x9d "Campanitas de cristal,\xe2\x80\x9d "Cachita,\xe2\x80\x9d "<a href="/wiki/Silencio_(Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_song)" title="Silencio (Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez song)">Silencio</a>,\xe2\x80\x9d "El cumbanchero,\xe2\x80\x9d "Ausencia,\xe2\x80\x9d and "Perfume de gardenias.\xe2\x80\x9d\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Career">Career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Early_years">Early years</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Mar\xc3\xadn was born in the town of <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguadilla, Puerto Rico">Aguadilla, Puerto Rico</a>, into a poor family, on October 24, 1892. His parents were Mar\xc3\xada Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Mar\xc3\xadn and Miguel Angel Rosa, though he was given only his mother\'s surname.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As a child, he learned the craft of <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigar</a> making, from which he made a modest living. He also grew to love music and asked his parents to permit him to become a full-time music student. When he was 12 years old, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez studied music in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>, under the guidance of music professors <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jose_Ruellan_Lequenica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jose Ruellan Lequenica (page does not exist)">Jose Ruellan Lequenica</a> and Jes\xc3\xbas Figueroa. He learned to play many musical instruments, including the <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Soul_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soul-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At 14, he played for the Cocolia Orquestra. Hern\xc3\xa1ndez moved to San Juan where he played for the municipal orchestra under the director <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Tizol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Manuel Tizol (page does not exist)">Manuel Tizol</a>. In 1913, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez begot his first child, Antonio Hern\xc3\xa1ndez (out of wedlock), to Ana Bone.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="World_War_I_and_the_Orchestra_Europe">World War I and the Orchestra Europe</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War I and the Orchestra Europe"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Rafael_Hernandez_with_nephew.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Rafael_Hernandez_with_nephew.jpg/220px-Rafael_Hernandez_with_nephew.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Rafael_Hernandez_with_nephew.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="235" data-file-height="195" /></a><figcaption>Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez (left) with his brother Jes\xc3\xbas during World War I, c. 1917.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In 1917, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez was working as a musician in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, when the United States entered World War I. The <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> bandleader, <a href="/wiki/James_Reese_Europe" title="James Reese Europe">James Reese Europe</a>, recruited brothers Rafael and Jes\xc3\xbas Hern\xc3\xa1ndez, along with 16 more Puerto Ricans, to join the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>\'s Harlem Hell fighters musical band, the Orchestra Europe. He enlisted and was assigned to the <a href="/wiki/369th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="369th Infantry Regiment (United States)">U.S. 369th Infantry Regiment</a> (formerly known as the 15th Infantry Regiment, New York National Guard, created in New York City June 2, 1913). The regiment, nicknamed "The Harlem Hell Fighters,\xe2\x80\x9d by the Germans, served in France. Hern\xc3\xa1ndez toured Europe with the Orchestra Europe. The 369th was awarded the French <a href="/wiki/Croix_de_Guerre" title="Croix de Guerre">Croix de Guerre</a> for battlefield gallantry by the President of France.<sup id="cite_ref-STL_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STL-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Hernandez_and_Pedro_Flores,_Life_in_Cuba_and_Mexico"><span id="Hernandez_and_Pedro_Flores.2C_Life_in_Cuba_and_Mexico"></span>Hernandez and Pedro Flores, Life in Cuba and Mexico</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hernandez and Pedro Flores, Life in Cuba and Mexico"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>After World War I ended, he returned to the United States in 1919. Hern\xc3\xa1ndez began a long and intense period of artistic composition and performance. He was part of the Lucky Roberts Band, with which he made his first musical tour of the United States. Later, he moved to Cuba, where he directed the Fausto Theater orchestra in Havana.<sup id="cite_ref-Rafael_Hern\xc3\xa1ndez-Mar\xc3\xadn_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rafael_Hern\xc3\xa1ndez-Mar\xc3\xadn-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Later on, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez moved to New York City. In 1925,<sup id="cite_ref-Rafael_Hern\xc3\xa1ndez-Mar\xc3\xadn_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rafael_Hern\xc3\xa1ndez-Mar\xc3\xadn-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> he started writing songs and organized a trio called "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Trio_Borincano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trio Borincano (page does not exist)">Trio Borincano</a>.\xe2\x80\x9d In 1926, fellow Puerto Rican <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Flores_(composer)" title="Pedro Flores (composer)">Pedro Flores</a>, who was a composer, joined the Trio. Even though Hern\xc3\xa1ndez and Flores became and always remained good friends, they soon went their separate ways and artistically competed against each other. After the trio broke up, he formed a quartet called "Cuarteto Victoria," which included singer <a href="/wiki/Myrta_Silva" title="Myrta Silva">Myrta Silva</a>, also known as <i>La Guarachera</i> and <i>La Gorda de Oro</i>. With both groups, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez traveled and played his music all over the United States and Latin America.<sup id="cite_ref-RH_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RH-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On September 2, 1927, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez\'s sister, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Victoria Hern\xc3\xa1ndez">Victoria</a>, opened <a href="/wiki/Casa_Amadeo,_antigua_Casa_Hernandez" title="Casa Amadeo, antigua Casa Hernandez">Casa Hernandez</a>, a music store which also acted as a booking agency and base of operations for her brother.<sup id="cite_ref-STL_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STL-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1929, Tr\xc3\xado Borinquen recorded <i>Linda Quisqueya</i> (originally titled <i>Linda Borinquen</i>) and that same year, he founded the "Cuarteto Victoria" (also known as "El Cuarteto Rico") named after his sister.<sup id="cite_ref-STL_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STL-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1932, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez moved to Mexico. There, he directed an orchestra and enrolled in Mexico\'s National Music Conservatory, to further enrich his musical knowledge. Hern\xc3\xa1ndez also became an actor and organized musical scores in Mexico\'s "golden age" of movies. His wife (and eventual widow) is Mexican.<sup id="cite_ref-Hernandez_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="&quot;Lamento_borincano&quot;_and_&quot;Preciosa&quot;"><span id=".22Lamento_borincano.22_and_.22Preciosa.22"></span>"Lamento borincano" and "Preciosa"</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: &quot;Lamento borincano&quot; and &quot;Preciosa&quot;"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1937, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez wrote "<a href="/wiki/Lamento_Borincano" title="Lamento Borincano">Lamento Borincano</a>.\xe2\x80\x9d That same year, he also wrote "<a href="/wiki/Preciosa_(song)" title="Preciosa (song)">Preciosa</a>.\xe2\x80\x9d In 1947, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez returned to Puerto Rico and became the director of the orchestra at the government-owned <a href="/wiki/WIPR_(AM)" title="WIPR (AM)">WIPR</a> Radio.<sup id="cite_ref-RH_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RH-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Hernandez also composed <a href="/wiki/Christmas_music" title="Christmas music">Christmas music</a>, Danzas, Zarzuelas, Guarachas, Lullabies, Boleros, <a href="/wiki/Waltz" title="Waltz">Waltzes</a>, and more.<sup id="cite_ref-RH_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RH-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Hern\xc3\xa1ndez\'s works include "Ahora seremos felices" (Now, We Will Be Happy), "Campanitas de cristal" (Crystal Bells), "Capullito de Alhel\xc3\xad,\xe2\x80\x9d "Culpable" (Guilty), "El Cumbanchero"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (also known as <i>"Rockfort Rock"</i> or <i>"Comanchero" (sic)</i> to <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> aficionados), "Ese soy yo" (That\'s Me), "Perfume de Gardenias" (Gardenia\'s Perfume), "<a href="/wiki/Silencio_(Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_song)" title="Silencio (Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez song)">Silencio</a>" (Silence), and "T\xc3\xba no comprendes" (You Don\'t Understand), among 3,000 others. Many of his compositions were strongly based on Cuban musical idioms, such as the guaracha\'s "<a href="/wiki/Cachita_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cachita (song)">Cachita</a>" and "Buchipluma na\' ma,\xe2\x80\x9d  which were often mistaken as songs by Cuban authors.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> His music became an important part of Puerto Rican culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Hernandez_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Later_years_and_death">Later years and death</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later years and death"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Hern\xc3\xa1ndez was Honorary President of the Authors and Composers Association. He was also the founder of little league <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> in Puerto Rico. President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> christened him "Mr. Cumbanchero".<sup id="cite_ref-Soul_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soul-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Hern\xc3\xa1ndez died in San Juan on December 11, 1965, shortly after <a href="/wiki/Popular,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Popular, Inc.">Banco Popular</a> de Puerto Rico produced a TV special in his honor in which he addressed the people for the last time. The special was simulcast on all TV and most island radio stations. The TV special was rebroadcast on May 13, 2007. Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez\'s remains are buried in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_Magdalena_de_Pazzis_Cemetery" title="Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery">Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legacy">Legacy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Mar%C3%ADn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Puerto Rico has honored his memory by naming public buildings, avenues, and schools after him. The airport in Aguadilla is named <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Hern%C3%A1ndez_Airport" title="Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport">Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Airport</a>. There are schools in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a> named after Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez. Renowned Puerto Rican sculptor <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Batista" title="Tom\xc3\xa1s Batista">Tom\xc3\xa1s Batista</a> created a statue of Hern\xc3\xa1ndez, which is in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Bayam%C3%B3n,_Puerto_Rico" title="Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico">Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Interamerican_University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Interamerican University of Puerto Rico">Interamerican University of Puerto Rico</a>, the repository of his works, operates a small museum in his honor at its Metropolitan Campus in San Juan, which is directed by his son, Alejandro (Chal\xc3\xad) Hern\xc3\xa1ndez. The <a href="/wiki/Hernandez_Houses" title="Hernandez Houses">Hernandez Houses</a> housing complex in New York City is named after Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez.\n</p><p>At the behest former senator <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Arce" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucy Arce">Lucy Arce</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Senate of Puerto Rico">Senate of Puerto Rico</a>, under the first term of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rivera_Schatz" title="Thomas Rivera Schatz">Thomas Rivera Schatz</a> built the Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez Plaza with a bigger-than-lifesize statue of the composer, and a statue of a Puerto Rican j\xc3\xadbaro riding a horse honoring his <b>Lamento Borincano</b>.  The park is located at the easternmost tip of San Juan\'s <b>Paseo de la Covadonga</b>.\n</p><p>\nPuerto Rican singer <a href="/wiki/Marc_Anthony" title="Marc Anthony">Marc Anthony</a> recorded Hern\xc3\xa1ndez\'s "Preciosa" and sang the song in a 2005 concert in New York City\'s <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a>. According to an article in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>: <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Mr. Anthony did his version of <i>Preciosa</i>. It may have been the night\'s most popular love song, precisely because it\'s not about a woman: it\'s about a whole island, instead."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In 1969, Puerto Rican actor Orlando Rodriguez played Hernandez in the bio-pic <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Jibarito_Rafael&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="El Jibarito Rafael (page does not exist)">El Jibarito Rafael</a></i>, which was directed by the Mexican actor <a href="/wiki/Juli%C3%A1n_Soler" title="Juli\xc3\xa1n Soler">Juli\xc3\xa1n Soler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999, Hern\xc3\xa1ndez was posthumously inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Latin_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Latin Music Hall of Fame">International Latin Music Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>On March 23, 2001, Casa Hernandez, the music store which served as Hernandez\'s booking agency and base of operations, was listed in the National Register of Historic Places, reference #01000244, as "Casa Amadeo, antigua Casa Hernandez".<sup id="cite_ref-nrhpinv_ny_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpinv_ny-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2017, Rafael Hern\xc3\xa1ndez was posthumously inducted to the Puerto Rico Veterans Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span
