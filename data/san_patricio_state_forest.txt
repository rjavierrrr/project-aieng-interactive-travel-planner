title="Puerto Rican moist forests">Puerto Rican moist forests</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>San Patricio State Forest</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i>Bosque Estatal de San Patricio</i>), also known as the <b>San Patricio Urban Forest</b> (Spanish: <i>Bosque Urbano de San Patricio</i>) is one of the 20 forests that make up the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Puerto_Rico_state_forests" title="List of Puerto Rico state forests">public forest system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. This is a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_forest" title="Secondary forest">secondary or second-growth forest</a> is located in the <a href="/wiki/Gobernador_Pi%C3%B1ero,_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Gobernador Pi\xc3\xb1ero, San Juan, Puerto Rico">Gobernador Pi\xc3\xb1ero</a> district of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>, between the neighborhoods of Villa Borinquen, Caparra Heights and Borinquen Towers complex. The urban forest has entrances on <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Highway_23" title="Puerto Rico Highway 23">Roosevelt Avenue</a> and Ensenada Street. The forest extends to almost 70 acres and it is the smallest protected area in the Puerto Rico state forest system. One of the most distinctive features of the forest is the <a href="/wiki/Mogote" title="Mogote">mogote</a> on its northern edge which can be observed from many parts of San Juan and <a href="/wiki/Guaynabo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guaynabo, Puerto Rico">Guaynabo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The forest is part of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Karst_(Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Karst (Puerto Rico)">Northern Karst</a>.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg/220px-BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="336" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg/330px-BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg/440px-BosqueSanPatricio_2020_simple.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1668" data-file-height="2550" /></a><figcaption>Map of the forest published by the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Natural_and_Environmental_Resources" title="Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources">DRNA</a>.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The site of the forest was first developed for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Pastoral farming">cattle grazing</a> and used to be a farm called <i>Finca San Patricio</i>. There used to be three mogotes on the site but two were destroyed to make way for the construction of the Villa Borinquen neighborhood and the Borinquen Towers complex. The third mogote still exists and it hosts a communications tower. The rest of the land was originally acquired by the US Army with the intention of developing residences for nearby <a href="/wiki/Fort_Buchanan,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Buchanan, Puerto Rico">Fort Buchanan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1962 the city of San Juan was experiencing a population boom and urban sprawl destroyed much of the original forest areas of the region. The area where the forest is located was not spared as if it was cleared to make way for more neighborhoods, and the only part of the forest to remain relatively untouched was that around the only remaining mogote. In 1960, the army left and abandoned the residences there and from 1974 to 1999 most of those residences were demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At the time the land was owned by the governmental Corporation for Housing and Urban Development (Spanish: <i>Corporaci\xc3\xb3n de Renovaci\xc3\xb3n Urbana y Vivienda</i>) or CRUV (today known as the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Housing" title="Puerto Rico Department of Housing">Puerto Rico Department of Housing</a>) and the land was intended to be developed to build housing of social interest but these were never built and the modern forest started to grow in the abandoned land. The area was considered one of the last green zones in San Juan and as such in 1998 the environmentalist group <i>Ciudadanos Pro Bosque San Patricio</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> along with scientists and local residents, pushed to have the land be proclaimed a forest preserve. On August 3, 2000, the forest is proclaimed through an executive order and granted to the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Department_of_Natural_and_Environmental_Resources" title="Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources">Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources</a> (DRNA) to manage and preserve the secondary forest and its ecology. The goal of this proclamation is to preserve the forest for the purpose of recreation and scientific research.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After being closed due to the destruction caused by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> in 2017, the forest has recently been revitalized and it re-opened in April of 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Geology">Geology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>There are three kinds of <a href="/wiki/Geological_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Geological unit">geological units</a> in the forest: <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fan</a> deposits composed of red-colored sandy and silty <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, watered <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcarenite" title="Calcarenite">calcarenite</a> and chalky limestone rock which is very common in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Karst_(Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Karst (Puerto Rico)">Northern Karst</a> of Puerto Rico, and the unit representative of the fossil-rich <a href="/wiki/Cibao_Marl" title="Cibao Marl">Cibao Formation</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, soft limestone rock, and sandy clay.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Ecology">Ecology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Flora">Flora</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flora"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The forest is home to about 70 species of trees, all of which are of <a href="/wiki/Secondary_forest" title="Secondary forest">secondary growth</a>. Some of the most common trees are flamboyant (<i><a href="/wiki/Delonix_regia" title="Delonix regia">Delonix regia</a></i>), pink trumpet tree or Puerto Rican oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Tabebuia_heterophylla" title="Tabebuia heterophylla">Tabebuia heterophyll</a></i>) and the invasive African tulip tree (<i><a href="/wiki/Spathodea" title="Spathodea">Spathodea campanulata</a></i>). Due to its secondary origin, most of the plant species in the forest are of exotic origin, although the species found in higher areas such as in the <a href="/wiki/Mogote" title="Mogote">mogote</a> are native to Puerto Rico. Some endangered or threatened native species have been introduced to the forest for the purpose of their conservation; some of these species are the elegant goetzea or <i>matabuey</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Goetzea_elegans" title="Goetzea elegans">Goetzea elegans</a></i>) and the Puerto Rican manac (<i><a href="/wiki/Calyptronoma_rivalis" title="Calyptronoma rivalis">Calyptronoma rivalis</a></i>), both of which have successfully adapted to the forest.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Fauna">Fauna</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The forest is good for <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">birdwatching</a><sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as it is home to at least 33 bird species, 9 of which are endemic to Puerto Rico: the Puerto Rican bullfinch (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_bullfinch" title="Puerto Rican bullfinch">Loxigilla&#160; portoricencis</a></i>), the Puerto Rican screech owl (<a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_owl" title="Puerto Rican owl"><i>Otus&#160; nudipe</i></a>), the Puerto Rican woodpecker (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_woodpecker" title="Puerto Rican woodpecker">Melanerpes&#160; portoricencis</a></i>), the Puerto Rican oriole (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_oriole" title="Puerto Rican oriole">Icterus portoricensis</a></i>), the Puerto Rican flycatcher (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_flycatcher" title="Puerto Rican flycatcher">Myiarchus antillarum</a></i>), the Puerto Rican spindalis (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_spindalis" title="Puerto Rican spindalis">Spindalis&#160; portoricencis</a></i>), the Adelaide\'s warbler (<i><a href="/wiki/Adelaide%27s_warbler" title="Adelaide&#39;s warbler">Setophaga adelaidae</a></i>), the Puerto Rican vireo (<i><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_vireo" title="Puerto Rican vireo">Vireo latimeri</a></i>) and the green mango hummingbird (<i><a href="/wiki/Green_mango" title="Green mango">Anthracothorax viridis</a></i>).\n</p><p>Three species of coqui occur in the forest: the common coqui (<i><a href="/wiki/Common_coqu%C3%AD" title="Common coqu\xc3\xad">Electherodactylus coqui</a></i>), the whistling coqui (<i><a href="/wiki/Whistling_coqui" class="mw-redirect" title="Whistling coqui">Electherodactylus cochranae</a></i>) and the red-eyed coqui or churi (<i><a href="/wiki/Red-eyed_coqui" class="mw-redirect" title="Red-eyed coqui">Eleutherodactylus antillensis</a></i>). Other amphibian species commonly found in the forest are the white-lipped frog (<i><a href="/wiki/Leptodactylus_albilabris" title="Leptodactylus albilabris">Leptodactylus albilabris</a></i>) and the cane toad (<i><a href="/wiki/Cane_toad" title="Cane toad">Rhinella marina</a></i>). Reptiles such as the Puerto Rican racer (<i><a href="/wiki/Borikenophis_portoricensis" title="Borikenophis portoricensis">Borikenophis portoricensis</a></i>), the Puerto Rican ground lizard (<i><a href="/wiki/Common_Puerto_Rican_ameiva" title="Common Puerto Rican ameiva">Pholidoscelis exsul</a></i>), the crested anole (<i><a href="/wiki/Anolis_cristatellus" title="Anolis cristatellus">Anolis cristatellus</a></i>) and the Puerto Rican anole (<i><a href="/wiki/Anolis_pulchellus" title="Anolis pulchellus">Anolis pulchellus</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_boa" title="Puerto Rican boa">Puerto Rican boa</a>, which is endangered and endemic to the island, can also be found in the forest.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Recreation">Recreation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Patricio_State_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recreation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The forest has infrastructure for visitors with an information center, parking, handicap access, bathrooms, gazebos for picnics, a <a href="/wiki/Plant_nursery" title="Plant nursery">plant nursery</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and various interpretative trails with information about the forest\'s wildlife. It is a good place for birdwatching<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and educational tours about the various scientific research projects that are conducted in the area. Biking is allowed in the forest.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The forest also hosts occasional educational and environmental events.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is open to visitors by appointment only (787-268-5353, 787-707-0730) from 7:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. on weekdays and from 8:00 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. on weekends.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is no admission fee.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2
