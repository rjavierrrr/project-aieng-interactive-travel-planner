10px}</style>\n<p>The <b>Mona ground iguana</b> (<i><b>Cyclura stejnegeri</b></i>) is a critically endangered species of <a href="/wiki/Rock_iguana" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock iguana">rock iguana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Mona Island">Mona Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. It is one of the island nation\'s few large land animals, and it is the largest endemic <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a> in the country, and one of the biggest rock iguanas within the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a>. It was previously considered a <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros_iguana" title="Rhinoceros iguana">rhinoceros iguana</a> (<i>Cyclura cornuta</i>).\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The Mona ground iguana belongs to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cyclura" title="Cyclura">Cyclura</a></i>. It was named by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Barbour" title="Thomas Barbour">Thomas Barbour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gladwyn_Kingsley_Noble" class="mw-redirect" title="Gladwyn Kingsley Noble">Gladwyn Kingsley Noble</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1937, Barbour considered it to be a subspecies of <i>Cyclura cornuta</i>, although he doubted it was particularly distinct. Research into <a href="/wiki/Scalation" class="mw-redirect" title="Scalation">scalation</a> patterns in 1977 found little difference between this taxon and the <a href="/wiki/Nominotypical_subspecies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominotypical subspecies">nominate subspecies</a> on Hispaniola, but markedly more differences in the subspecies <i>onchiopsis</i>, and thus found Barbour\'s 1937 taxonomic interpretation best approximated the obvious relatedness of these taxa.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its specific name, <i>stejnegeri</i> honors <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Hess_Stejneger" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonhard Hess Stejneger">Leonhard Hess Stejneger</a>, who, when writing his <i>Herpetology of Porto Rico</i> in 1902, suspected the rhinoceros iguanas of Mona Island might be slightly different in scale count than those in Haiti, although he was unsure.<sup id="cite_ref-Stegneger_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stegneger-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1993 Robert Powell suggested that all three subspecies of the rhinoceros iguana could be seen as distinct species, if the word "species" was redefined to mean any population which was separated from another population. Although it does not appear to be distinct from the nominotypical subspecies morphologically, in a 1999 article, Powell again recommended recognizing this population as a different species, based on what he had said in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some authorities have decided to follow Powell.<sup id="cite_ref-itis_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-itis-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Still, others consider it a regional variant of the parent species, <i>C. cornuta</i>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Description">Description</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg/300px-Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="400" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg/450px-Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg/600px-Mona_ground_iguana_no.1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a><figcaption></figcaption></figure>\n<p>The Mona ground iguana is a large-bodied, heavy-headed lizard with strong legs and a vertically flattened tail, which is capable of reaching 1.22&#160;m (4&#160;ft 0&#160;in) in length (from <a href="/wiki/Snout" title="Snout">snout</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Stegneger_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stegneger-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A crest of pointed, horned scales extends from the nape of the neck to the tip of the tail. The color is a uniform gray to olive drab, with slight brown or blue colorations. <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(animal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Juvenile (animal)">Juveniles</a> differ from adults in that they have gray transverse bands across their bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These bands last until they are sexually mature at about three years of age.\n</p><p>Males possess bony, prominent tubercles on their snouts resembling horns, adipose pads in the form of a helmet on the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/occipital" class="extiw" title="wikt:occipital">occipital</a> region of their heads, and large <a href="/wiki/Dewlap" title="Dewlap">dewlaps</a>. This subspecies, like other species of <i>Cyclura</i>, is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>; males are larger than females, and have more prominent <a href="/wiki/Dorsum_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorsum (biology)">dorsal</a> crests, "horns" and <a href="/wiki/Femoral_pore" title="Femoral pore">femoral pores</a> on their thighs, which are used to release <a href="/wiki/Pheromones" class="mw-redirect" title="Pheromones">pheromones</a>. Thomas Wiewandt, who spent an extended period on <a href="/wiki/Mona_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Mona Island">Mona Island</a> studying <i>C. stejnegeri</i>, suggested that the horns, along with lateral spines and prominent parietal bulges, function as protective armor against sharp rocks or as defensive tools to facilitate the escape of males from the grasp of one another.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiewandt_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiewandt-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-devosjoli_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devosjoli-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Martins_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martins-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Habitat">Habitat</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Mona ground iguanas are <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Diurnal animal">diurnal</a> and spend most of the day basking in the sun conserving <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. Mona ground iguanas are endemic to Mona Island; they are scattered through the entire <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a>, though the southwest part of the island is only used during the nesting season. They live a considerable portion of their lives underground, and are usually found in <a href="/wiki/Scree" title="Scree">talus</a> slopes, <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a> and sinkhole depressions. The average depth underground that they can be found is 1.5&#160;m (4&#160;ft 11&#160;in).\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Reproduction">Reproduction</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg/300px-Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="400" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg/450px-Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg/600px-Mona_ground_iguana_hatchling.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a><figcaption> Mona ground iguana hatchling</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Although Mona ground iguanas use the whole island as their habitat, only 1% of the <a href="/wiki/Territory_(animal)" title="Territory (animal)">territory</a>, located on the southwest coast, is suitable for nesting because it contains loose sand, and receives direct sunlight. The females bury their eggs in the sand, and the sunlight incubates the eggs. Males reach sexual maturity at a size of 28\xe2\x80\x9331&#160;cm (11\xe2\x80\x9312&#160;in) in length from snout to vent, usually in their third to fourth year, while females mature one year later at a size of 35\xe2\x80\x9340&#160;cm (14\xe2\x80\x9316&#160;in).\n</p><p>Nesting season begins in the second week of June. Usually, one female mates with more than one male in the two weeks the <a href="/wiki/Estrus_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Estrus cycle">mating season</a> lasts. <a href="/wiki/Copulation_(zoology)" title="Copulation (zoology)">Copulation</a> may last from 15&#160;<a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">sec</a> to 2&#160;<a href="/wiki/Minute" title="Minute">mins</a> and 15&#160;sec. One month later, nesting begins. Females will dig a tunnel 0.91&#160;m (3&#160;ft) long located one to two feet underground, where they deposit from five to 19 eggs, with 12 being the average. They will guard their nests for several days, but provide no parental care for the <a href="/wiki/Hatchling" title="Hatchling">hatchlings</a>, which hatch three months later. Hatchlings measure, on average, 32&#160;cm (13&#160;in) and weigh 73.7&#160;g (2.60&#160;oz) and grow at a rate of 5.23&#160;cm (2.06&#160;in) per year.<sup id="cite_ref-iverson_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iverson-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Diet">Diet</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Mona ground iguanas, like most <i>Cyclura </i> species, are primarily <a href="/wiki/Herbivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbivorous">herbivorous</a>, consuming leaves, flowers, berries, and fruits from different plant species.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrd_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrd-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A study in 2000 by Dr Allison Alberts of the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Zoo" title="San Diego Zoo">San Diego Zoo</a> revealed that seeds passing through the digestive tracts of <i>Cycluras</i> germinate more rapidly than those that do not.<sup id="cite_ref-Derr_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Derr-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alberts_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These seeds in the fruits consumed by <i>Cyclura</i> have an adaptive advantage, by sprouting before the end of very short rainy seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alberts_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Mona ground iguana is also an important means of distributing these seeds to new areas (particularly since females migrate to nesting sites) and, as the largest native <a href="/wiki/Herbivores" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, they are essential for maintaining the balance between climate and vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alberts_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Their diets are very rarely supplemented with insect larvae, crabs, slugs, dead birds, and fungi; individual animals do appear to be opportunistic carnivores.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wiewandt_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiewandt-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Fewer than a dozen animal species and 71 plant species are found in the Mona ground iguanas\' diets.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiewandt_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiewandt-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Mona ground iguanas eat the <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillar</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sphingidae" title="Sphingidae">sphingid moths</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These larvae feed on poisonous plants and are <a href="/wiki/Aposematism" title="Aposematism">aposematically</a> colored and avoided by other predators.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell2-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Endangered_status">Endangered status</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Endangered status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Population numbers are estimated at 1,500 with lower densities than similar iguana-inhabited islands in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. Immature iguanas are scarce and represent only 5&#8211;10% of the population, revealing that the population is aging and in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-knapp_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knapp-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Reasons_for_decline">Reasons for decline</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reasons for decline"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The biggest threat to <i>C. stejnegeri</i> are <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>; <a href="/wiki/Feral_pig" title="Feral pig">feral pigs</a> pose the most serious threat, as they root up iguana nests, and like most <i>Cyclura</i> species, the Mona ground iguana nests communally and at high density.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrd_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrd-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-knapp_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knapp-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Introduced <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a> and pigs are a major competitor for food and overbrowsing by goats also leads to loss of protective cover from birds of prey such as the <a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">osprey</a> and predation of juveniles by <a href="/wiki/Feral_cat" title="Feral cat">feral cats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrd_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrd-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-knapp_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knapp-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Recovery_efforts">Recovery efforts</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mona_ground_iguana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recovery efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Headstarting" title="Headstarting">headstarting</a> program was put into place by the Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources, with cooperation from the <a href="/wiki/IUCN" class="mw-redirect" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> Iguana Specialist Group, the <a href="/wiki/US_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="US Fish and Wildlife Service">US Fish and Wildlife Service</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Zoo" class="mw-redirect" title="Toledo Zoo">Toledo Zoo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a> in 1999, to aid in the recovery of the Mona ground iguana.<sup id="cite_ref-isg_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isg-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> From within the safety of this program, the iguanas are reared until they are large enough to survive in the wild, and predators, such as pigs and feral cats, are no longer a threat.<sup id="cite_ref-alberts_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The facility also carries out health screening prior to the release of specimens.<sup id="cite_ref-alberts_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alberts-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This health screening has been used to provide baselines of the normal physiologic values of the species, identifying potential future problems due to parasites, diseases, etc. which might threaten the population.<sup id="cite_ref-knapp_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knapp-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<style
