class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></td></tr><tr style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Francisco Manuel Oller y Cestero</b> (June 17, 1833 &#8211; May 17, 1917) was a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican</a> painter, the only <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a> painter to have played a role in the development of <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a>. One of the most distinguished transatlantic painters of his day, Oller helped transform painting in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Biography">Biography</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Early_years">Early years</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p><b>Francisco Manuel Oller y Cestero</b> was born in <a href="/wiki/Bayam%C3%B3n,_Puerto_Rico" title="Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico">Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico</a>, the third of four children of aristocratic and wealthy <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spanish</a> parents Cayetano Juan Oller y Fromesta and Mar\xc3\xada del Carmen Cestero D\xc3\xa1vila.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> When he was eleven he began to study art under the tutelage of Juan Cleto Noa, a painter who had an art academy in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>. There, Oller demonstrated that he had an enormous talent in art and in 1848, when Oller was fifteen years old, General <a href="/wiki/Juan_Prim" title="Juan Prim">Juan Prim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Governor of Puerto Rico</a>, offered Oller the opportunity to continue his studies in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. However, the offer was not accepted as Oller\'s mother felt that he was too young to travel abroad alone.\n</p><p>When Oller was eighteen, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, where he studied painting at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_San_Fernando" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy of San Fernando">Royal Academy of San Fernando</a>, under the tutelage of <a href="/wiki/Don_Federico_de_Madrazo_y_Kuntz" class="mw-redirect" title="Don Federico de Madrazo y Kuntz">Don Federico de Madrazo y Kuntz</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Prado_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Prado Museum">Prado Museum</a>. In 1858, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> where he studied under <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Couture" title="Thomas Couture">Thomas Couture</a>. Later he enrolled to study art in the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> under the instruction of <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Courbet" title="Gustave Courbet">Gustave Courbet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oller_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oller-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During his free time, Oller, who had a <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">baritone</a> type of singing voice, worked and participated in local <a href="/wiki/Italian_opera" title="Italian opera">Italian operas</a>. He frequently visited caf\xc3\xa9s where he met with fellow artists. He also became a friend of fellow <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Ricans</a> <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Emeterio_Betances" title="Ram\xc3\xb3n Emeterio Betances">Ram\xc3\xb3n Emeterio Betances</a> y Alac\xc3\xa1n and Salvador Carbonell del Toro, who were expatriates in France because of their political beliefs. In 1859, Oller exhibited some of his artistic works next to those of <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Bazille" title="Fr\xc3\xa9d\xc3\xa9ric Bazille">Bazille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Renoir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Monet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Sisley" title="Alfred Sisley">Sisley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oller_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oller-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>For a short time, <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul C\xc3\xa9zanne">Paul C\xc3\xa9zanne</a> was one of Oller\'s students, although their professional relationship deteriorated with time. By 1865, Oller was the first Puerto Rican and <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Impressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Impressionist">Impressionist</a> artist. In 1868, he founded The Free Academy of Art of Puerto Rico.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Realism_Period_(1840\xe2\x80\x931903)"><span id="Realism_Period_.281840.E2.80.931903.29"></span>Realism Period (1840\xe2\x80\x931903)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Realism Period (1840\xe2\x80\x931903)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><p>\nOller\xe2\x80\x99s paintings were a big contribution of history during this time. &#160;His paintings showed conflicts and daily lives of people outside of his home country as well as subjects internally.  The author Edward J. Sullivan describes in his book, <i>From San Juan to Paris and Back: Francisco Oller and Caribbean Art in the Era of Impressionism</i>:</p><blockquote><p>In the normal course of his day, the artist would have observed objects of quotidian use to the slaves and free persons of color with whom he regularly interacted. His world was not only the cultivated, Europeanized milieus of the Puerto Rican bourgeoisie, but also the realities of the relatively small city of San Juan, where he was born and where most of his career developed. Even during his many years of residence in Madrid and Paris, he would have carried with him the cultural baggage of his place of origin. His familiarity with the different strata of society on the Caribbean island of his birth was a constant reality as he intermingled with Realist and Impressionist artists and others who constituted his world on the other side of the Atlantic. This aspect of his personality cannot be easily separated from the other. Oller was a person of multiple cultural affinities, which allowed him to embrace what he saw abroad, but also to interiorize and reformulate those elements for purposes that conformed to his vision of tropical reality.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Upon his return to Puerto Rico from France in 1866 he found himself face-to-face with slavery and he would create a number of works including <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">El negro flageado</i></span> (The negro being flogged), <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">El castigo del negro enamorado</i></span> (the punishment of the negro in love), and others depicting slavery in Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Impression_Period_(1874\xe2\x80\x931893)"><span id="Impression_Period_.281874.E2.80.931893.29"></span>Impression Period (1874\xe2\x80\x931893)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impression Period (1874\xe2\x80\x931893)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Francisco-Oller.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Francisco-Oller.jpg/220px-Francisco-Oller.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="318" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Francisco-Oller.jpg/330px-Francisco-Oller.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Francisco-Oller.jpg/440px-Francisco-Oller.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3676" data-file-height="5319" /></a><figcaption>Francisco Oller portrait, c. 1892</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Oller spent nearly two decades in Europe working alongside the pioneers of Impressionism, and, through his travels, participated in a vibrant exchange of aesthetic ideas, forging his own brand of international modernism while engaging social issues unique to the Caribbean.  During his three trips to Paris, Oller affiliated himself with Paul C\xc3\xa9zanne, fellow Caribbean artist <a href="/wiki/Camille_Pissarro" title="Camille Pissarro">Camille Pissarro</a> (born in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a>), and other members of the Impressionist and Post-Impressionist movements.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Later_years">Later years</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Throughout his later life and after his death, Oller was compared to <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Campeche" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Campeche">Jose Campeche</a> as one of the most outstanding figures of the arts in his country. Campeche created an impressive body of portraits and religious subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1884, he founded an art school for young women<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegelman_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegelman-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which was later to be known as the Universidad Nacional. In 1871, Spain honored Oller by naming him a member of the Caballeros de la Orden de Carlos III (which translates to Knighthood of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Charles_III" title="Order of Charles III">Order of Carlos III</a>), and a year later he became the official painter of the Royal Court of <a href="/wiki/Amadeo_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Amadeo I">Amadeo I</a>. Oller developed an interest in bringing out the reality of Puerto Rico\'s landscape, its people, and culture through his works of art. Oller\'s paintings can be found in museums worldwide, including the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Orsay" title="Mus\xc3\xa9e d&#39;Orsay">Mus\xc3\xa9e d\'Orsay</a> in France.\n</p><p>Oller died on May 17, 1917, in San Juan, Puerto Rico. He was buried at the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_Magdalena_de_Pazzis_Cemetery" title="Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery">Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Styles_and_influences">Styles and influences</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Styles and influences"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><p>\nOller was a prolific painter with works ranging in topic from still-lifes and landscapes to historical events and portraiture.<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegelman_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegelman-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While he was involved with several painting movements, <a href="/wiki/Realism_(art_movement)" title="Realism (art movement)">Realism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)#Naturalism" title="Realism (arts)">Naturalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a>; he is best known for his Impressionist works.<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegelman_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegelman-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Oller\xe2\x80\x99s style of painting made him stand out among the many artists that took part in the depiction of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s lush tropical landscapes of both rural and urban landscapes and provided a realistic image of Puerto Rico compared to the European artists that flocked over to the Caribbean to paint lush, idealized landscapes of the environment.  Oller\xe2\x80\x99s main subject matter of focus was everything within Puerto Rico, whether it was still life paintings of plantains and bananas or historical battle scenes. Oller\'s painting style provided a historical context for his painting, providing a cultural view through his own lens. Oller was involved with many art movements from the realist art movement, the naturalist art movement, and the impressionist art movement of which he is best known for.  In his life, Oller spent nearly two decades in Europe working alongside the pioneers of these art movements, drawing influence from them in the evolution of his artwork. One characteristic that is quite apparent in his work that he drew influence from another artist is his sober, unidealized depictions of rural workers in Puerto Rico.</p><blockquote><p>The "plantation portraits" by Oller, as well as those by Mendes Belisario in Jamaica and dozens of others throughout the Caribbean and the southern United States (where the plantations mostly served the cotton industry), were part of a long tradition of depictions of agriculture and servitude. Rarely, if ever, depicting the negative associations of enslaved or indentured labor, they attempted to create nostalgic views of the "old homestead," or a faraway venue of nostalgia. While Oller\xe2\x80\x99s plantation pictures were executed in a somewhat different spirit, they nonetheless pertain to this genre that played a significant role throughout the development of visualization in a Caribbean context over a period of some 150 years.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote><p> Oller was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Barbizon_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Barbizon school">Barbizon school</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Millet" title="Jean-Fran\xc3\xa7ois Millet">Jean-Fran\xc3\xa7ois Millet</a> to paint in this way, allowing him to provide a realistic depiction of the life of Puerto Ricans in their historical context. In his early years, Francisco Oller was taught by European painters at the Academia, including French neoclassical painter <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Vermay" title="Jean Baptiste Vermay">Jean Baptiste Vermay</a>. His main source of influence in his early years comes from two painters, the Spaniard <a href="/wiki/Luis_Paret_y_Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Luis Paret y Alc\xc3\xa1zar">Luis Paret y Alc\xc3\xa1zar</a> and, most significantly, the Puerto Rican <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Campeche_y_Jord%C3%A1n" class="mw-redirect" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Campeche y Jord\xc3\xa1n">Jos\xc3\xa9 Campeche y Jord\xc3\xa1n</a>. The artwork of Jos\xc3\xa9 Campeche provided Oller a major point of reference for him in his young years as a developing artist. Francisco Oller was compared to Campeche as one of the most prominent artists in his country\xe2\x80\x99s history. Oller not only drew inspiration from the work of Campeche but copied works of art by him and even lived in the artist\xe2\x80\x99s former house.<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegelman_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegelman-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Selected_works">Selected works</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Selected works"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<ul><li><i>El pleito de la herencia</i> (1854\xe2\x80\x931856)</li>\n<li><i>Retrato de Manuel Sicard\xc3\xb3</i> (1866\xe2\x80\x931868)</li>\n<li><i>El Estudiante</i> (1874)</li>\n<li><i>Las lavanderas</i> (1887\xe2\x80\x931888)</li>\n<li><i>Battle of Trevi\xc3\xb1o</i> (1879)\n<ul><li>Oller painted Colonel Juan Contreras as the central figure during a battle that took place on July 8, 1875. The battle was part of the <a href="/wiki/Carlist_Wars" title="Carlist Wars">Carlist Wars</a>, a 19th-century war over the succession of the Spanish throne.  Oller painted this during his final visit to Europe, where he was appointed as the official painter of the Spanish Court of <a href="/wiki/King_Alfonso_XII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Alfonso XII">King Alfonso XII</a>.  The artist opposed the conventions of realism and precision of more traditional military paintings.  Instead, he uses Impressionistic style to capture the atmosphere and drama of the moment.  Dabs of color blend together to create an out-of-focus effect.  Oller also effectively used lines to draw attention to Colonel Contreras; the clouds, hills, and soldiers direct focus to the central figure.  The author Edward J. Sullivan wrote:</li></ul></li></ul>\n<blockquote><p>The Battle of Trevi\xc3\xb1o denotes a significant moment for Oller within the world of Spanish art. The painting represents an official theme of recent national history, through which the artist places himself in the tradition of military images that was of great importance in later nineteenth-century Iberian painting. The genre of battle painting is one of the least avant-garde artistic forms. Oller, an artist often associated with the "new" in art, is here going against that grain and inserting himself directly within a mode most often practiced by artists of a decidedly conservative personality. Nevertheless, he used this form as a way to experiment with the possibilities of a spontaneity and pictorial freedom within the framework of traditional subject matter.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<ul><li><i>La Escuela del Maestro Rafael Cordero</i> (1890\xe2\x80\x931892)\n<ul><li>The subject of this painting, <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Cordero_(educator)" title="Rafael Cordero (educator)">Rafael Cordero</a>, was the founder of the first school in Puerto Rico for both the formerly enslaved and children of enslaved persons. By the late 1820s, the school admitted male children of all races and social strata.  Cordero was both the sole instructor and manual laborer, making cigars and cigarettes in his workshop, where class took place. We can see his tobacco work table in the bottom right-hand corner. The children are of different socioeconomic classes and skin tones; the artist portrays the importance and accessibility of education to all races. The two children in the foreground, one holding an egg and the other holding bread, suggests the intermingling of race.  As an abolitionist himself, Oller portrays the acceptance of race in the developing country during this time. Cordero\'s expression is exasperated and exhausted; despite this, he persists to provide education to the children.  The setting is very conservative with religious symbolism on the walls. The walls are plain and cracked. Oller portrayed his subject as glamorous, as an "unsung hero".</li></ul></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/El_Velorio" title="El Velorio">El Velorio</a></i> ("The Wake") (1893)<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegelman_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegelman-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arte_TV_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arte_TV-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n<ul><li>This 8-by-13-foot painting was one of the few that had survived while many of his works were lost or damaged beyond repair. &#160;<i>El Velorio</i> was not seen as the most glamorous but was shown in the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Salon" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Salon">Paris Salon</a> in 1895.  Oller uses small, visible brush strokes and an emphasis on an accurate depiction of light and mastery of color in its changing qualities in his paintings. The painting depicts the wake or, in Puerto Rican Spanish, "baquin\xc3\xa9" of a dead child laid on a table covered with flowers. Participants totally ignore the child and instead celebrate with food, drink, games, songs, dancing and prayer.  The reason for this is that they believe it is a time for celebration for the child has become an angel and should be properly sent off. All the while the parents are mourning over the loss of their child and some are consoling the mother. &#160;Oller\xe2\x80\x99s painting is considered a Puerto Rican <a href="/wiki/National_treasure" title="National treasure">national treasure</a> and is not allowed to leave the Museum of History, Anthropology, and Art at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico,_R%C3%ADo_Piedras_Campus" title="University of Puerto Rico, R\xc3\xado Piedras Campus">University of Puerto Rico\'s R\xc3\xado Piedras campus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul></li>\n<li><i>Bodeg\xc3\xb3n con pi\xc3\xb1as</i></li>\n<li><i>El Cesante</i></li>\n<li><i>Hacienda La Fortuna de Ponce</i> (1885)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Parque_de_la_Ceiba" title="Parque de la Ceiba">La Ceiba de Ponce</a></i> (1888)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legacy_and_remembrance">Legacy and remembrance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy and remembrance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Oller was responsible for bringing Realism and Impressionism to San Juan, Puerto Rico. Combining them with the artistic traditions of San Juan, he revolutionized the school of painting in Puerto Rico and throughout the Caribbean region.  Oller also helped bring outside world events by drawing them and displaying them publicly.  Many Puerto Rican artists have followed in Oller\'s footsteps, including <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Frade" title="Ram\xc3\xb3n Frade">Ram\xc3\xb3n Frad\xc3\xa9</a> (1875-1954) and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Pou" title="Miguel Pou">Miguel Pou</a> (1880-1968).<sup id="cite_ref-Arte_TV_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arte_TV-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Cata%C3%B1o,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cata\xc3\xb1o, Puerto Rico">Cata\xc3\xb1o</a> in Puerto Rico, named a high school after him and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">City of New York</a> renamed P.S.61 in The <a href="/wiki/Bronx" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronx">Bronx</a> P.S. Francisco Oller.  There is also a Francisco Oller Library in the <a href="/wiki/Escuela_de_Artes_Pl%C3%A1sticas_y_Dise%C3%B1o_de_Puerto_Rico" title="Escuela de Artes Pl\xc3\xa1sticas y Dise\xc3\xb1o de Puerto Rico">Escuela de Artes Pl\xc3\xa1sticas</a> (School of Plastic Arts) in San Juan.  The Francisco Oller Museum where many artists, such as <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Batista" title="Tom\xc3\xa1s Batista">Tom\xc3\xa1s Batista</a>, exhibit their work is located in the city of <a href="/wiki/Bayam%C3%B3n,_Puerto_Rico" title="Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico">Bayam\xc3\xb3n</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a> there is a Francisco Oller and <a href="/wiki/Diego_Rivera" title="Diego Rivera">Diego Rivera</a> Museum of Art where <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Rivera-Ortiz" title="Manuel Rivera-Ortiz">Manuel Rivera-Ortiz</a> and other artists have exhibited their work.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Gallery">Gallery</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Oller&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gallery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 1458px;">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 235px">\n\t\t\t<div class="thumb" style="width: 230px; height: 230px;"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Hacienda_La_Fortuna.jpg" class="mw-file-description" title="Hacienda La Fortuna, 1885.
