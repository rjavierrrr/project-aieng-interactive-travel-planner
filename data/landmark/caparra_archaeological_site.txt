0;">Designated&#160;RNSZH</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">February 3, 2000</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Caparra</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a> in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Guaynabo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guaynabo, Puerto Rico">Guaynabo, Puerto Rico</a>.  It was declared a U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1994.  The site contains the remains of the first Spanish capital of the island, settled in 1508 and officially abandoned in 1521.  It represents the oldest known European settlement on United States territory.<sup id="cite_ref-nhlsum_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The site is on the grounds of the <b>Museum of the Conquest and Colonization of Puerto Rico</b> (Museo de la Conquista y Colonizaci\xc3\xb3n), which features artifacts from the site and other archaeological sites in Puerto Rico.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caparra_Archaeological_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1508, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a> founded the original Spanish settlement in Puerto Rico at Caparra, named after the abandoned ancient Roman village of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1parra" title="C\xc3\xa1parra">C\xc3\xa1parra</a> in the province of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1ceres_(province)" class="mw-redirect" title="C\xc3\xa1ceres (province)">C\xc3\xa1ceres</a>, Spain, the birthplace of then-governor of Spain\'s Caribbean territories <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando">Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Today it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Pueblo_Viejo,_Guaynabo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Pueblo Viejo, Guaynabo, Puerto Rico">Pueblo Viejo</a> barrio of <a href="/wiki/Guaynabo" class="mw-redirect" title="Guaynabo">Guaynabo</a>, just to the west of the present <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> metropolitan area.<sup id="cite_ref-OfficeSanger1900_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OfficeSanger1900-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n</p><p>The air was not wholesome and the <a href="/wiki/Mendicant_orders" title="Mendicant orders">mendicant friars</a> insisted on moving the settlement closer to the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">bay</a> and to the sea. They complained that the infants were dying. Their preferred area was that of the <a href="/wiki/Isleta_de_San_Juan" class="mw-redirect" title="Isleta de San Juan">Islet of Puerto Rico</a> ("rich port" or "good port"), because of its similar geographical features to the island of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It was not until the end of Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n\'s tenure as governor that they had their wish. By 1521, the move was complete and it was known as "Villa de Puerto Rico." With time the name of the island, <i>San Juan Bautista de Puerto Rico</i>, traded places with what is now the capital of Puerto Rico: <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>According to Floyd, "Ponce built the only stone house in the village, which for years functioned additionally as the <a href="/wiki/Casa_de_Contrataci%C3%B3n" title="Casa de Contrataci\xc3\xb3n">Casa de Contrataci\xc3\xb3n</a>, the archive, and the arsenal."  It became his permanent home, where he was joined by his family in 1509.<sup id="cite_ref-Floyd_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Archaeological_history">Archaeological history</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caparra_Archaeological_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeological history"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The Caparra Site was first identified as important during a survey in 1936, as part of a program to develop tourist facilities on the island.  Preliminary excavations spearheaded by Puerto Rico\'s fifth official historian, <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_de_Hostos" title="Adolfo de Hostos">Adolfo de Hostos</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in 1936 and 1937, identified a large <i><a href="/wiki/Tabby_concrete" title="Tabby concrete">tapia</a></i> structure, bisected by a two-lane highway, that matched de Le\xc3\xb3n\'s description of his own residence, the only non-wooden structure in the settlement.  Further excavation identified the main plaza and the sites of other buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-nhl_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The property was acquired by the Puerto Rican government in 1948, which relocated the northern section of the house ruins in order to widen the road.  The roadway was again widened in 1963, destroying the southern portion of the structure excavated in 1936.  The museum was established in 1958; the site continues to be examined by archaeologists.<sup id="cite_ref-nhl_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="In_folklore">In folklore</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caparra_Archaeological_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In folklore"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>A story from 1530 says that two Spanish men, Diego Ramos de Orozco and Diego Guilarte de Salazar, were living in Caparra and searching for gold in Puerto Rico\'s rivers, for Spain. They each had at their disposal 40 <a href="/wiki/Encomienda" title="Encomienda">Ta\xc3\xadno slaves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dLOC_Home_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dLOC_Home-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Good friends, they elaborated a plan to travel to a hard to reach, secret place in order to excavate and find gold for themselves and their own fortunes.<sup id="cite_ref-P.R._Legends_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P.R._Legends-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dLOC_Home_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dLOC_Home-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Immediately upon finding a large piece of gold that they had originally agreed to share, their friendship was tested. In conniving to keep the gold, Orozco used fake dice, but then suffered a bad fall. Days later, when his friend Guilarte finally returned with help, Orozco lay dying, and confessed to having used fake dice and asked his friend for forgiveness. <i>Sierra de Orozco</i>, a mountain located in the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Cordillera Central (Puerto Rico)">Cordillera Central</a>, is named after Orozco.<sup id="cite_ref-P.R._Legends_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P.R._Legends-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dLOC_Home_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dLOC_Home-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="See_also">See
