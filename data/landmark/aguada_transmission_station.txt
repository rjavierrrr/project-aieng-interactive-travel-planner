class="mw-redirect" title="US Navy">US Navy</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Naval Radio Transmitter Facility Aguada</b> is a tall <a href="/wiki/Guyed_mast" title="Guyed mast">guyed radio mast</a> erected by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>. It is used as a facility of the US Navy for ashore and U.S. and <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> ships, planes, and submarines operating at sea in areas of broadcast coverage near <a href="/wiki/Aguada,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguada, Puerto Rico">Aguada, Puerto Rico</a> at <span class="geo-inline"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1156832818"><span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="https://geohack.toolforge.org/geohack.php?pagename=Naval_Radio_Transmitter_Facility_Aguada&amp;params=18_23_55_N_67_10_38_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">18\xc2\xb023\xe2\x80\xb255\xe2\x80\xb3N</span> <span class="longitude">67\xc2\xb010\xe2\x80\xb238\xe2\x80\xb3W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">18.39861\xc2\xb0N 67.17722\xc2\xb0W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">18.39861; -67.17722</span></span></span></a></span></span> by using radio waves in the <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">very low frequency</a> range.\n</p><p>The Aguada transmission station, originally part of "The Naval Radio Transmitter Facility Aguada", works on <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">very low frequency</a> (VLF) 40.75&#160;kHz with the callsign NAU. The Naval Radio Transmitter Facility Aguada, apparently consisted of three locations with guyed towers: the primary Aguada transmission station in a 330-acre site; the Naval Radio Receiver at a 242-acre site at <a href="/wiki/Campamento_Santiago" title="Campamento Santiago">Campamento Santiago</a> in <a href="/wiki/Salinas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Salinas, Puerto Rico">Salinas, Puerto Rico</a>; and the Naval Radio Transmitter at a 408-acre site in <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Only the tallest with a height of 367.3&#160;metres (1205&#160;ft) is still standing and in use., while the other two were dismantled. It is the tallest structure in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> region.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Communication_with_submarines">Communication with submarines</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naval_Radio_Transmitter_Facility_Aguada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Communication with submarines"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Communication_with_submarines" title="Communication with submarines">Communication with submarines</a></div>\n<p>This transmission station emits signals below 50&#160;kHz, being capable of penetrating ocean depths to approximately 200&#160;metres. The <a href="/wiki/US_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="US Navy">United States Navy</a> communicates with <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a> on these frequencies.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the low frequency, a <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">VLF</a> broadcast antenna needs to be quite large. In fact, broadcasting sites are usually a few square kilometres. This prevents such antennas being installed on submarines. Submarines carry only a VLF reception aerial and do not respond on such low frequencies, so a ground-to-submarine VLF broadcast is always a one-way broadcast, originating on the ground and received aboard the boat. If two-way communication is needed, the boat must ascend to periscope depth (just below the surface) and raise a telescopic mast antenna to communicate on higher frequencies (such as <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">HF</a>, <a href="/wiki/UHF" class="mw-redirect" title="UHF">UHF</a>, or <a href="/wiki/VHF" class="mw-redirect" title="VHF">VHF</a>).\n</p><p>Because of the narrow <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> of this band, VLF radio signals cannot carry <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">audio</a> (voice), and can transmit text messages only at a slow data rate. VLF data transmission rates are around 300&#160;bit/s &#8211; or about 35&#160;eight-bit <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> characters per second (or the equivalent of a sentence every two seconds) &#8211; a total of 450&#160;words per minute. Simply shifting to 7&#160;bit&#160;ASCII increases the number of transmitted characters per time unit by 14%. An additional shift to a 6&#160;bit or a 5&#160;bit code (such as the <a href="/wiki/ITA2" class="mw-redirect" title="ITA2">baudot code</a>) would result in speeds of more than 600 and 700&#160;words per minute.\n</p><p>In situations where <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> is denied to U.S. forces, VLF may be used to help provide position navigation and timing. The VLF stations, such as this in Puerto Rico, are in fixed locations. As part of a backup system, the VLF signals may be used to triangulate a user\'s position on the globe, in much the same way a GPS receiver is used.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a
