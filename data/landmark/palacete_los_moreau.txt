style="font-weight:bold; border: 0;">Designated&#160;RNSZH</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">May 4, 1994</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Palacete Los Moreau</b> is a house museum located in <a href="/wiki/Moca,_Puerto_Rico" title="Moca, Puerto Rico">Moca, Puerto Rico</a>. Historically known as the <b>Labadie Mansion</b>, the house inspired writer <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Laguerre" title="Enrique Laguerre">Enrique Laguerre</a> to write <i>La Llamarada</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moca_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moca-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The property was restored as a museum and renamed the <b>Palacete Los Moreau</b> in honor of Laguerre\'s novel. It\'s listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> as <b>Hacienda Iruena Manor House</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-nris_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and on the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Register_of_Historic_Sites_and_Zones" title="Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones">Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones</a> as the <b>Labadie House</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palacete_Los_Moreau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Built in 1893, it is the only building still standing of an agricultural conglomerate where both coffee and sugar were planted and processed.<sup id="cite_ref-nrhp_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhp-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  It belonged to the French family Pellot.\n</p><p>The hacienda was sold to Juan Labadi\xc3\xa9 in 1860. At the time of acquisition, the property had an area of 1,300 acres, of which three quarters were planted with coffee. The other quarter was divided between cane, timber and cattle.\n</p><p>Juan Labadi\xc3\xa9 lived on the plantation until his death in 1893. His widow, Cornelia Pellot decided to demolish the old house of wood to build a new one made out of concrete.\n</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a> Puerto Rico became a commonwealth of the United States in 1898, the estate became solely a sugar plantation belonging to <a href="/wiki/Central_Coloso" title="Central Coloso">Central Coloso</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Architecture">Architecture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palacete_Los_Moreau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Plans for the construction of the house started in 1893. The house was designed by Paul Servajean,<sup id="cite_ref-nris_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  the administrator of the sugar mill "Central Coloso" in Aguada.  Serva conceived the design as a Caribbean adaptation of a <a href="/wiki/Chateau" class="mw-redirect" title="Chateau">Chateau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teauesque" title="Ch\xc3\xa2teauesque">Ch\xc3\xa2teauesque</a> <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The main element of the house is the front porch that is flanked by two towers. One of the towers originally housed the library of the house.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="La_Llamarada">La Llamarada</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palacete_Los_Moreau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: La Llamarada"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The house, under the name \xe2\x80\x9cHacienda Palmares de la Familia Moreau\xe2\x80\x9d, became immortalized in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_literature" title="Puerto Rican literature">Puerto Rican literature</a> by "<i>La Llamarada</i>", a novel about the Depression-era sugar cane industry written by Puerto Rican author <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Laguerre" title="Enrique Laguerre">Enrique Laguerre</a>. He describes the house as it existed during the early 20th century, and the Moreau family are largely based upon the Labadies.<sup id="cite_ref-nrhp_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrhp-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Laguerre recognized how the novel transformed the house into a legend, "it\'s like when a great man dies, and literature replaced history and the legend begins."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1993 the municipality of Moca acquired the property and restored the house calling it "El Palacete Los Moreau" in honor of the novel.<sup id="cite_ref-Moca_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moca-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The estate is open free of charge to the public. In accordance with his wishes, Laguerre\'s body was cremated, and his ashes are interred in a small mausoleum on the grounds of the estate.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2
