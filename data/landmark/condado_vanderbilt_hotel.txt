style="border: 0;"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://npgallery.nps.gov/AssetDetail/NRIS/08001110">08001110</a><sup id="cite_ref-nris_6-0" class="reference"><a 0;">Added to NRHP</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">25 November 2008</td></tr><tr style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Condado Vanderbilt Hotel</b> is a historic <a href="/wiki/Luxury_hotel" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxury hotel">luxury hotel</a> built in 1919 and located on Ashford Avenue in the district of <a href="/wiki/Condado_(Santurce)" title="Condado (Santurce)">Condado</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>,  capital city of the U.S. territory of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. It was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The hotel was designed by the architectural firm <a href="/wiki/Warren_and_Wetmore" title="Warren and Wetmore">Warren and Wetmore</a>, who also designed <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a>. It was built by the <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_family" title="Vanderbilt family">Vanderbilt family</a> and it marked the beginning of high end tourism in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Que_Pasa_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Que_Pasa-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Early_years">Early years</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Construction of the <b>Condado Vanderbilt Hotel</b> was begun in 1917 by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_Vanderbilt" title="Frederick William Vanderbilt">Frederick William Vanderbilt</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Vanderbilt" title="William Henry Vanderbilt">William Henry Vanderbilt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reopening1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reopening1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He selected the prominent architectural firm of <a href="/wiki/Warren_and_Wetmore" title="Warren and Wetmore">Warren and Wetmore</a>, which had designed New York City\'s <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a>, as well as the Biltmore, Commodore, and Ambassador Hotels. On 16 October 1919, the hotel, having cost a million dollars to construct, was inaugurated.<sup id="cite_ref-CVH_Review_1919_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CVH_Review_1919-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LAHT_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAHT-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was suggested that the early Spanish Revival style architecture be used by Fredrick Vanderbilt.<sup id="cite_ref-OurStory_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OurStory-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Capitalizing on the property\'s setting adjacent to the Atlantic Ocean, the designers created a  Beaux Arts-style structure with white walls, red tiles, French windows, lofty ceilings and other design details typical of the style at the time. The original roofing featured antique Spanish tiles sourced from Puerto Rico\'s older Spanish buildings. The floors and the public areas including the main staircase were decorated with marble and mosaics and the area between the ocean and the hotel building was developed to help the building withstand damage from the Atlantic coast. The hotel also featured a <a href="/wiki/Flagstone" title="Flagstone">flagstone</a> paved terrace.<sup id="cite_ref-CVH_Review_1919_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CVH_Review_1919-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pandanus" title="Pandanus">Giant pandanus</a>, several kinds of <a href="/wiki/Bougainvillea" title="Bougainvillea">bougainvillea</a>, fruit trees and <a href="/wiki/Coconut_palms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coconut palms">coconut palms</a> could also be found in the hotel\'s gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-CVH_Review_1919_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CVH_Review_1919-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The Condado Vanderbilt was the first luxury hotel to open in Puerto Rico and was also the first hotel in Puerto Rico to have a casino after <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a> was legalized in 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-First_luxury_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-First_luxury-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Among the famous guests to have stayed at the Condado Vanderbilt are the former <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">Presidents of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a> and Roosevelt\'s wife <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Reopening1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reopening1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the American aviator <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> composer <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Moner%C3%B3" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Luis Moner\xc3\xb3">Jos\xc3\xa9 Luis Moner\xc3\xb3</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">Carlos Gardel</a>, Hollywood actor <a href="/wiki/Errol_Flynn" title="Errol Flynn">Errol Flynn</a>, comedian <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">Bob Hope</a>, and pianist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rubinstein" title="Arthur Rubinstein">Arthur Rubinstein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OurStory_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OurStory-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Activities that were available included American golf, tennis and motoring among other things. These were advertised in various American newspapers during the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Expansion">Expansion</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Manuel Gonzalez bought the hotel from the Vanderbilts during <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Depression" class="mw-redirect" title="The Great Depression">The Great Depression</a> in 1930,<sup id="cite_ref-pr_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pr-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and changed its name to the <b>Condado Hotel</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-LAHT_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAHT-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the 1940s, the hotel\'s East Wing was built, a five-story L-shaped addition, containing additional guest rooms and public areas.<sup id="cite_ref-pr_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pr-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the 1950s, after a series of changes in ownership, the name was changed to the <b>Condado Beach Hotel</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-OpeningToday_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpeningToday-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1962, another addition was built on the other side of the hotel, the West Wing, a nine-story concrete structure containing 156 air-conditioned guest rooms, all facing the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-pr_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pr-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Difficult_Years">Difficult Years</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Difficult Years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In the early 1970s, with the hotel threatened with demolition, governor <a href="/wiki/Luis_A._Ferr%C3%A9" title="Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9">Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9</a> issued an executive order, declaring the structure a cultural heritage site.<sup id="cite_ref-OurStory_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OurStory-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1973, the Condado Beach Hotel was united with the adjacent <a href="/wiki/La_Concha_Renaissance_San_Juan_Resort" title="La Concha Renaissance San Juan Resort">La Concha Hotel</a> into one resort, known as the <b>Hyatt Puerto Rico</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The 1940s East Wing of the Condado Beach Hotel was demolished in 1975 and a huge convention wing was built in its place, joining the Condado Beach and La Concha Hotels into one physical complex. In 1976,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with construction complete, management of the complex was taken over by Hilton International<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and it was renamed the <b>Condado Beach La Concha Convention Center</b>.  Management later transferred to <a href="/wiki/Carnival_Cruise_Line" title="Carnival Cruise Line">Carnival Cruise Line</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which renamed the resort <b>The Condado Beach Trio</b>. The La Concha Hotel wing closed in 1995 and the state-owned hotel was known in its final years as <b>The Condado Beach Hotel &amp; Casino</b>, before the entire complex closed on June 30, 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as it was losing $7 million a year.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The properties sat vacant for many years, and the La Concha and Condado Beach Hotels were finally severed in 2004. The convention center that joined them was demolished to build a public park.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebirth_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebirth-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Restoration">Restoration</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Restoration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1997, the administration of <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Rossell%C3%B3" title="Pedro Rossell\xc3\xb3">Pedro Rossell\xc3\xb3</a> proposed that the Condado area be redeveloped. Brian McLaughlin suggested renovating and expanding the hotel, but then-San Juan mayor <a href="/wiki/Sila_M._Calderon" class="mw-redirect" title="Sila M. Calderon">Sila M. Calderon</a> challenged the proposal in court. This led to the hotel being abandoned until 2002 when she finally agreed.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebirth_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebirth-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Having been abandoned for seven years, the hotel\'s renovation was finally undertaken at a cost of US$270 million in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-OpeningToday_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpeningToday-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The goal was to transform it into a five-star hotel with resemblance to the original 1919 structure. The 1962 West Wing was demolished in 2003, in preparation for this work.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The remaining original 1919 central hotel structure was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>On 16 October 2012, after ten years and 53,441 hours of renovations, two years longer than originally planned, a part of the renovated hotel comprising several new banquet halls, bars and restaurants was finally opened for business.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On December 1, 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the newly built 11-story twin towers on either side of the central structure were opened.<sup id="cite_ref-OpeningToday_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpeningToday-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The renovated rooms are 17 feet wide with high ceilings.<sup id="cite_ref-First_luxury_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-First_luxury-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The bathrooms have double sinks and both a shower and tub. The original staircases, however, remain in place. The remodeling was handled by a team led by Hugh Andrews and Jorge Rossello.<sup id="cite_ref-LAHT_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAHT-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Among the newly built restaurants, "1919", "Tacos &amp; Tequila by Patron", "Veritas", "Marabar" and "Avo Lounge"; "1919" has been credited as "Puerto Rico\'s finest restaurant" by Turismo, the Puerto Rico Tourism Company.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="In_Popular_culture">In Popular culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condado_Vanderbilt_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The hotel was one of the main filming locations of the 1976 teen comedy musical, <a href="/wiki/La_Pandilla_en_Apuros" title="La Pandilla en Apuros">La Pandilla en Apuros</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div
