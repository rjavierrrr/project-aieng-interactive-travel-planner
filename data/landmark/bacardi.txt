<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1177148991">.mw-parser-output .IPA-label-small{font-size:85%}.mw-parser-output .references .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">Spanish:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="es-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">&#91;baka\xc9\xbe\xcb\x88\xc3\xb0i&#93;</a></span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1177148991"><span title="Distilled beverage">spirits</a> company in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Originally known for its Bacard\xc3\xad brand of white <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, it now has a portfolio of more than 200 brands and labels.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_200+_brands_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_200+_brands-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Founded in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> in 1862 by <a href="/wiki/Facundo_Bacardi" title="Facundo Bacardi">Facundo Bacard\xc3\xad Mass\xc3\xb3</a>, Bacardi Limited has been family-owned for seven generations, and employs more than 8,000 people with sales in approximately 170 countries. Bacardi Limited is the group of companies as a whole and includes Bacardi International Limited.<sup id="cite_ref-BL_companies_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BL_companies-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Bacardi-logo-600x348.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bacardi-logo-600x348.png/220px-Bacardi-logo-600x348.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bacardi-logo-600x348.png/330px-Bacardi-logo-600x348.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bacardi-logo-600x348.png/440px-Bacardi-logo-600x348.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="348" /></a><figcaption>Bacardi logo</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Bacardi Limited is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Bermuda" title="Hamilton, Bermuda">Hamilton, Bermuda</a>, and has a board of directors led by the original founder\'s great-great-grandson, Facundo L. Bacard\xc3\xad, the chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacardi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Early_history">Early history</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacardi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Facundo_Bacardi" title="Facundo Bacardi">Facundo Bacard\xc3\xad Mass\xc3\xb3</a>, a Spanish <a href="/wiki/Winemaker" title="Winemaker">wine merchant</a>, was born in <a href="/wiki/Sitges" title="Sitges">Sitges</a>, Catalonia, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, on October 16, 1814, and immigrated to <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Santiago de Cuba">Santiago</a>, Cuba, in 1830. At the time, rum was cheaply made and not considered a refined drink, and rarely sold in upmarket taverns or purchased by the growing emerging middle class on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Facundo began attempting to "tame" rum by isolating a proprietary strain of yeast harvested from local sugar cane still used in Bacard\xc3\xad production today. This yeast gives Bacard\xc3\xad rum its flavour profile. After experimenting with several techniques for close to ten years, Facundo pioneered charcoal rum filtration, which removed impurities from his rum. Facundo then created two separate distillates that he could blend together, balancing a variety of flavors: <a href="/wiki/Aguardiente" title="Aguardiente">Aguardiente</a> (a robust, flavorful distillate) and Redestillado (a refined, delicate distillate). Once Facundo achieved the perfect balance of flavors by marrying the two distillates together, he purposefully aged the rum in white oak barrels to develop subtle flavors and characteristics while mellowing out those that were unwanted. The final product was the first clear, light-bodied and mixable "white" rum in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Moving from the experimental stage to a more commercial endeavour as local sales began to grow, Facundo and his brother Jos\xc3\xa9 purchased a <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Santiago de Cuba">Santiago de Cuba</a> distillery on October 16, 1862, which housed a <a href="/wiki/Still" title="Still">still</a> made of copper and <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>. In the rafters of this building lived <a href="/wiki/Leaf-nosed_bat" title="Leaf-nosed bat">fruit bats</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the inspiration for the Bacardi bat logo.<sup id="cite_ref-Bacardi_History_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bacardi_History-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was the idea of Do\xc3\xb1a Amalia, Facundo\'s wife, to adopt the bat to the rum bottle when she recognized its symbolism of family unity, good health, and good fortune to her husband\'s homeland of Spain. This logo was pragmatic considering the high illiteracy rate in the 19th century, enabling customers to easily identify the product.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1880s and 1890s were turbulent times for Cuba and the company. <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Bacard%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="Emilio Bacard\xc3\xad">Emilio Bacard\xc3\xad</a>, Don Facundo\'s eldest son, known for his forward thinking in both his professional and personal life and a passionate advocate for Cuban Independence was imprisoned twice for having fought in the rebel army against Spain in the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_War_of_Independence" title="Cuban War of Independence">Cuban War of Independence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gjelten_2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gjelten_2008-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2023)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Emilio\'s brothers, Facundo and Jos\xc3\xa9, and their brother-in-law Enrique \'Henri\' Schueg, remained in Cuba with the difficult task of sustaining the company during a period of war. With Don Facundo\'s passing in 1886, Do\xc3\xb1a Amalia sought refuge by exile in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a>. At the end of the Cuban War of Independence during the US occupation of Cuba, "The Original <a href="/wiki/Cuba_Libre" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuba Libre">Cuba Libre</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Daiquiri" title="Daiquiri">Daiquiri</a> cocktails were both created, with the then Cuban based Bacard\xc3\xad rum.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1899, Emilio Bacard\xc3\xad became the first democratically elected mayor of Santiago, appointed US General <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Wood" title="Leonard Wood">Leonard Wood</a>.\n</p><p>During his time in public office, Emilio established schools and hospitals, completed municipal projects such as the famous Padre Pico Street and the Bacardi Dam, financed the creation of parks, and decorated the city of Santiago with monuments and sculptures.<sup id="cite_ref-Gjelten_2008_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gjelten_2008-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2023)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1912, Emilio and his wife travelled to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, where he purchased a <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a> (still on display) for the future <a href="/wiki/Museo_Municipal_Emilio_Bacard%C3%AD_Moreau" title="Museo Municipal Emilio Bacard\xc3\xad Moreau">Emilio Bacardi Moreau Municipal Museum</a> in Santiago de Cuba.<sup id="cite_ref-Coulombe_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coulombe-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2023)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In Santiago, his brother Facundo M. Bacard\xc3\xad continued to manage the company along with Schueg, who began the company\'s international expansion by opening bottling plants in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> (1910) and New York City (1916).<sup id="cite_ref-Bacardi_History_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bacardi_History-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The New York plant was soon shut down due to <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a>, yet during this time Cuba became a hotspot for US tourists, kicking off a period of rapid growth for the Bacardi company and the onset of cocktail culture in America.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1922 the family completed the expansion and renovation of the original distillery in Santiago, increasing the sites rum production capacity. In 1930 Schueg oversaw the construction and opening of <a href="/wiki/Bacardi_Building_(Havana)" title="Bacardi Building (Havana)">Edificio Bacard\xc3\xad</a> in Havana, regarded as one of the finest <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> buildings in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, as the third generation of the Bacard\xc3\xad family entered the business. In 1927, Bacardi ventured outside the realm of spirits for the first time, with the introduction of an authentic Cuban Malt beer: <a href="/wiki/Hatuey_(beer)" title="Hatuey (beer)">Hatuey beer</a>.\n</p><p>Bacardi\'s success in transitioning into an international brand and company was due mostly to Schueg, who branded Cuba as "The home of rum", and Bacard\xc3\xad as "The king of rums and the rum of Kings". Expansion began overseas, first to <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in 1931 where it had architects Ludwig Mies Van Der Rohe and Felix Candela design office buildings and a bottling plant in Mexico City during the 1950s. The building complex was added to the tentative list of UNESCO\'s World Heritage Site list on 20 November 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1936, Bacardi began producing rum on U.S. territory in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> after Prohibition which enabled the company to sell rum tariff-free in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-washpost_2008-08-24_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washpost_2008-08-24-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The company later expanded to the United States in 1944 with the opening of Bacardi Imports, Inc. in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bacardilimited.com_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bacardilimited.com-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the company was led by Schueg\'s son-in-law, Jos\xc3\xa9 "Pepin" Bosch. Pepin founded Bacardi Imports in New York City, and became Cuba\'s <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_and_Prices_(Cuba)" title="Ministry of Finance and Prices (Cuba)">Minister of the Treasury</a> in 1949.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Cuban_Revolution">Cuban Revolution</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacardi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cuban Revolution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a> in 1959, the Bacard\xc3\xad family (and hence the company) supported and aided the rebels.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, after the triumph of the revolutionaries, and turn to communism, the family maintained a fierce <a href="/wiki/Cuban_dissident_movement" title="Cuban dissident movement">opposition to Fidel Castro\'s</a> policies in Cuba in the 1960s. In his book, <i>Bacardi and the Long Fight for Cuba</i>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Gjelten" title="Tom Gjelten">Tom Gjelten</a> describes how the Bacard\xc3\xad family and the company left Cuba in exile after the Cuban&#160;government confiscated the company\'s Cuban assets without compensation on 14 October 1960, particularly nationalizing and banning all private property on the island as well as all bank accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, due to concerns over the previous Cuban leader, <a href="/wiki/Fulgencio_Batista" title="Fulgencio Batista">Fulgencio Batista</a>, the company had started foreign branches a few years before the revolution; the company moved the ownership of its trademarks, assets and proprietary formulas out of the country to the Bahamas prior to the revolution and already produced Bacard\xc3\xad rum at other distillery sites&#160;in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. This helped the company survive after the Cuban government confiscated all Bacard\xc3\xad assets in the country without any compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1965, over 100 years after the company was established in Cuba, Bacardi established new roots and found a new home with global headquarters in Hamilton, Bermuda. In February 2019, Bacardi\'s CEO, Mahesh Madhavan, stated that Bacard\xc3\xad\'s global headquarters would remain in Bermuda for the next "500 years" and that "Bermuda is our home now."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg/220px-Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg/330px-Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg/440px-Bacardi_Headquarters.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a><figcaption>Bacardi Building, Bermuda. Location of Bacardi\'s world headquarters</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Reich" title="Otto Reich">Otto Reich</a>, a lobbyist in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> on behalf of Bacard\xc3\xad, drafted section 211 of the <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_Consolidated_and_Emergency_Appropriations_Act,_FY1999" class="mw-redirect" title="Omnibus Consolidated and Emergency Appropriations Act, FY1999">Omnibus Consolidated and Emergency Appropriations Act, FY1999</a>, a bill that became known as the Bacardi Act. Section 211 denied trademark protection to products of Cuban businesses expropriated after the Cuban revolution, a provision sought by Bacard\xc3\xad. The act was aimed primarily at the <a href="/wiki/Havana_Club" title="Havana Club">Havana Club</a> brand in the United States. The brand was created by the <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Arechabala_S.A." title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Arechabala S.A.">Jos\xc3\xa9 Arechabala</a> S.A. and nationalised without compensation in the Cuban revolution, the Arechabala family left Cuba and stopped producing rum. They therefore allowed the US trademark registration for "Havana Club" to lapse in 1973. Taking advantage of the lapse, the Cuban government registered the mark in the United States in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-Miami_Herald_2012-07-20_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miami_Herald_2012-07-20-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This new law was drafted to invalidate the trademark registration. Section 211 has been challenged unsuccessfully by the Cuban government and the European Union in US courts. It was ruled illegal by the <a href="/wiki/WTO" class="mw-redirect" title="WTO">WTO</a> in 2001 and 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The US Congress has yet to re-examine the matter. The brand was assigned by the Cuban government to <a href="/wiki/Pernod_Ricard" title="Pernod Ricard">Pernod Ricard</a> in 1993.\n</p><p>Bacardi rekindled the story of the Arechabala family and Havana Club in the United States when it launched the AMPARO Experience in 2018, an immersive play experience based in Miami, the city with the highest population of Cuban exiles. Exiled Cuban architect Enrique Gutierrez created a building that was hurricane-proof, using a system of steel cables and pulleys which allow the building to move slightly in the event of a strong shock. The steel cables are anchored into the bedrock and extend through marble-covered shafts up to the top floor, where they are led over large pulleys. Outside, on both sides of the eight-story building, more than 28,000 tiles painted and fired by <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Brennand" title="Francisco Brennand">Francisco Brennand</a>, depicting abstract blue flowers, were placed on the walls according to the artist\'s exact specifications.\n</p><p>In 1973, the company commissioned the square building in the plaza. Architect Ignacio Carrera-Justiz used cantilevered construction, a style invented by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>. Wright observed how well trees with taproots withstood hurricane-force winds. The building, raised 47 feet (14&#160;m) off the ground around a central core, features four massive walls, made of sections of inch-thick hammered glass mural tapestries, designed and manufactured in France. The striking design of the annex, affectionately known as the \'Jewel Box\' building,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> came from a painting by German artist Johannes M. The main brand of rum in Cuba is <a href="/wiki/Havana_Club" title="Havana Club">Havana Club</a>, produced by a company that was confiscated and nationalized by the government following the revolution. Several Bacard\xc3\xad spirits have performed notably well.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2020, <a href="/wiki/Bacardi_Superior" title="Bacardi Superior">Bacard\xc3\xad Superior</a>, Bacard\xc3\xad Gold, Bacard\xc3\xad Black, Bacard\xc3\xad A\xc3\xb1ejo Cuatro were each awarded a gold medal by the International Quality Institute Monde Selection. In addition, both Bacard\xc3\xad Reserva Ocho and Bacard\xc3\xad Gran Reserva Diez were awarded the top honor of Grand Gold quality award.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hemingway_connection">Hemingway connection</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacardi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hemingway connection"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a> lived in Cuba from 1939 until shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a>. He lived at <a href="/wiki/Finca_Vig%C3%ADa" title="Finca Vig\xc3\xada">Finca Vig\xc3\xada</a>, in the small town of San Francisco de Paula, located very close to Bacardi\'s <a href="/wiki/Modelo_Brewery" title="Modelo Brewery">Modelo Brewery</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hatuey_(beer)" title="Hatuey (beer)">Hatuey Beer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cotorro,_Havana" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotorro, Havana">Cotorro, Havana</a>.\n</p><p>In 1954, Compa\xc3\xb1\xc3\xada Ron Bacardi S.A. threw Hemingway a party when he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> \xe2\x80\x93 soon after the publication of his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Man_and_the_Sea" title="The Old Man and the Sea">The Old Man and the Sea</a></i> (1952) \xe2\x80\x93 in which he honored the company by mentioning its Hatuey beer. Hemingway also mentioned Bacard\xc3\xad and Hatuey in his novels <i><a href="/wiki/To_Have_and_Have_Not" title="To Have and Have Not">To Have and Have Not</a></i> (1937) and <i><a href="/wiki/For_Whom_the_Bell_Tolls" title="For Whom the Bell Tolls">For Whom the Bell Tolls</a></i> (1940). <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_Cabrera_Infante" title="Guillermo Cabrera Infante">Guillermo Cabrera Infante</a> wrote an account of the festivities for the periodical <i>Cicl\xc3\xb3n</i>, titled "El Viejo y la Marca" ("The Old Man and the Brand", a play on "El Viejo y el Mar", the book\'s Spanish title). In his account he described how "on one side there was a wooden stage with two streamers&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Hatuey beer and Bacardi rum&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 on each end and a Cuban flag in the middle. Next to the stage was a bar, at which people crowded, ordering daiquiris and beer, all free."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A sign at the event read "Bacardi rum welcomes the author of <i>The Old Man and the Sea</i>".\n</p><p>In his article "The Old Man and the Daiquiri", Wayne Curtis writes about how Hemingway\'s "home bar also held a bottle of Bacard\xc3\xad rum". Hemingway wrote in <i>Islands in the Stream</i>, "...this frozen daiquir\xc3\xad, so well beaten as it is, looks like the sea where the wave falls away from the bow of a ship when she is doing thirty knots."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading
