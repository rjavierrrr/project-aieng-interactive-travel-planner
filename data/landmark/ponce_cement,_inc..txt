The company was located at the intersection of <a href="/wiki/PR-123" class="mw-redirect" title="PR-123">PR-123</a> and <a href="/wiki/PR-500" class="mw-redirect" title="PR-500">PR-500</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Barrio" title="Barrio">Barrio</a> <a href="/wiki/Magueyes" class="mw-redirect" title="Magueyes">Magueyes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  It was founded in 1941<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> by Antonio Ferr\xc3\xa9 Bacallao, a Puerto Rican industrialist of Cuban origin.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1963, the company became the first <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican people">Puerto Rican</a> company to go public and be listed in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce Cement was part of the <i>Empresas Ferr\xc3\xa9</i> enterprise from 1941 to 2002. In 1950, Empresas Ferr\xc3\xa9 purchased another cement enterprise, the Puerto Rico Cement Company, then owned by the Government of Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2002, Ponce Cement, Inc., was sold to <a href="/wiki/Cemex" title="Cemex">Cemex</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mexican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican people">Mexican</a> business concern that is both the world\'s largest building materials supplier and the third largest cement producer, of which Ponce Cement is now a subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The plant continues to operate at the same location, and continues to sell its products to the Puerto Rico market, but with the change in ownership, the company is no longer named <i>Ponce Cement, Inc.</i>; it is now <i>Cemex, Puerto Rico</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The new owners did keep the <i>Cemento Ponce</i> product label.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span title="CEMEX">CEMEX</a>)</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The municipality of Ponce was the perfect place to establish a cement plant, as the type of soil needed for cement production is abundant in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After founding the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Iron_Works" title="Puerto Rico Iron Works">Puerto Rico Iron Works</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/El_Nuevo_D%C3%ADa" title="El Nuevo D\xc3\xada">El Dia</a> newspaper, Empresas Ferr\xc3\xa9 entered the construction business with Ponce Cement, Inc., and subsequently with Puerto Rican Cement, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-puertorico-herald.org_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puertorico-herald.org-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Over the 1940s, the company enlarged and <a href="/wiki/Luis_A._Ferr%C3%A9" title="Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9">Luis A. Ferr\xc3\xa9</a> became its chief engineer.  By 1960, the company had become the leading cement supplier on the island, much of it the result of increasing new highway and housing construction projects spreading throughout the Island.<sup id="cite_ref-puertorico-herald.org_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puertorico-herald.org-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Approvals">Approvals</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ponce_Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Approvals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>On February 23, 1989, the Ponce Cement plant received approval for conversion from a wet to a dry manufacturing process, which allowed it to almost double its output.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As of year 2000, cement was Puerto Rico\'s leading nonfuel mineral\ncommodity.<sup
