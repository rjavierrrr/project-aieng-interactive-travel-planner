border: 0;">Added to NRHP</th><td class="infobox-data" style="border: 0;">September 23, 2008<sup id="cite_ref-newlistings20081003_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newlistings20081003-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></td></tr><tr style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" class="infobox-below"><div class="metadata" style="text-align: right;">&#91;<a href="https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q102327045" class="extiw" title="d:Q102327045">edit on Wikidata</a>]</div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Arecibo Observatory</b>, also known as the <b>National Astronomy and Ionosphere Center</b> (<b>NAIC</b>) and formerly known as the <b>Arecibo Ionosphere Observatory</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Esperanza,_Arecibo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Esperanza, Arecibo, Puerto Rico">Barrio Esperanza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arecibo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Arecibo, Puerto Rico">Arecibo, Puerto Rico</a> owned by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF).\n</p><p>The observatory\'s main instrument was the <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Telescope" title="Arecibo Telescope">Arecibo Telescope</a>, a 305&#160;m (1,000&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Spherical_reflector" class="mw-redirect" title="Spherical reflector">spherical reflector</a> <a href="/wiki/Dish_antenna" class="mw-redirect" title="Dish antenna">dish</a> built into a natural <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a>, with a cable-mount steerable receiver and several <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> transmitters for emitting signals mounted 150&#160;m (492&#160;ft) above the dish. Completed in 1963, it was the world\'s largest single-aperture telescope for 53 years, surpassed in July 2016 by the <a href="/wiki/Five-hundred-meter_Aperture_Spherical_Telescope" title="Five-hundred-meter Aperture Spherical Telescope">Five-hundred-meter Aperture Spherical Telescope</a> (FAST) in China. Following two breaks in cables supporting the receiver platform in mid-2020, the NSF decommissioned the telescope. A full collapse of the telescope occurred on December 1, 2020, before either repairs or controlled demolition could be conducted. In 2022, the NSF announced the telescope will not be rebuilt, with an educational facility to be established on the site.\n</p><p>The observatory also includes a smaller <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">LIDAR</a> facility, and a visitor center, which remained operational after the telescope\'s collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The asteroid <a href="/wiki/4337_Arecibo" title="4337 Arecibo">4337 Arecibo</a> is named after the observatory by <a href="/wiki/Steven_J._Ostro" title="Steven J. Ostro">Steven J. Ostro</a>, in recognition of the observatory\'s contributions to the characterization of Solar System bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-MPC-object_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Telescope#History" title="Arecibo Telescope">History of the Arecibo Telescope</a></div>\n<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> (DoD) <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Research Projects Agency">Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (ARPA) missile defense program, ARPA had sought a means to try to detect incoming missiles while they traveled through the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. On November 6, 1959, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> entered into a contract with ARPA to carry out development studies for a large-scale ionospheric radar probe.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Arecibo Telescope was funded as a means to study Earth\'s ionosphere for this purpose, and serving a dual-use as a general-purpose <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a>. Construction of the telescope and its supporting facilities were started in September of 1960, with the telescope operational by 1963. The telescope and supporting observatory were formally opened as the Arecibo Ionospheric Observatory on November 1, 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-congrecord_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-congrecord-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Ownership of the observatory transferred from the DoD to the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> on October 1, 1969. NSF named Cornell University to manage the observatory\'s functions. By September 1971, NSF renamed the observatory as the National Astronomy and Ionosphere Center (NAIC) and had made it a <a href="/wiki/Federally_funded_research_and_development_center" class="mw-redirect" title="Federally funded research and development center">federally funded research and development center</a> (FFRDC).<sup id="cite_ref-congrecord_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-congrecord-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> began contributing towards funding of the observatory alongside NSF as to support its planetary radar mission.<sup id="cite_ref-SP4218_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SP4218-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the early 2000s, NASA started to reduce their contribution to the Arecibo Observatory, putting more pressure on NSF to continue to fund the facility.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2006, NSF made its first possible suggestion of significantly reducing its funding towards Arecibo and potentially decommissioning the observatory.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Academics and politicians lobbied to increase funding bookmarked for Arecibo to stave off its closure, and NASA recommitted funding in 2011 for study of <a href="/wiki/Near-earth_object" class="mw-redirect" title="Near-earth object">near-earth objects</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> To further cut losses, in 2011 NSF delisted Arecibo as a FFRDC, removed Cornell as the site operator, and replaced them with a collaborative team led by <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>, which allowed the observatory to be able to offer its facilities to a wider range of projects.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Damage to the telescope from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> in 2017 led NSF again to consider the possibility of decommissioning the observatory as the costs of maintaining it had become too great.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A consortium led by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Central_Florida" title="University of Central Florida">University of Central Florida</a> (UCF) stepped forward to offer to manage the observatory and cover a significant portion of the operations and maintenance costs, and in 2018, NSF made UCF\'s consortium the new site operators,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ucf_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucf-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> though no specific actions were announced.\n</p><p>After an auxiliary and main cable failure on the telescope in August and November 2020, respectively, the NSF announced the decision that they would decommission the telescope through controlled demolition, but that the other facilities on the observatory would remain operational in the future. Before the safe decommission of the telescope could occur, remaining support cables from one tower rapidly failed in the morning of December 1, 2020, causing the instrument platform to crash through the dish, shearing off the tops of the support towers, and partially damaging some of the other buildings, though there were no injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_collapsed_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_collapsed-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> NSF stated in 2020 that it was their intention to have the other observatory facilities operational as soon as possible and were looking at plans to rebuild a new telescope instrument in its place.<sup id="cite_ref-verge_decommission_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge_decommission-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2022, the NSF announced the telescope will not be rebuilt, with an educational facility to be established on the site.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cold_Spring_Harbor_Laboratory" title="Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory">Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory</a> in New York; the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_Baltimore_County" title="University of Maryland, Baltimore County">University of Maryland, Baltimore County</a>; the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico,_R%C3%ADo_Piedras_Campus" title="University of Puerto Rico, R\xc3\xado Piedras Campus">University of Puerto Rico, R\xc3\xado Piedras Campus</a> in San Juan; and the <a href="/wiki/Universidad_del_Sagrado_Coraz%C3%B3n" title="Universidad del Sagrado Coraz\xc3\xb3n">University of the Sacred Heart</a>, also in San Juan were selected by NSF in 2023 to set up and run an education center called <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://areciboc3.org">Arecibo C3</a> (Arecibo Center for Culturally Relevant and Inclusive Science Education, Computational Skills, and Community Engagement).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Facilities">Facilities</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Arecibo_Telescope">Arecibo Telescope</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arecibo Telescope"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Telescope" title="Arecibo Telescope">Arecibo Telescope</a></div>\n<p>The observatory\'s main feature was its large <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Telescope" title="Arecibo Telescope">radio telescope</a>, whose main collecting dish was an inverted <a href="/wiki/Spherical_dome" class="mw-redirect" title="Spherical dome">spherical dome</a> 1,000 feet (305&#160;m) in diameter with an 869-foot (265&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_curvature" title="Radius of curvature">radius of curvature</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-arecibo-multifeed_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arecibo-multifeed-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> constructed inside a <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The dish\'s surface was made of 38,778 perforated aluminum panels, each about 3 by 7 feet (1 by 2&#160;m), supported by a mesh of steel cables.<sup id="cite_ref-arecibo-multifeed_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arecibo-multifeed-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ground beneath supported shade-tolerant vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Since its completion in November 1963, the Telescope had been used for <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">radar astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, and had been part of the <a href="/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence" title="Search for extraterrestrial intelligence">Search for extraterrestrial intelligence</a> (SETI) program. It was also used by NASA for <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">Near-Earth object</a> detection. Since around 2006, NSF funding support for the telescope had waned as the Foundation directed funds to newer instruments, though academics petitioned to the NSF and Congress to continue support for the telescope. Numerous hurricanes, including <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a>, had damaged parts of the telescope, straining the reduced budget.\n</p><p>Two cable breaks, one in August 2020 and a second in November 2020, threatened the structural integrity of the support structure for the suspended platform and damaged the dish. The NSF determined in November 2020 that it was safer to decommission the telescope rather than to try to repair it, but the telescope collapsed before a controlled demolition could be carried out. The remaining support cables from one tower failed around 7:56&#160;a.m. local time on December 1, 2020, causing the receiver platform to fall into the dish and collapsing the telescope.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_collapsed_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_collapsed-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-APNews1Dec2020_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APNews1Dec2020-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>NASA led an extensive failure investigation and reported the findings,<sup id="cite_ref-collapsed_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collapsed-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> along with a technical bulletin with industry recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The investigation concluded that "a combination of low socket <a href="/wiki/Design_margin" class="mw-redirect" title="Design margin">design margin</a> and a high percentage of sustained loading revealed an unexpected vulnerability to <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">zinc creep</a> and environments, resulting in long-term cumulative damage and progressive zinc/wire failure".\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Additional_telescopes">Additional telescopes</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arecibo_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Additional telescopes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The Arecibo Observatory also has other facilities beyond the main telescope, including a 12-meter (39&#160;ft) radio telescope intended for <a href="/wiki/Very-long-baseline_interferometry" title="Very-long-baseline interferometry">very-long-baseline interferometry</a> (VLBI) with the main telescope;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and a LIDAR facility<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> whose research has continued since the main telescope\'s collapse.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto;overflow:hidden;width:auto;max-width:808px"><div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize" 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Arecibo_Observatory%2C_sign_at_entrance_gate.jpg/440px-Arecibo_Observatory%2C_sign_at_entrance_gate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5312" data-file-height="2988" /></a><figcaption>Logo of the observatory at the entrance gate</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Opened in 1997, the \xc3\x81ngel Ramos Foundation Visitor Center features interactive exhibits and displays about the operations of the radio telescope, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" class="mw-redirect" (industrialist)">\xc3\x81ngel Ramos</a>, owner of the <i><a href="/wiki/El_Mundo_(Puerto_Rico)" title="El Mundo (Puerto Rico)">El Mundo</a></i> newspaper and founder of <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a>. The Foundation provided half of the funds to build the Visitor Center, with the remainder received from private donations and <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>.\n</p><p>The center, in collaboration with the Caribbean Astronomical Society,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> hosts a series of Astronomical Nights throughout the year, which feature diverse discussions regarding <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanets</a>, astronomical phenomena, and discoveries (such as <a href="/wiki/Comet_ISON" title="Comet ISON">Comet ISON</a>).
