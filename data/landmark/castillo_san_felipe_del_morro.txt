style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Castillo San Felipe del Morro</b> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: Promontory Castle of Saint Philip), most commonly known as <i><b>El Morro</b></i> <i>(</i>The Promontory<i>)</i>, is a large <a href="/wiki/Medieval_fortification" title="Medieval fortification">fortress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">citadel</a> in the historic district of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. Commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">King Charles I of Spain</a> in 1539, it was first built as a <a href="/wiki/Fortified_tower" title="Fortified tower">fortified tower</a> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">King Philip II</a>, who oversaw its expansion into a <a href="/wiki/Hornwork" title="Hornwork">hornwork fort</a> by 1595. Over the next 200 years, especially in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">King Charles III</a>, <i>El Morro</i> continued to be developed to reach its current form in 1787. Rising 140 ft from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> shoreline with 18 to 25 ft thick walls, the six-leveled edifice stands on a steep, rocky <a href="/wiki/Headland" title="Headland">headland</a> <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontory</a> on <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Islet" title="San Juan Islet">San Juan Islet</a> guarding the entry to <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>, the harbor of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>. <i>El Morro</i>, alongside <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Crist%C3%B3bal_(San_Juan)" title="Castillo San Crist\xc3\xb3bal (San Juan)">San Crist\xc3\xb3bal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort%C3%ADn_San_Juan_de_la_Cruz" title="Fort\xc3\xadn San Juan de la Cruz">El Ca\xc3\xb1uelo</a>, and other forts part of the <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Old_San_Juan" title="Walls of Old San Juan">Walls of Old San Juan</a>, protected <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Puerto_Rico#Strategic_geography" title="Geography of Puerto Rico">strategically and militarily</a> important <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, or <i>La Llave de las Indias</i> (The Key to the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Indies" title="Council of the Indies">Indies</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from invasion by competing <a href="/wiki/List_of_modern_great_powers#Early_modern_powers" title="List of modern great powers">world powers</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was designated a <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Lying on the northwesternmost point of the islet of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Old San Juan, Puerto Rico">Old San Juan</a>, Castillo San Felipe del Morro is named in honor of King <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a>. The fortification, also referred to as <i>el Morro</i> or \'the <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">promontory</a>,\' was designed to guard the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>, and defend the Spanish colonial port city of San Juan from seaborne enemies.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_310_pgs_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS_310_pgs-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1983, the citadel was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_National_Historic_Site" title="San Juan National Historic Site">San Juan National Historic Site</a>. Over two million visitors a year explore the <i>castillo</i>, making it one of Puerto Rico\'s leading tourist attractions. Facing the structure, on the opposite side of the bay, a smaller fortification known as <i><a href="/wiki/El_Ca%C3%B1uelo" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ca\xc3\xb1uelo">El Ca\xc3\xb1uelo</a></i> complemented the castillo\'s defense of the entrance to the bay.<sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPS_310_pgs_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS_310_pgs-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span data-file-height="1960" /></a><figcaption>El Morro in 1591</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The construction of the citadel and its surrounding walls began in 1539 on orders of King <a href="/wiki/Carlos_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlos V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The original fortress was built under the direction of conquistador <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diego_Ramos_de_Orozco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Diego Ramos de Orozco (page does not exist)">Diego Ramos de Orozco</a> and its main purpose was to defend the port of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> by controlling the entry to its harbor. In order to have a viable defense while the rest of the fort was being completed, a small proto-fortress was erected during the first year of construction. It is estimated that this section comprises about 10% of the whole structure.\n</p><p>It was not till 1587, however, that Field Marshal Juan de Tejeda and the Italian engineer-architect <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Antonelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Bautista Antonelli">Juan Bautista Antonelli</a> drew the fort\'s final design. The plan, which was based on the then firmly established Spanish military fortification principles of the time, included fortifying nine other sites in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Main" title="Spanish Main">Spanish Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_West_Indies" title="Spanish West Indies">Spanish West Indies</a>: <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Marta" title="Santa Marta">Santa Marta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">Cartagena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nombre_de_Dios,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Nombre de Dios, Col\xc3\xb3n">Nombre de Dios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portobelo,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Portobelo, Col\xc3\xb3n">Portobelo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chagres_River" title="Chagres River">Chagres River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama_City" title="Panama City">Panama City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">St. Augustine</a>. San Juan construction began in March 1589 with skilled artisans, 12 <a href="/wiki/Stonemasonry" title="Stonemasonry">stonecutters</a>, 18 masons, 2 smiths, a <a href="/wiki/Cooper_(profession)" title="Cooper (profession)">cooper</a>, metal founder, and an overseer assigned to the task, with the help of 150 slaves. <a href="/wiki/Captain_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain General">Captain General</a> Diego Men\xc3\xa9ndez de Vald\xc3\xa9s, who was the governor of Puerto Rico, took over construction after Tejeda and Antonelli left to start construction of Santo Domingo\'s fortifications. Capt. Pedro de Salazar took over construction in 1591.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 29\xe2\x80\x9331">&#58;&#8202;29\xe2\x80\x9331&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:El_morro_from_front.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/El_morro_from_front.jpg/250px-El_morro_from_front.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="166" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/El_morro_from_front.jpg/375px-El_morro_from_front.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/El_morro_from_front.jpg/500px-El_morro_from_front.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a><figcaption>View of El Morro\'s entrance</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The new fortifications consisted of a <a href="/wiki/Hornwork" title="Hornwork">hornwork</a>, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Headland" title="Headland">headland</a>, to protect the landward side of the existing tower and water battery. Two half-<a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastions</a>, one on the Atlantic side called "Tejeda", and another on the harbor side called "Austria", were connected by a <a href="/wiki/Curtain_wall_(fortification)" title="Curtain wall (fortification)">curtain wall</a> fronted by a <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a>, and spanned by a <a href="/wiki/Drawbridge" title="Drawbridge">drawbridge</a> in the center. The gate and drawbridge were protected by a <a href="/wiki/Ravelin" title="Ravelin">ravelin</a>, and just inside the gate was a guardhouse. A pair of <a href="/wiki/Artillery_battery" title="Artillery battery">batteries</a> behind the hornwork overlooked the sea and harbor.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 30\xe2\x80\x9331, 54\xe2\x80\x9355">&#58;&#8202;30\xe2\x80\x9331,&#8202;54\xe2\x80\x9355&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p><p>In 1634, construction of the city walls surrounding San Juan began. By 1650, the town was enclosed on the east, south and west, while natural <a href="/wiki/Battlement" title="Battlement">battlements</a> protected the city along the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 52\xe2\x80\x9355">&#58;&#8202;52\xe2\x80\x9355&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p><p>In 1765, <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_O%27Reilly" title="Alejandro O&#39;Reilly">Alejandro O\'Reilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inspector_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Inspector General">Inspector General</a> of Cuba, and Colonel Tom\xc3\xa1s O\'Daly, San Juan Chief of Engineers, agreed on a plan to strengthen San Juan\'s defenses, which was approved by <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III of Spain</a>. San Juan became a Defense of the First Order, and one of the most powerful plazas in the Americas by 1790. El Morro\'s improvements included 3 <a href="/wiki/Cistern" title="Cistern">cisterns</a> under the main plaza containing 216,000 gallons of water collected from times of rain. The walls were strengthened to be 18\xe2\x80\x9340 feet thick. These walls consisted of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> blocks forming the exterior and interior, with <a href="/wiki/Rubble" title="Rubble">rubble</a> sandwiched in between. The Santa B\xc3\xa1rbara battery became the main battery with 37 cannon, supported by <a href="/wiki/Casemate" title="Casemate">casemate</a> guns at a lower level, and backed by a great wall with casemates at a higher level.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 54\xe2\x80\x9355, 59\xe2\x80\x9361, 64\xe2\x80\x9365">&#58;&#8202;54\xe2\x80\x9355,&#8202;59\xe2\x80\x9361,&#8202;64\xe2\x80\x9365&#8202;</span></sup>\n</p><p>The top of the <a href="/wiki/Castillo_de_San_Felipe_del_Morro_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Castillo de San Felipe del Morro Lighthouse">Castillo de San Felipe del Morro Lighthouse</a> was destroyed during the 1898 bombardment of the city by the United States, and the American flag replaced the Spanish on 18 November 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Spanish_Rule_(1539\xe2\x80\x931898)"><span id="Spanish_Rule_.281539.E2.80.931898.29"></span>Spanish Rule (1539\xe2\x80\x931898)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spanish Rule (1539\xe2\x80\x931898)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Castillo_san_felipe_del_moro.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Castillo_san_felipe_del_moro.jpg/175px-Castillo_san_felipe_del_moro.jpg" decoding="async" width="175" height="257" class="mw-file-element" Morro</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Upon the advice of <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Oviedo_y_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Gonzalo Fern\xc3\xa1ndez de Oviedo y Vald\xc3\xa9s">Gonzalo Fern\xc3\xa1ndez de Oviedo y Vald\xc3\xa9s</a>, a battery was constructed on the rocky promontory called "the Morro", when the location of <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a> was deemed unsuitable. This battery consisted of a tower with 4 <a href="/wiki/Embrasure" title="Embrasure">embrasures</a>, and a Water Battery at the foot of the slope for 3 guns. By 1555, Morro had 8 bronze cannons, as a defense against French <a href="/wiki/Privateer" title="Privateer">privateers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPS_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>During the Spanish government of the island, El Morro, also known as Castillo de San Felipe, survived several attacks from foreign powers on various occasions.\n</p><p>In 1593 Portuguese soldiers, sent from Lisbon by order of Phillip II, composed the first garrison of the San Felipe del Morro fortress in Puerto Rico. Some brought their wives, while others married Puerto Rican women, and today there are many Puerto Rican families with Portuguese last names.\n</p><p>In 1595, Englishman Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> unsuccessfully attacked <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> with his fleet in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_(1595)" title="Battle of San Juan (1595)">Battle of San Juan (1595)</a>.\n</p><p>In 1598, the English attacked again, led by <a href="/wiki/George_Clifford,_3rd_Earl_of_Cumberland" title="George Clifford, 3rd Earl of Cumberland">George Clifford</a>, 3rd Earl of Cumberland. Clifford succeeded because he attacked San Juan over land instead of trying to enter through the San Juan Bay. However, an epidemic of <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a> forced him to flee the island after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_(1598)" title="Battle of San Juan (1598)">Battle of San Juan (1598)</a>.\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg/220px-Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg/330px-Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg/440px-Gate_El_Morro_SJU_06_2019_6612.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2000" /></a><figcaption>Main gate of the castle</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In 1625, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Boudewijn_Hendricksz" title="Boudewijn Hendricksz">Boudewijn Hendricksz</a>, also attacked the island emulating George Clifford\'s overland invasion. To the amazement of the citizens, the invaders were able to pass in front of the castle\'s defenders and into the harbor, out of reach of the city\'s cannons. El Morro managed to resist the siege and eventually made the Dutch retire, although the attackers were able to sack and burn the city before leaving the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_(1625)" title="Battle of San Juan (1625)">Battle of San Juan (1625)</a>.\n</p><p>In 1797, British General Ralph Abercromby and Admiral Henry Harvey, with a force of 7,000\xe2\x80\x9313,000 men, invaded the island of Puerto Rico. Captain General Don Ram\xc3\xb3n de Castro and his forces repelled the attack. Abercromby and Harvey were defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Juan_(1797)" title="Battle of San Juan (1797)">Battle of San Juan (1797)</a>.\n</p><p>El Morro\'s last active fight occurred during a naval bombardment by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> during the 1898 <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War">Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War</a>, ending the age of naval warfare in the Caribbean, at least in the classical sense. During the Spanish\xe2\x80\x93American War, the castle was attacked at least three times by American naval forces, the largest being the <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_San_Juan" title="Bombardment of San Juan">Bombardment of San Juan</a> on May 12, 1898. The war ended with the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1898)" title="Treaty of Paris (1898)">Treaty of Paris</a>, in which Spain ceded ownership of the islands of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, Guam, and the Philippines to the United States.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="American_military_occupation_(1898\xe2\x80\x931961)"><span id="American_military_occupation_.281898.E2.80.931961.29"></span>American military occupation (1898\xe2\x80\x931961)</h3><span (Library of Congress Prints and Photographs Division Washington).</figcaption></figure>\n<p>El Morro and many other Spanish government buildings in Old San Juan became part of a large U.S. Army post, called <a href="/wiki/Fort_Brooke,_Puerto_Rico" title="Fort Brooke, Puerto Rico">Fort Brooke</a>. In the early 20th century, the U.S. military filled up the <a href="/wiki/Esplanade" title="Esplanade">esplanade</a> (the green space in front of "El Morro") with baseball diamonds, hospitals, officers\' quarters, an officers\' club and even a <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> course.\n</p><p>On March 21, 1915, Lt. <a href="/wiki/Te%C3%B3filo_Marxuach" title="Te\xc3\xb3filo Marxuach">Te\xc3\xb3filo Marxuach</a> was the officer of the day at the El Morro fortress. The <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_America_Line" title="Hamburg America Line">Hamburg America Line</a> <a href="/wiki/Cargo_liner" title="Cargo liner">cargo liner</a> <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Newport_News_(AK-3)" title="USS Newport News (AK-3)">Odenwald</a></i> tried to leave port without permission from the Customs Collector. Lt. Marxuach gave the order to open fire on her, which forced her to stop.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The shots ordered by Lt. Marxuach are widely regarded to be the first shots fired by the United States in World War I, although the first actual wartime shot fired by the US came on the day war was declared, during the <a href="/wiki/Scuttling_of_SMS_Cormoran" title="Scuttling of SMS Cormoran">scuttling of SMS <i>Cormoran</i></a> off <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>.\n</p><p>During World War II the United States Army added a massive concrete bunker to the top of El Morro to serve as a Harbor Defense Fire Control Station to direct a network of coastal artillery sites, and to keep watch for German submarines which were ravaging shipping in the Caribbean. A lighthouse, rebuilt by the U.S. Army in 1906\xe2\x80\x9308 is the tallest point on El Morro, standing 180 feet (55 m) above sea level. Flagpoles on El Morro today customarily fly the <a href="/wiki/United_States_flag" class="mw-redirect" title="United States flag">United States flag</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_flag" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican flag">Puerto Rican flag</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cross_of_Burgundy" title="Cross of Burgundy">Cross of Burgundy</a> flag, also known in Spanish as las Aspas de Borgo\xc3\xb1a, a standard which was widely used by Spanish armies around the world from 1506 to 1785.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="National_Park_(1961\xe2\x80\x93present)"><span id="National_Park_.281961.E2.80.93present.29"></span>National Park (1961\xe2\x80\x93present)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National Park (1961\xe2\x80\x93present)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-halign-center" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg/750px-El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg" decoding="async" width="750" height="164" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg/1125px-El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg/1500px-El_Morro_Old_San_Juan_Panorama.jpg 2x" data-file-width="11547" data-file-height="2523" /></a><figcaption>Panorama view of the city from the castle</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In 1961, the United States Army officially retired from El Morro. The fort became a part of the National Park Service to be preserved as museums. In 1983, the Castillo and the city walls were declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. In honor of the Quincentennial of the voyages of Columbus in 1992 the exterior esplanade was cleared of palm trees that had been planted by the U.S. Army in the Fort Brooke era, and restored to the open appearance this "field-of-fire" for El Morro\'s cannon would have had in colonial Spanish times. Parking lots and paved roads were also removed, and the El Morro lighthouse repaired and restored to its original appearance. The 1963 film <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Island_(1963_film)" title="Thunder Island (1963 film)">Thunder Island</a> featured an extensive foot chase and shootout scene filmed at El Morro. El Morro was also used as a film set in the 1996 motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Amistad_(1997_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amistad (1997 film)">Amistad</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> used it to represent a fort in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> where African slaves were auctioned in 1839. African slave labor was used in addition to local labor to help build the castillo. El Morro was a defensive military fortification and a major component of San Juan\'s harbor defense system. Puerto Rico as such was considered by the Spanish crown as the "Key to the Antilles"; no enemy ship could navigate its waters without fear of capture.\n</p><p>An annual artisans festival is held at the fort every July.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The fort is home to a resident colony of around 200 stray cats that are believed to have been descended from cats introduced either during colonial times or in the mid-20th century as part of rat eradication programs. Following concerns over an increase in their population, in 2023 the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> announced it would contract an animal welfare organization to remove the cats and threatened to hire a pest control agency if it were to fail. However, the plan was suspended following a lawsuit by animal welfare groups in 2024.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span
