style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>Paseo de la Princesa</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: <i>Promenade of the Princess</i> or <i>Princess Promenade</i>), is a partially <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">waterside</a> 19th-century pedestrian <a href="/wiki/Esplanade" title="Esplanade">promenade</a> about .50 miles (0.80&#160;km) in length, located in the <a href="/wiki/Historic_district" title="Historic district">historic district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. Constructed between 1852 and 1854 in honor of <a href="/wiki/Isabella_II" title="Isabella II">Queen Isabella II of Spain</a>\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Firstborn" title="Firstborn">first-born</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infanta_Isabel,_Countess_of_Girgenti" title="Infanta Isabel, Countess of Girgenti">Princess of Asturias Infanta Isabel</a>, the promenade runs adjacent and parallel to the southwestern section of the <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Old_San_Juan" title="Walls of Old San Juan">Walls of Old San Juan</a><i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></i> Lined with <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">lampposts</a> and benches, large trees and gardens, and varying fountains and sculptures, it hosts food and artisan vendors, musical and theatrical <a href="/wiki/Street_performance" title="Street performance">entertainers</a>, and cultural restaurants and festivals.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The promenade starts at <i>Basti\xc3\xb3n de la Derecha de San Justo y Pastor</i> on the <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Old_San_Juan" title="Walls of Old San Juan">Walls of Old San Juan</a> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marina,_Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marina, Old San Juan (page does not exist)">Marina</a><i>,</i> the southernmost <a href="/wiki/Barrios_of_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Barrios of San Juan, Puerto Rico">sub-barrio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a> on the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Islet" title="San Juan Islet">San Juan Islet</a>, passing through passing by the <i>Antigua Prisi\xc3\xb3n La Princesa</i> (<a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Princesa_Prison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="La Princesa Prison (page does not exist)">Old La Princesa Prison</a>) from 1837, currently housing the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Tourism_Company" title="Puerto Rico Tourism Company">Puerto Rico Tourism Company</a>, and the popular <i>Fuente Ra\xc3\xadces</i> (Roots Fountain), a large sculptural fountain overlooking <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a> since 1992 that represents <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican identity</a>, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Taino" class="mw-redirect" title="Taino">Taino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_colonial_Spanish_America" title="Slavery in colonial Spanish America">Sub-Saharan African</a> ancestry and culture, and ending in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Barrios_of_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Barrios of San Juan, Puerto Rico">sub-barrio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Catedral,_Old_San_Juan" title="Catedral, Old San Juan">Catedral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a> at the beginning of the western section of the <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Old_San_Juan" title="Walls of Old San Juan">Walls of Old San Juan</a> at <i>Puerta de San Juan</i> (San Juan Gate), formerly known a<i>s Puerta de Agua</i> (meaning \'water gate\'), which lies next to <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a>, the 16th-century executive residence of the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Governor of Puerto Rico</a>, and is the starting location of <a href="/wiki/Paseo_del_Morro" title="Paseo del Morro">Paseo del Morro</a> (Morro Promande)<i>,</i> the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">waterfront</a> promenade covering the rest of the western section of the defensive walls, culminating on <i>Punta del Morro</i> (Morro Point) at the bottom of <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">Castillo San Felipe del Morro</a> at the westernmost position on <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Islet" title="San Juan Islet">San Juan Islet</a> overlooking the entrance to <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><i><sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></i>\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paseo_de_la_Princesa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p><i>Paseo de la Princesa</i> is often described as one of the most scenic city walkways in the world and, as a result of its location close to the cruise ship harbor, it is often the starting point for sightseeing in <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Located on a plaza at the midpoint of the promenade, <i>Fuente Ra\xc3\xadces</i> (Roots Fountain) is a 1992 sculpture fountain by Spanish sculptor <a href="/wiki/Luis_Sanguino" title="Luis Sanguino">Luis Sanguino</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> representing and celebrating the ancestral roots of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican identity</a>: the <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">European</a> culture brought by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_settlement_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Spanish settlement of Puerto Rico">Spanish</a> and other settlers, the <a href="/wiki/Afro%E2%80%93Puerto_Ricans" title="Afro\xe2\x80\x93Puerto Ricans">African culture</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Sun-Saharan African slaves</a> and the indigenous culture by the native <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">Taino</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This plaza also hosts a small wooden dock that offers great views of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Yunque_(Puerto_Rico)" title="El Yunque (Puerto Rico)">El Yunque</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Luquillo" title="Sierra de Luquillo">Sierra de Luquillo</a> to the east, and of other municipalities, such as <a href="/wiki/Cata%C3%B1o,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cata\xc3\xb1o, Puerto Rico">Cata\xc3\xb1o</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bayam%C3%B3n,_Puerto_Rico" title="Bayam\xc3\xb3n, Puerto Rico">Bayam\xc3\xb3n</a> to the west.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery iconic <i>Puerta de San Juan</i> (<i>San Juan Gate</i>), originally called the <i>Puerta de Agua</i> (<i>Water Gate</i>) because it was the first gate to offer water access to the city of <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>, is found at the end of the promenade. The gate is near <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capilla_del_Cristo" title="Capilla del Cristo">Capilla del Cristo</a> and <i>Parque de las Palomas</i>. There is also waterside promenade named <a href="/wiki/Paseo_del_Morro" title="Paseo del Morro">Paseo del Morro</a> that continues along the western defensive walls towards <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">El Morro</a> at the entrance to <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>.\n</p><p>Other landmarks and attractions are the <i>Americas Heritage Fountain</i>, the <i>San Justo y Pastor Bastion</i>, the <i>La Concepci\xc3\xb3n Bastion</i>, and a playground for children. The promenade also hosts an open air caf\xc3\xa9 and a restaurant that serves traditional <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_cuisine" title="Puerto Rican cuisine">Puerto Rican food</a> called <i>Princesa Gastrobar</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Piragua_(food)" title="Piragua (food)">Piraguas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pi%C3%B1a_colada" title="Pi\xc3\xb1a colada">pi\xc3\xb1a coladas</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fried_plantain" title="Fried plantain">platanutres</a></i> (fried plantain chips) are popular drinks and snacks to buy in the esplanade.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During the weekends and festival celebrations, one can find live music, street vendors, traditional street food and temporary art exhibitions. There is live <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> on Fridays and Saturdays, and <a href="/wiki/Salsa_music" title="Salsa music">salsa music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salsa_(dance)" title="Salsa (dance)">dance</a> on Saturdays and Sundays.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paseo_de_la_Princesa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png/250px-1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="124" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png/375px-1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png/500px-1898_promenade_San_Juan_Porto_Rico.png 2x" data-file-width="972" data-file-height="483" /></a><figcaption>View from midpoint of <i>Paseo de la Princesa</i> towards entrance of  promenade in 1898</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In 1837, the interim prison of <i>Antigua Prisi\xc3\xb3n La Princesa (The Princess Old Prison</i>), originally known as <i>Real C\xc3\xa1rcel La Princesa (The Princess Royal Jail</i>), was first built in the named after <a href="/wiki/Isabella_II_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabella II of Spain">Queen Isabella II of Spain</a>, who in 1833 was proclaimed sovereign at the age of three following her father <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII" title="Ferdinand VII">Ferdinand VII</a>\xe2\x80\x99s death. \n</p><p>In 1854, Puerto Rico\'s Governor and Lieutenant General Don <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A1ndo_Norzagaray_y_Escudero" class="mw-redirect" title="Fern\xc3\xa1ndo Norzagaray y Escudero">Fernando de Norzagaray</a> foresaw an expansion of the prison and the construction of its tower. The prison had capacity for about 240 prisoners and continued to operate until 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>While the promenade itself dates to the 1850s, its location was originally used by the Spanish military as a cleared field of fire for the cannons and guns that were located outside of the Walls of Old San Juan. \n</p><p>The promenade and surrounding areas fell into disrepair during the 20th century, but it was rehabilitated and restored in 1989. The <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Tourism_Company" title="Puerto Rico Tourism Company">Puerto Rico Tourism Company</a> is now headquartered at the former prison. The company also installed a time capsule in 1995, located under the promenade section in front of the historic building. The time capsule was opened in 2020, and two more were installed afterwards. These capsules are stated to be opened in 2045.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i>La Princesa</i> prison building also houses a small art exhibition and often hosts cultural events.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Gallery">Gallery</h2><span
