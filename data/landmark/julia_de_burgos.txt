(February 17, 1914 \xe2\x80\x93 July 6, 1953), also known as Julia de Burgos, was a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican</a> poet, journalist, and teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-BC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BC-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>As an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Independence_movement_in_Puerto_Rico" title="Independence movement in Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican independence</a>, she served as Secretary General of the Daughters of Freedom, the women\'s branch of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Nationalist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican Nationalist Party">Puerto Rican Nationalist Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> She was also a <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activist</a> for women and African and <a href="/wiki/Afro-Caribbean_people" title="Afro-Caribbean people">Afro-Caribbean</a> writers.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Early_years">Early years</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julia_de_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Julia de Burgos (birth name: <b>Julia Constanza Burgos Garc\xc3\xada</b>) was born to Francisco Burgos Hans, a farmer, and Paula Garc\xc3\xada de Burgos.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Her father was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_National_Guard" title="Puerto Rico National Guard">Puerto Rico National Guard</a> and had a farm near the town of <a href="/wiki/Carolina,_Puerto_Rico" title="Carolina, Puerto Rico">Carolina, Puerto Rico</a>, where she was born. The family later moved to the <a href="/wiki/Barrio" title="Barrio">barrio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_Carolina,_Puerto_Rico" title="Santa Cruz, Carolina, Puerto Rico">Santa Cruz</a> of the same city. She was the oldest of thirteen children. Six of her younger siblings died of malnutrition. Her first work was <i>R\xc3\xado Grande de Lo\xc3\xadza</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>After Burgos graduated from Mu\xc3\xb1oz Rivera Primary School in 1928, her family moved to <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Piedras,_Puerto_Rico" title="R\xc3\xado Piedras, Puerto Rico">Rio Piedras</a>, where she was awarded a scholarship to attend <a href="/wiki/University_High_School_(San_Juan)" title="University High School (San Juan)">University High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1931, she enrolled in <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico,_Rio_Piedras_Campus" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Puerto Rico, Rio Piedras Campus">University of Puerto Rico, Rio Piedras Campus</a> to become a teacher.\n</p><p>In 1933, at the age of 19, Burgos graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a> with a degree in teaching. She became a teacher and taught at Feijoo Elementary School in Barrio <a href="/wiki/Cedro_Arriba" title="Cedro Arriba">Cedro Arriba</a> of <a href="/wiki/Naranjito,_Puerto_Rico" title="Naranjito, Puerto Rico">Naranjito, Puerto Rico</a>. She also worked as writer for a children\'s program on <a href="/wiki/Public_radio" class="mw-redirect" title="Public radio">public radio</a>, but was reportedly fired for her political beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Among her early influences were <a href="/wiki/Luis_Llor%C3%A9ns_Torres" title="Luis Llor\xc3\xa9ns Torres">Luis Llor\xc3\xa9ns Torres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_Negr%C3%B3n_Mu%C3%B1oz" title="Mercedes Negr\xc3\xb3n Mu\xc3\xb1oz">Mercedes Negr\xc3\xb3n Mu\xc3\xb1oz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Alberti" title="Rafael Alberti">Rafael Alberti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">Pablo Neruda</a>. According to Burgos, "My childhood was all a poem in the river, and a river in the poem of my first dreams."<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Nationalism">Nationalism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julia_de_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nationalism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1934, Burgos married Ruben Rodriguez Beauchamp and ended her teaching career. In 1936, she became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Nationalist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican Nationalist Party">Puerto Rican Nationalist Party</a> (Partido Nacionalista de Puerto Rico), led by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Albizu_Campos" title="Pedro Albizu Campos">Pedro Albizu Campos</a>. She was elected to the position of Secretary General of the Daughters of Freedom, the women\'s branch of the party.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> She divorced her husband in 1937.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Literature">Literature</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julia_de_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literature"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1237879389"><table class="infobox" style="clear: right; float:right;margin:0 0 1.5em 1.5em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" class="infobox-above" style="font-size:115%">External audio</th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" class="infobox-full-data" style="text-align: left"><span typeof="mw:File"><span><img alt="audio icon" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/16px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png" decoding="async" width="16" height="16" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/24px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/32px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="128" data-file-height="128" /></span></span> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JCw9UJl52uo"><span class="plainlinks"> "R\xc3\xado Grande de Lo\xc3\xadza" recited</span></a> on <a href="/wiki/YouTube_video_(identifier)" class="mw-redirect" title="YouTube video (identifier)">YouTube</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>By the early 1930s, Burgos had published her work in journals and newspapers. She published two collections of poetry, <i>Poema en 20 surcos</i> (1938) and <i>Canci\xc3\xb3n de la verdad sencilla</i> (1939) in her lifetime. Her third collection, <i>El mar y t\xc3\xba: otros poemas</i> (1954) was edited and published after her death by her sister, Consuelo Burgos.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For her first two books, she traveled around the island promoting her work by giving book readings.  Her third book was published <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Posthumous work">posthumously</a> in 1954. Burgos\' lyrical poems are a combination of the intimate, the land and the social struggle of the oppressed.  Many critics assert that her poetry anticipated the work of <a href="/wiki/Feminist_writers" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminist writers">feminist writers</a> and poets as well as that of other <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic literature">Hispanic authors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In one of her poems, she writes: "I am life, strength, woman."<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Burgos received awards and recognition for her work and was celebrated by poets including <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">Pablo Neruda</a>, whom she met in Cuba, and stated that her calling was to be one of the greatest poet of the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Among Burgos\' works are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Poema en veinte surcos</i> (1938)</li>\n<li><i>Canci\xc3\xb3n de la verdad sencilla</i> (1939)</li>\n<li><i>El mar y t\xc3\xba: otros poemas</i> (1954)</li></ul>\n<ul><li><span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">R\xc3\xado Grande de Lo\xc3\xadza</i></span><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li><span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Poema para Mi Muerte</i></span>  (My Death Poem),</li>\n<li><span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Yo Misma Fui Mi Ruta</i></span>  (I Was My Own Path),</li>\n<li><span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Alba de Mi Silencio</i></span>  (Dawn of My Silence),</li>\n<li><span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">Alta Mar y Gaviota</i></span></li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Later_years">Later years</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julia_de_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Later in life, Burgos became romantically involved with Dr. <a href="/wiki/Juan_Isidro_Jimenes_Grull%C3%B3n" title="Juan Isidro Jimenes Grull\xc3\xb3n">Juan Isidro Jimenes Grull\xc3\xb3n</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican</a> physician. According to Grull\xc3\xb3n, many of her poems during that time were inspired by the love that she felt for him.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  In 1940, Burgos and Jimenes Grull\xc3\xb3n traveled first to Cuba, where she briefly attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Havana" title="University of Havana">University of Havana</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and then later to New York City, where she worked as a journalist for <i>Pueblos Hispanos</i>, a progressive newspaper.\n</p><p>Shortly after their arrival in Cuba, Burgos\' relationship with Jimenes Grull\xc3\xb3n became strained.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After trying to save her relationship, she instead left and returned once again to New York in 1942, however this time alone, where she took menial jobs to support herself.  In 1944, she married Armando Mar\xc3\xadn, a musician from <a href="/wiki/Vieques" class="mw-redirect" title="Vieques">Vieques</a>. In 1947, the marriage also ended in divorce, lapsing Burgos into further depression and <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>.\n</p>\n<style class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span In the poem she foreshadows her death and reveals an ever darker concept of life.<sup id="cite_ref-CJDB_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CJDB-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Death">Death</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julia_de_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>On June 28, 1953, Burgos left the home of a relative in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, where she had been residing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> She disappeared without leaving a clue as to where she went.<sup id="cite_ref-CJDB_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CJDB-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It was later discovered that in the early morning hours of July 5, 1953, she had collapsed on a sidewalk in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Harlem" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Harlem">Spanish Harlem</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, and she died of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> at a hospital in <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> shortly after midnight on July 6, 1953 at the age of 39. Since no one claimed her body and she had no identification on her, the city gave her a pauper\'s burial on <a href="/wiki/Hart_Island_(New_York)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hart Island (New York)">Hart Island</a>, the city\'s only <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_field" title="Potter&#39;s field">potter\'s field</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CJDB_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CJDB-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Eventually, some of her friends and relatives were able to trace her, find her grave, and claim her body. A committee was organized in Puerto Rico, presided over by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Margot_Arce_de_V%C3%A1zquez" title="Margot Arce de V\xc3\xa1zquez">Margot Arce de V\xc3\xa1zquez</a>, to have her remains transferred to the island. Burgos\'s remains arrived on September 6, 1953 and funeral services for her were held at the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Atheneum" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican Atheneum">Puerto Rican Atheneum</a>.  She was given a hero\'s burial at the Municipal Cemetery of Carolina.  A monument was later built at her burial site by the City of <a href="/wiki/Carolina,_Puerto_Rico" title="Carolina, Puerto Rico">Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Honors">Honors</h2><span
