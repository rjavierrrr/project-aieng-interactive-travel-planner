style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Old San Juan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Viejo San Juan</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Historic_district" title="Historic district">historic district</a> located at the "northwest triangle"<sup id="cite_ref-72001553nom_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72001553nom-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Isleta_de_San_Juan" class="mw-redirect" title="Isleta de San Juan">islet of San Juan</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>. Its area roughly correlates to the <a href="/wiki/Ballaj%C3%A1,_Old_San_Juan" title="Ballaj\xc3\xa1, Old San Juan">Ballaj\xc3\xa1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catedral,_Old_San_Juan" title="Catedral, Old San Juan">Catedral</a>, Marina, <a href="/wiki/Mercado,_Old_San_Juan" title="Mercado, Old San Juan">Mercado</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Crist%C3%B3bal,_Old_San_Juan" title="San Crist\xc3\xb3bal, Old San Juan">San Crist\xc3\xb3bal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_Old_San_Juan" title="San Francisco, Old San Juan">San Francisco</a> sub-barrios (sub-districts) of barrio <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Antiguo" title="San Juan Antiguo">San Juan Antiguo</a> in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>. \n</p><p>Old San Juan is the oldest settlement within <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Historic_district" title="Historic district">historic</a> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">colonial district</a> of the city of San Juan. This historic district is a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Register_of_Historic_Sites_and_Zones" title="Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones">Puerto Rico Registered Historic Zone</a> and a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark District</a>, <b>Old San Juan Historic District</b>, and is also listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">United States National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RNSZH_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNSZH-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Several historical buildings and structures, particularly <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a>, the <a href="/wiki/City_Wall_of_San_Juan" class="mw-redirect" title="City Wall of San Juan">city walls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">El Morro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Crist%C3%B3bal_(San_Juan)" title="Castillo San Crist\xc3\xb3bal (San Juan)">San Crist\xc3\xb3bal</a> castles, have been inscribed in the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> list since 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Historically the mixed-use commercial and residential real estate in the main street<i>s</i> of <i>Calle Cristo</i> and <i>Calle Fortaleza</i> from <i>Calle Tanca</i> to the <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">Governor\xe2\x80\x99s Mansion</a> is the most valuable in the area and it has kept its value and increased steadily through several years despite the past economic turmoils.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Location">Location</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:SJ_aerial_View_1952.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/SJ_aerial_View_1952.JPG/180px-SJ_aerial_View_1952.JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="145" Juan in 1952</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Old San Juan is located on a small and narrow island which lies along the north coast, about 35 miles (56&#160;km) from the east end of Puerto Rico, and is united to the <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">mainland</a> of Puerto Rico by three bridges. It is bounded by the Atlantic Ocean to the north and to the south by <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a> (or "Bahia de San Juan")\xe2\x80\x94which lies between the city and the mainland. On a promontory (Spanish: <i>morro</i>) about 100 feet (30&#160;m) high, at the west end of the island and commanding the entrance to the harbor, rise the battlements of <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">Fort San Felipe del Morro</a>, in which there is a lighthouse.\n</p><p>The "Ca\xc3\xb1o de San Antonio" lies to the southeast, where the island of Old San Juan connects to the mainland through <a href="/wiki/Santurce,_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Santurce, San Juan, Puerto Rico">Santurce</a>, by three bridges, "Puente Dos Hermanos" (Ashford Avenue), "Puente G. Esteves" (<a href="/wiki/Avenida_Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Avenida Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n Avenue</a>) and "Puente San Antonio" (Fern\xc3\xa1ndez Juncos Avenue).\n</p><p>The city is characterized by its narrow, blue <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> streets, and flat-roofed brick and stone buildings, which date back to the 16th and 17th centuries\xe2\x80\x94when Puerto Rico was a Spanish possession. Near Fort San Felipe del Morro, is the <a href="/wiki/Casa_Blanca_(San_Juan)" title="Casa Blanca (San Juan)">Casa Blanca</a>, a palace built on land which belonged to the family of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Origins">Origins</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1508, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a> founded the original settlement, <a href="/wiki/Caparra_Archaeological_Site" title="Caparra Archaeological Site">Caparra</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (named after the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_C%C3%A1ceres" title="Province of C\xc3\xa1ceres">Province of C\xc3\xa1ceres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, birthplace of the then-governor of Spain\'s Caribbean territories, <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando">Nicol\xc3\xa1s de Ovando</a>). The ruins of Caparra are known as the Pueblo Viejo sector of <a href="/wiki/Guaynabo" class="mw-redirect" title="Guaynabo">Guaynabo</a>, behind the almost land-locked harbor just to the west of the present San Juan metropolitan area. In 1509, the settlement was abandoned and moved to a site which was called at the time "Puerto Rico" (meaning "rich port" or "good port"), a name that evoked that of a similar <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> harbor in the island of <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. In 1521, the name "San Juan" was added, and the newer settlement was given its formal name of "San Juan Bautista de Puerto Rico", following the usual custom of <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">christening</a> the town with both its formal name and the name which <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> had originally given the islands, honoring <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (December 2015)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Expansion_and_growth">Expansion and growth</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span title="Casa Blanca (San Juan)">Casa Blanca</a> served as the first fortification of the settlement and residence of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a> descendants, until the mid-eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>La Fortaleza was built between 1533 and 1540, which was followed by the construction of a battery at "the Morro." Plans for the castle portion of San Felipe del Morro were made in 1584. Extensions to the Morro, plus construction of <a href="/wiki/Fort%C3%ADn_San_Juan_de_la_Cruz" title="Fort\xc3\xadn San Juan de la Cruz">El Ca\xc3\xb1uelo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fort%C3%ADn_de_San_Ger%C3%B3nimo" title="Fort\xc3\xadn de San Ger\xc3\xb3nimo">El Boquer\xc3\xb3n</a>, were made between 1599 and 1609. <a href="/wiki/Circumvallation" class="mw-redirect" title="Circumvallation">Circumvallation</a> of the city commenced in 1630 and was concluded by 1641. San Cristobal fort was completed by 1771.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1776, the population totaled 6000, half of which were soldiers. The garrison town of San Juan included 250 acres of military installations and 62 acres of public and private use. By 1781, the city\'s fortifications included 376 cannon. By 1876, 24,000 lived inside the walls of San Juan, encompassing 62 acres, and 926 buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Prior to the 19th century, the area outside the city walls occupying the east side of Old San Juan Island, was almost uninhabited. In 1838 the so-called area of <a href="/wiki/Puerta_de_Tierra,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerta de Tierra, Puerto Rico">Puerta de Tierra</a> (or "Land Gate") had a population of 168 residents, mainly of African descent.<sup id="cite_ref-Historia_de_Puerta_de_Tierra_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_Puerta_de_Tierra-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to a census made in 1846, the population had risen to 223 inhabitants living in 58 houses. On March 3, 1865, the municipal government of San Juan approved a resolution promoting the city expansion across the Puerta de Tierra which included the plan for demolishing the city walls along the eastern side.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On May 28, 1897, the wall demolition was officially started after a proclamation was issued by Queen <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">Maria Christina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Historia_de_Puerta_de_Tierra_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_Puerta_de_Tierra-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By the year 1899, the population of Puerta de Tierra had risen to 5,453; while the area comprising the old walled city had a civilian population of roughly 18,103 inhabitants.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Maintaining_architectural_integrity">Maintaining architectural integrity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Maintaining architectural integrity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>During the late 1940s, disrepair in the old city was evident. The local authorities were considering development proposals for renovating the old city and incorporating modern architecture on new constructions. Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Alegr%C3%ADa" title="Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada">Ricardo Alegr\xc3\xada</a> vehemently advised against the idea of demolishing old colonial buildings in favor of contemporary building designs. He followed the example suggested by his father, a local civic leader who had successfully prevented the demolition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Capilla_del_Cristo" title="Capilla del Cristo">Capilla del Cristo</a></i> in favor of a traffic redesign. He advised mayor <a href="/wiki/Felisa_Rinc%C3%B3n_de_Gautier" title="Felisa Rinc\xc3\xb3n de Gautier">Rinc\xc3\xb3n de Gautier</a> in having local zoning laws changed to favor remodeling and the incorporation of Spanish colonial motifs in any new construction. This helped preserve the city\'s architectural profile, and has been a key to San Juan\'s current status as a tourist destination.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. In 1948 the government invited one of the organizers of Colonial Williamsburg to lead its tourism development efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These efforts were further supported by the 1955 creation of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Puerto_Rican_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Puerto Rican Culture">Institute of Puerto Rican Culture</a> (<i>Instituto de Cultura Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a</i>) head by Alegr\xc3\xada. He sought legal and administrative changes that would allow for major remodeling efforts to be successful. At the time, most real estate in Old San Juan had devalued under appraised values because the city was perceived as unsafe (particularly because of building disrepair and social ills such as <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>) and not profitable for business (because of <a href="/wiki/Rent_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Rent control">rent control</a> statutes, as well as the reluctance of commercial banks to fund remodeling). Under combined efforts by the institute and the Government Development Bank for Puerto Rico, "model" remodelings were made to show potential property owners how their renewed properties could look. Strict remodeling codes were implemented to prevent new constructions from affecting the common colonial Spanish architectural themes of the old city. As with other Operation Bootstrap projects, mainland corporate investors were lured in with tax breaks and other incentives. When a project proposal suggested that the old Carmelite Convent in San Juan be demolished to erect a new hotel, the Institute had the building declared a historic building, and asked that it be converted to a hotel in a renewed facility. As in other Operation Bootstrap projects, the Woolworth family was invited in 1957 to reconstruct the former convent (which had more recently been a low-rent apartment building and city garage) into the luxury <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_El_Convento" title="Hotel El Convento">Hotel El Convento</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Bank provided low interest loans to remodelers, and the government gave triple tax exemptions to commercial activities in the old city (they were exempted for a limited time, not only from property taxes and municipal taxes, but from income tax from activities generated in their properties as well). At least one retailer from the shopping district in Charlotte Amalie was given incentives to set up shop in the <i>Calle del Cristo</i> (the city\'s red light district during the early 1940s) in order to persuade other retailers to follow suit and somehow "clean up" the district. Potential developers were offered sketches of their properties after a remodeling, as to suggest which architectural elements to improve or change. The paradigm to reconstruct and renovate the old city and revitalize it has been followed by other cities in Latin America, particularly <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">Cartagena de Indias</a>.\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-center" Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n</p><p>In 1951, the broader area within the old city walls became the first listing of the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Register_of_Historic_Sites_and_Zones" title="Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones">Puerto Rico Register of Historic Sites and Zones</a> as the Zona Antigua Hist\xc3\xb3rica Casco de San Juan (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>: <i>Historic Old Town Area of San Juan</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-RNSZH_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNSZH-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1972, the same area was recognized by the National Register of Historic Places as the San Juan Historic Zone (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Zona Hist\xc3\xb3rica de San Juan</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-72001553nom_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72001553nom-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2012, the National Register increased the boundaries and renamed the district to Old San Juan Historic District (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Distrito Hist\xc3\xb3rico del Viejo San Juan</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-12000465nom_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12000465nom-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Old San Juan was then designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark_District" title="National Historic Landmark District">National Historic Landmark District</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-13000284nom_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13000284nom-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Landmarks_and_attractions">Landmarks and attractions</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Landmarks and attractions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="La_Fortaleza_and_the_San_Juan_National_Historic_District_sites">La Fortaleza and the <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_National_Historic_Site" title="San Juan National Historic Site">San Juan National Historic District</a> sites</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: La Fortaleza and the San Juan National Historic District sites"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<ul><li><b>San Crist\xc3\xb3bal Castle</b> (<i><a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Crist%C3%B3bal_(San_Juan)" title="Castillo San Crist\xc3\xb3bal (San Juan)">Castillo San Crist\xc3\xb3bal</a></i>), the largest fortification built by the Spanish in the Americas, which provided defense against both land-based and sea attacks by pirates and foreign powers during the colonial period.</li>\n<li><b>San Felipe del Morro Castle</b> (<i><a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">Castillo San Felipe del Morro</a></i>, or just <i>El Morro</i>), a 16th century citadel in the northwestern edge of the islet that provided defense against foreign and pirate attacks during the Spanish colonization, also the site of a <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro_Lighthouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro Lighthouse">historic lighthouse</a>.</li>\n<li><b>Santa Catalina Palace</b>, popularly known as <b><a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a></b> (Spanish for <i>the fortress</i>), a former fortification and currently the official residence of the governor of Puerto Rico. It is the oldest executive residence in the Western Hemisphere.</li>\n<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/City_Wall_of_San_Juan" class="mw-redirect" title="City Wall of San Juan">San Juan City Walls</a></b> surround Old San Juan and it gives the city one of its nicknames, "La ciudad amurallada" (<i>the walled city</i>). About 3/4 of the city walls remain intact but only one of the city gates remain in existence. San Juan is one of the six cities under United States jurisdiction that possessed defensive walls and it is the only US city that still preserves its colonial defensive wall system.</li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Plazas_and_squares">Plazas and squares</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Street_art,_statues_in_Old_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img <i>promenade of the princess</i>) is a famous promenade named after the former La Princesa Prison. It extends for a quarter of a mile and runs along the southern city wall of Old San Juan. It provides views of San Juan\'s city walls and San Juan Bay, and it is a popular location that becomes very lively during festivals and other cultural celebrations. A boardwalk at the end of the promenade connects it to <b>Puerta de San Juan</b> (the San Juan City Gate), formerly known as <b>Puerta de Agua</b> (Spanish for <i>water gate</i>), the only remaining city gate.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Plaza_Col%C3%B3n_(San_Juan)" title="Plaza Col\xc3\xb3n (San Juan)">Plaza Col\xc3\xb3n</a></b> (Spanish for <i>Columbus square</i> or <i>plaza</i>) is located in the eastern end of Old San Juan where the former eastern city walls used to be located. Originally named <b>Santiago Square</b>, the plaza is now a popular meeting place for locals and tourists as it is located next to famous landmarks such as the San Crist\xc3\xb3bal castle and the Tapia Theater.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Armas,_San_Juan" title="Plaza de Armas, San Juan">Plaza de Armas</a></b> is the main town square of San Juan where several administrative buildings such as the historic <b><a href="/wiki/San_Juan_City_Hall" title="San Juan City Hall">San Juan City Hall</a></b> are located. A <i><a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_Armas" title="Plaza de Armas">plaza de armas</a></i> is a large square often found in Spanish-founded cities across the Americas and the Philippines that were originally intended for military parades, hence the name "plaza de armas" (literally <i>arms</i> or <i>weapons square</i>).</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_la_Catedral_(San_Juan)" title="Plaza de la Catedral (San Juan)">Plaza de la Catedral</a></b> (English: <i>Cathedral Square</i>), popularly known as <b>Plazuela Las Monjas</b>, is located south of Hotel El Convento and west of the main entrance to San Juan\'s cathedral.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plaza_de_San_Jos%C3%A9_(San_Juan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plaza de San Jos\xc3\xa9 (San Juan) (page does not exist)">Plaza de San Jos\xc3\xa9</a></b> (English: <i>San Jos\xc3\xa9 Squar</i>e), formerly known as <b>Plaza de Santo Domingo</b>, is located next to San Jos\xc3\xa9 Church. It is also a popular meeting place due to its location next to restaurants and other businesses important to Old San Juan\'s nightlife. The statue of Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n located in the center of the square is famous for being built from melted British cannons obtained after <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Abercromby" title="Ralph Abercromby">Sir Ralph Abercromby</a>\'s failed attempt to capture San Juan.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Plaza_del_Quinto_Centenario" title="Plaza del Quinto Centenario">Plaza del Quinto Centenario</a></b> (English: Square of the Fifth Centenary) is a modern square built to commemorate the 500th year anniversary of San Juan and the European discovery of Puerto Rico and the Americas. The square is located next to the Ballaj\xc3\xa1 Barracks and El Morro.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plazuela_de_la_Rogativa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plazuela de la Rogativa (page does not exist)">Plazuela de la Rogativa</a></b> is a small plaza located close to La Fortaleza that is famous for the bronze sculpture of <i>La Rogativa</i>. The sculpture, completed by <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Lindsay_Daen" title="Lindsay Daen">Lindsay Daen</a> in 1971, portrays "La Rogativa" (Spanish for <i>the pleading</i>), from a legend that says that the British invasion led by Sir Abercromby was foiled thanks to a pleading procession led by the bishop of San Juan and a group of women carrying torches. The story states that the British mistook the procession for Spanish reinforcements.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li><b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parque_de_las_Palomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Parque de las Palomas (page does not exist)">Parque de las Palomas</a></b> (Spanish for <i>park of the pigeons</i>), despite its name is a public plaza rather than a park located at the end of <i>Calle del Cristo</i> next to the <a href="/wiki/Capilla_del_Cristo" title="Capilla del Cristo">chapel</a> of the same name. In addition to the wonderful views of San Juan and the bay that this space offers, the plaza is famous, specially among children, for the numerous <a href="/wiki/Rock_dove" title="Rock dove">pigeons</a> that live there. There are vendors who sell bird food to feed the pigeons.</li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Churches_and_religious_buildings">Churches and religious buildings</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Churches and religious buildings"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg/170px-Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="128" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg/255px-Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg/340px-Capilla_del_Santo_Cristo_de_la_Salud.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3577" data-file-height="2683" /></a><figcaption>Capilla del Santo Cristo de la Salud</figcaption></figure>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Catedral_Metropolitana_Bas%C3%ADlica_de_San_Juan_Bautista_(San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catedral Metropolitana Bas\xc3\xadlica de San Juan Bautista (San Juan, Puerto Rico)">San Juan Cathedral</a></b>, officially the <b>Metropolitan Cathedral Basilica of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">Saint John the Baptist</a></b> (Spanish: <i>Catedral Bas\xc3\xadlica Metropolitana de San Juan Bautista</i>), the Catholic cathedral and one of the oldest buildings in Old San Juan. It is considered the oldest cathedral in the United States and the second oldest cathedral in the Americas (after the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_Cathedral_of_Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_la_Menor" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica Cathedral of Santa Mar\xc3\xada la Menor">Santo Domingo Cathedral</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>). The church was closed for renovation for many years and it reopened for visitors and religious services in 2021.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Church_of_Saint_Francis_of_Assisi,_San_Juan" title="Church of Saint Francis of Assisi, San Juan">Saint Francis of Assisi Church</a></b> (<i>Iglesia de San Francisco de As\xc3\xads</i>) is a Catholic parish church located in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_Old_San_Juan" title="San Francisco, Old San Juan">San Francisco</a> sub-barrio of Old San Juan.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Santa_Ana_Church_(San_Juan)" title="Santa Ana Church (San Juan)">Santa Ana Church</a></b> (<i>Iglesia de Santa Ana</i>) is an often-missed historical 17th century church located on Tetu\xc3\xa1n Street.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Capilla_del_Cristo" title="Capilla del Cristo">Santo Cristo de la Salud Chapel</a></b> (<i>Capilla del Santo Cristo de la Salud</i>), popularly known as <b><a href="/wiki/Capilla_del_Cristo" title="Capilla del Cristo">Capilla del Cristo</a></b>, a small 18th century chapel which was saved from demolition in the 20th century that has become a famous landmark and <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Culture of Puerto Rico">cultural symbol</a> of Puerto Rico and its folklore. The chapel also houses a small museum.</li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Museums">Museums</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Museums"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Casa_Blanca_(San_Juan)" title="Casa Blanca (San Juan)">Casa Blanca</a></b> (Spanish for <i>white house</i>) is a house museum located in a historical fortification which was the official residence of Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n and his family.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Casa_de_la_Familia_Puertorrique%C3%B1a_del_Siglo_XIX&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Casa de la Familia Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a del Siglo XIX (page does not exist)">Casa de la Familia Puertorrique\xc3\xb1a del Siglo XIX</a></b> (Spanish for house of the 19th-century Puerto Rican family) is a traditional Old San Juan residence showcasing domestic life during the 19th century.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Casa_Rosa" title="Casa Rosa">Casa Rosa</a></b> (Spanish for <i>pink house</i>) is a historical house and former military barracks which once housed a museum for traditional arts and crafts. Culture of Puerto Rico</b> (<i>Fundaci\xc3\xb3n Nacional Para la Cultura Popular</i>) also hosts art exhibits and educational activities.</li>\n<li>The <b>Power y Giralt House</b> (<i>Casa The building is of architectural importance and it is a member of <a href="/wiki/Historic_Hotels_of_America" title="Historic Hotels of America">Historic Hotels of America</a>.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jose_V._Toledo_Federal_Building_and_United_States_Courthouse" title="Jose V. Toledo Federal Building and United States Courthouse">Jose V. Toledo Federal Building and United States Courthouse</a></b> is a historic post office building located in the south of Old San Juan famous for its <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Colonial_Revival_architecture" title="Spanish Colonial Revival architecture">Spanish Colonial Revival</a> architecture.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Edificio_Patio_Espa%C3%B1ol" title="Edificio Patio Espa\xc3\xb1ol">Patio Espa\xc3\xb1ol Building</a></b>, also known as <i>La Filarmonica</i> building, is an eclectic Mission/Spanish revival-style building from 1937 which has been listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> since 2005.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Escuela_de_Artes_Pl%C3%A1sticas_y_Dise%C3%B1o_de_Puerto_Rico" title="Escuela de Artes Pl\xc3\xa1sticas y Dise\xc3\xb1o de Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico School of Plastic Arts and Design</a></b> (<i>Escuela de Artes Pl\xc3\xa1sticas y Dise\xc3\xb1o de Puerto Rico</i>) is a tertiary <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">art school</a> founded by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Puerto_Rican_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Puerto Rican Culture">Institute of Puerto Rican Culture</a> located in a historical building and former hospital next to El Morro fortress.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Station_San_Juan" title="Coast Guard Station San Juan">San Juan Coast Guard Station</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">USCG</a> station located in the port of San Juan close to the cruise ship docks. The former <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_of_Lighthouses%27_Dwelling" title="Superintendent of Lighthouses&#39; Dwelling">lighthouse superintendent\'s dwelling</a> still stands and was added to the NRHP in 1981.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_Magdalena_de_Pazzis_Cemetery" title="Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery">Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis Cemetery</a></b> (<i>Cementerio Santa Mar\xc3\xada Magdalena de Pazzis</i>) is a colonial-era cemetery located next to La Perla and El Morro. It is also the final resting place of numerous figures from Puerto Rico\'s history and culture such as nationalist leader <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Albizu_Campos" title="Pedro Albizu Campos">Pedro Albizu Campos</a>, Puerto Rico statehood activist <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Celso_Barbosa" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Celso Barbosa">Jos\xc3\xa9 Celso Barbosa</a>, and actors <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ferrer" title="Jos\xc3\xa9 Ferrer">Jos\xc3\xa9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Ferrer" title="Miguel Ferrer">Miguel Ferrer</a>.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Teatro_Tapia" title="Teatro Tapia">Tapia Theater</a></b> (<i>Teatro <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Tapia_y_Rivera" title="Alejandro Tapia y Rivera">Alejandro Tapia y Rivera</a></i>) is the oldest free-standing theater in San Juan and one of the oldest theaters in the Caribbean. It is located on Plaza Col\xc3\xb3n.</li>\n<li>The <b>Old Casino</b> (<i><a href="/wiki/Antiguo_Casino_de_Puerto_Rico" title="Antiguo Casino de Puerto Rico">Antiguo Casino de Puerto Rico</a></i>) is a former casino and current venue for cultural events and private weddings.</li>\n<li>The former <b><a href="/wiki/U.S._Custom_House_(San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Custom House (San Juan, Puerto Rico)">U.S. Custom House</a></b> (<i>Antigua Aduana</i>), with its pink-colored exterior, is one of the most distinctive buildings in the Old San Juan port area.</li>\n<li>The former <b>San Juan Arsenal</b> (<i><a href="/wiki/Arsenal_de_San_Juan" title="Arsenal de San Juan">Arsenal de San Juan</a></i>) is a historic building complex and former Spanish military installation.</li></ul>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Today">Today</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Today"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure /></a><figcaption>La Casita in Old San Juan</figcaption></figure>\n<p>With its abundance of shops, historic places, museums,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> open air caf\xc3\xa9s, restaurants, colorful homes, tree-shaded plazas, and its old beauty and <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architectural</a> peculiarity, Old San Juan is a main spot for domestic and international tourism.\n</p><p>The district is characterized by numerous public plazas, which are filled with local shops that sell traditional craftwork,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> sculptures and typical musical instruments. Moreover, Old San Juan is known for its historical churches, such as <a href="/wiki/San_Jos%C3%A9_Church" title="San Jos\xc3\xa9 Church">San Jos\xc3\xa9 Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Catedral_Metropolitana_Bas%C3%ADlica_de_San_Juan_Bautista_(San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catedral Metropolitana Bas\xc3\xadlica de San Juan Bautista (San Juan, Puerto Rico)">Cathedral of San Juan Bautista</a>, which contains the tomb of the Spanish explorer and first European to discover modern day <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a>.\n</p><p>Tourists\' main spot to visit at Old San Juan is Castillo San Felipe del Morro, a fort built by the Spaniards when settled on the Island. Besides the magnificent fort, tourists are drawn to <a href="/wiki/La_Perla,_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="La Perla, San Juan, Puerto Rico">La Perla</a>, a colorful neighborhood outside of the historic city wall.\n</p><p>Old San Juan is one of the two <i>barrios</i>, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Santurce,_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Santurce, San Juan, Puerto Rico">Santurce</a>, that made up the <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipality</a> of San Juan after 1864 and prior to 1951, in which the former independent municipality of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Piedras,_Puerto_Rico" title="R\xc3\xado Piedras, Puerto Rico">R\xc3\xado Piedras</a> was annexed.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Blockhouses_and_subbarrios">Blockhouses and subbarrios</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a view of Old San Juan as of 2022</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The oldest parts of the district of Old San Juan remain partly enclosed by massive walls. Several defensive structures and notable forts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Felipe_del_Morro" title="Castillo San Felipe del Morro">Fort San Felipe del Morro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castillo_San_Crist%C3%B3bal_(San_Juan)" title="Castillo San Crist\xc3\xb3bal (San Juan)">Castle of San Crist\xc3\xb3bal</a>, and <i>el <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">Palacio de Santa Catalina</a></i> (also known as <a href="/wiki/La_Fortaleza" title="La Fortaleza">La Fortaleza</a>), acted as the primary defense of the settlement which has been subjected to numerous attacks. La Fortaleza continues to serve also as the executive mansion for the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Governor of Puerto Rico</a>. Many of the historic fortifications are part of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_National_Historic_Site" title="San Juan National Historic Site">San Juan National Historic Site</a>.  Old San Juan\'s land area roughly correlates to the following subbarrios of barrio San Juan Antiguo: Ballaj\xc3\xa1, Catedral, Marina, Mercado, San Crist\xc3\xb3bal, and San Francisco.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Public_transportation">Public transportation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Public transportation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Old San Juan is the location for one of two transportation hubs serving Metropolitan San Juan. At one time, a free trolley provided limited service to Old San Juan seven days a week but is not available any longer. People get around the city on foot or by car.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There have been plans to turn the historic district into a <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_zone" title="Pedestrian zone">pedestrian-only zone</a>, and to either rebuild the trolley system or build a new <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light-rail train</a> system in the recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Old San Juan is the setting for films, books, song lyrics and music videos, including the following:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Noel_Estrada" title="Noel Estrada">Noel Estrada</a>, a Puerto Rican composer, wrote the popular song "<a href="/wiki/En_mi_Viejo_San_Juan" title="En mi Viejo San Juan">En mi Viejo San Juan</a>" in 1943.
