A\xc3\xb1asco</a> in Puerto Rico</figcaption></figure>\n<p><b>Diego Salcedo</b> (died 1511) was a semi-legendary <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a> who is said to have lived during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">colonization of the Americas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to legend, his death at the hands of the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta\xc3\xadno people">Ta\xc3\xadno people</a> ignited the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93Ta%C3%ADno_War_of_San_Juan%E2%80%93Borik%C3%A9n" title="Spanish\xe2\x80\x93Ta\xc3\xadno War of San Juan\xe2\x80\x93Borik\xc3\xa9n">Ta\xc3\xadno rebellion of 1511</a>.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Salcedo\'s_death"><span id="Salcedo.27s_death"></span>Salcedo\'s death</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legend_of_Diego_Salcedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Salcedo&#039;s death"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>According to the story, Salcedo died in 1511, during a trip to Puerto Rico, when <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta\xc3\xadno people">Ta\xc3\xadnos</a>, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Ag%C3%BCeyban%C3%A1_II" title="Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II">Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II</a> (brother of the great Taino Cacique <a href="/wiki/Ag%C3%BCeyban%C3%A1_(The_Great_Sun)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 (The Great Sun)">Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1</a>) and the Cacique of <a href="/wiki/A%C3%B1asco,_Puerto_Rico" title="A\xc3\xb1asco, Puerto Rico">A\xc3\xb1asco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urayo%C3%A1n" title="Urayo\xc3\xa1n">Urayo\xc3\xa1n</a>, drowned him in the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Grande_de_A%C3%B1asco" title="R\xc3\xado Grande de A\xc3\xb1asco">R\xc3\xado Grande de A\xc3\xb1asco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Historically, two versions about how Salcedo was lured to his death have collided. Many books assert that the soldier had been told he\'d be taken to a lake filled with Ta\xc3\xadno women that he could have <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sex</a> with, and, once there, he found not women, but men who then proceeded to drown him. The other version has Salcedo being offered a ride across a river by Ta\xc3\xadnos who carried him on their arms, and then drowned him and kept him for days, afraid he\'d still be alive and waiting until they were certain he was dead.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>A third, and most accepted version of Salcedo\'s death says that the Tainos, fearing that the Spaniards might be gods, refrained from harming them. After suffering under the Spaniards for so long, the Tainos, by order of Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1, ambushed Salcedo as he was drinking water at the edge of a river. Fearing that Salcedo might resurrect after three days&#8212;based on their understanding of the Christian teachings wielded by Catholic priests&#8212;they sat around for three days waiting for Salcedo to come back from the dead, but all they saw was Salcedo\'s body rotting due to the heat just as they would. At that moment the Tainos realized that these were no gods.<sup id="cite_ref-Aqui_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aqui-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>It was with Salcedo\'s death that the Ta\xc3\xadnos were encouraged to declare war on the <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> in Puerto Rico. This led to the Taino rebellion of 1511. However, the Tainos were quickly defeated due to the Spaniards\' better weaponry and war expertise. After the death of Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II, the native warriors retracted and became disorganized.<sup id="cite_ref-S\xc3\xa1nchez,_p._28_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S\xc3\xa1nchez,_p._28-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II\'s followers opted for engaging the Spaniards via guerilla tactics.<sup id="cite_ref-S\xc3\xa1nchez,_p._28_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S\xc3\xa1nchez,_p._28-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Such guerilla warfare lasted for the next eight years, until 1519.<sup id="cite_ref-Oliver,_pp._4,_41_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver,_pp._4,_41-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A second round of raids erupted in 1513 when Ponce de Leon departed the island to explore Florida. The settlement of <a href="/wiki/Caparra_Archaeological_Site" title="Caparra Archaeological Site">Caparra</a>, the seat of the island government at that time, was sacked and burned by an alliance between Ta\xc3\xadnos and natives from the northeastern Antilles.<sup id="cite_ref-Oliver,_p._4_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver,_p._4-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1520 the Ta\xc3\xadno presence on the island had almost disappeared. A government census in 1530 reports the existence of only 1,148 Ta\xc3\xadnos remaining in Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-Yale_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yale-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, oppressive conditions for the surviving Ta\xc3\xadno continued. Many of those who stayed on the island soon died either due to cruel treatment or the <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> epidemic, which attacked the island in 1519.<sup id="cite_ref-Bryan,_p._5_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan,_p._5-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-First_People_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-First_People-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legend">Legend</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legend_of_Diego_Salcedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legend"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>A local legend tells of a ghostly Taino woman, supposedly Salcedo\'s lover, that still haunts the site of his drowning at A\xc3\xb1asco. This belief is exemplified by a verse in the town\'s anthem:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>History tells that here Salcedo was drowned, and that in the fall a female Taino is seen at night.</p></blockquote>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Salcedo_in_popular_culture">Salcedo in popular culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Legend_of_Diego_Salcedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Salcedo in popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Salcedo is referenced in a song of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Fiel_a_la_Vega" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiel a la Vega">Fiel a la Vega</a>. The song is titled "El Asunto: Salcedo Sigue Siendo Mortal" (The Issue: Salcedo is still a Mortal) and it makes a comparison between the <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a>\' rule in the island and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>\' invasion of 1898.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="See_also">See also</h2><span
