colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Sixto Escobar</b> (March 23, 1913 \xe2\x80\x93 November 17, 1979) was a Puerto Rican professional <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxer</a>. Competing in the <a href="/wiki/Bantamweight" title="Bantamweight">bantamweight</a> division, he became <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>\'s first world champion.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Escobar was born in <a href="/wiki/Barceloneta,_Puerto_Rico" title="Barceloneta, Puerto Rico">Barceloneta</a> and raised in San Juan. There he received his primary education and took interest in boxing. After gathering a record of 21\xe2\x80\x931\xe2\x80\x931 as an amateur, Escobar debuted as a professional in 1931 defeating Luis "Kid Dominican" P\xc3\xa9rez by <a href="/wiki/Knockout" title="Knockout">knockout</a>. Early in his career, he moved to Venezuela due to the lack of opponents in his division. There he received an opportunity for the Venezuelan Bantamweight championship, but lost by <a href="/wiki/Unanimous_decision" title="Unanimous decision">points</a> to <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Chaffardet" title="Enrique Chaffardet">Enrique Chaffardet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Subsequently, he moved to New York and began boxing in other states, eventually capturing the Montreal Athletic Commission World Bantamweight Title. In 1936, he defeated <a href="/wiki/Tony_Marino_(boxer)" title="Tony Marino (boxer)">Tony Marino</a> to unify this championship with the one recognized by the <a href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Union_(1913%E2%80%931946)" class="mw-redirect" title="International Boxing Union (1913\xe2\x80\x931946)">International Boxing Union</a>, in the process becoming the third Latin American undisputed world boxing champion. After retiring, he worked as a spokesperson for beer companies in New York, before returning to Puerto Rico in the 1960s, where he resided until his death. He received several posthumous recognitions and his name was used in several sports venues and buildings. In 2002, Escobar was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Boxing Hall of Fame">International Boxing Hall of Fame</a>.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Early_life_and_amateur_career">Early life and amateur career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixto_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and amateur career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Escobar was born in La Boca in Barrio Palmas Altas, a sector of Barceloneta, Puerto Rico, to Jacinto Escobar and Adela Vargas.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Early in his life, he moved to Tras Talleres in Santurce, a subsection of San Juan, Puerto Rico. There he received his elementary and secondary education, up until the seventh grade, when he decided to dedicate himself full-time to his sports career.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Tras Talleres, he began developing an interest in boxing and received instruction in said discipline. Although at this time, boxing was illegal in Puerto Rico; remote places such as a house\'s backyard or rooftops were used to organize clandestine fights without attracting attention from the local police.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1928, a Puerto Rican boxer named \xc3\x81ngel "Sotito" Soto moved from New York to a house near the Escobar family\'s residence and established a gym in his backyard. There he gave boxing classes to Escobar and several other young athletes.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After several months of instruction, Soto prepared a boxing card with included three-round fights, in which each round lasted two minutes. In this event, Escobar earned his first victory, defeating a pugilist identified as "Gombar" by knockout in the first round.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After this match, Escobar continued fighting in clandestine gyms. He met and was received by Ignacio Pe\xc3\xb1agaricano, the owner of Victoria Gym.<sup id="cite_ref-p.21_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.21-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Escobar received his boxing license through Pe\xc3\xb1agaricano. On February 16, 1927, governor <a href="/wiki/Horace_Mann_Towner" class="mw-redirect" title="Horace Mann Towner">Horace Mann Towner</a> legalized boxing and allowed the establishment of organized boxing matches. Professionals would fight Fridays, Saturdays and Sundays which, were considered the best days of the week, while amateurs could compete the remaining days of the week without limitations.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As an amateur, he fought in 23 combats, gathering a record of 21 wins, one loss and a draw.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> His draw was against Ram\xc3\xb3n Rodr\xc3\xadguez of <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Lorenzo, Puerto Rico">San Lorenzo</a> and his only loss was in the hands of <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Monta%C3%B1ez" title="Pedro Monta\xc3\xb1ez">Pedro Monta\xc3\xb1ez</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cayey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cayey, Puerto Rico">Cayey</a>, in a fight that took place in March 1930.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Professional_career">Professional career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixto_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Escobar turned professional on September 1, 1930, in a <a href="/wiki/Fight_card" class="mw-redirect" title="Fight card">fight card</a> organized at the Victory Garden gym in San Juan. In this event, he defeated Luis "Kid Dominican" P\xc3\xa9rez, who entered the fight as the Dominican Republic\'s champion, by knockout in the third round.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Puerto Rico, Escobar fought in 15 contests, before moving to Venezuela after the popularity of professional boxing declined in his home country.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This move was motivated by Roberto Andrade, who provided transportation to the country. In Venezuela, he fought in Caracas, competing against Jos\xc3\xa9 Lugo, Sindulfo D\xc3\xadaz, Rafael "Moralito" Morales, Pica Pica and Sim\xc3\xb3n Ch\xc3\xa1vez among others.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On November 11, 1931, Escobar competed against <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Chaffardet" title="Enrique Chaffardet">Enrique Chaffardet</a> for the Venezuelan Bantamweight Title, but lost by decision. After 17 fights, he returned to Puerto Rico and competed in five cards. But professional boxing suffered another decline in interest, which forced Escobar to work as an accommodator in baseball games three days of the week, generally on weekends.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the meanwhile, Tony Rojas, a friend of his managers Gustavo Jim\xc3\xa9nez and Arturo Gigante, convinced them to release Escobar of the contract which bound them.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since Escobar turned professional while he was still legally a minor, his father had to sign the unbinding agreement. After succeeding in this task, Rojas presented Escobar with a steam boat ticket to New York, which covered his food, clothes and hotel expenses.<sup id="cite_ref-p.22_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.22-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Escobar reached New York in early 1934, intending to "obtain fame and honor" for "[his] motherland", there he was received by Rojas and professional trainer <a href="/wiki/Whitey_Bimstein" title="Whitey Bimstein">Whitey Bimstein</a>. His first fight in the United States took place at the <a href="/wiki/Valley_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Valley Arena">Valley Arena</a> in <a href="/wiki/Holyoke,_Massachusetts" title="Holyoke, Massachusetts">Holyoke, Massachusetts</a> on May 7, 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this contest he was paired against Canadian bantamweight titleholder Bobby Leitham, defeating him by technical knockout in the seventh round.\n</p><p>Escobar\'s manager, Lou Brix and his trainer recognized that Escobar could be close to a championship fight once the incumbent, Panamian <a href="/wiki/Panama_Al_Brown" title="Panama Al Brown">Alfonso Te\xc3\xb3filo Brown</a>, refused to defend the championship against the first contender, which led to it being stripped by the <a href="/wiki/World_Boxing_Association" title="World Boxing Association">National Boxing Association</a> (NBA).<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A rematch was then organized against Leitham. This was an eliminatory card organized by the Montreal Athletic Commission, which was to determine a contender for their vacant Bantamweight World Championship, against first contender <a href="/wiki/Baby_Casanova" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby Casanova">Baby Casanova</a>, who attended the card. After scoring three knockdowns in the fourth round, Escobar connected another punch that injured Leitham, after which Leitham\'s manager surrendered the fight. This marked Leitham\'s last fight. Following this result, the NBA immediately called for a fight between Escobar and Casanova.<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By this time he had gained a reputation as a quick and technical boxer, capable of gathering quick knockouts with body punches if confronted by an unprepared opponent.<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The fight took place on June 26, 1934, and it was organized in <a href="/wiki/Montreal,_Quebec" class="mw-redirect" title="Montreal, Quebec">Montreal, Quebec</a>, Canada. Escobar scored a knockdown in the third round; the fight concluded in the ninth round when Casanova lost by knockout. With this win, Escobar won the Montreal Athletic Commission World Bantamweight Title, thus becoming the first Puerto Rican to win a world boxing championship title.<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This had a significant impact on Puerto Rico, where it was constantly discussed by the general public. Songs, decimas and poetry were written about this accomplishment.<sup id="cite_ref-p.23_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.23-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Athletic_Commission" title="New York State Athletic Commission">New York State Athletic Commission</a> (NYSAC) and <a href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Union_(1913%E2%80%931946)" class="mw-redirect" title="International Boxing Union (1913\xe2\x80\x931946)">International Boxing Union</a> (IBU) of France didn\'t recognize the outcome of this fight, conserving Tony Marino and Brown as their recognized champions.<sup id="cite_ref-p.26_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.26-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Undisputed_world_bantamweight_title">Undisputed world bantamweight title</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixto_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Undisputed world bantamweight title"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>On August 8, 1934, he defeated Eugene Hart by points in a non-title match organized in Canada. This was the first time in his career that Escobar completed a 15-round contest. After this event, he stayed in Montreal expecting to fight against the winner of a matchup between <a href="/wiki/Louis_Salica" title="Louis Salica">Louis Salica</a> and Bobby Quintana, which was scheduled to take place on December of that year. However, Escobar was forced to cancel his plans after suffering a heel injury when he was hit by a car.<sup id="cite_ref-p.24_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.24-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He spent a month in inactivity while recovering, and in the winter he returned to Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-p.24_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.24-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The governor ordered to have all government buildings closed so that public employees could attend a welcoming ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-p.24_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.24-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The event, which took place in <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Old San Juan, Puerto Rico">Old San Juan</a>\'s dock, was followed by a parade.<sup id="cite_ref-p.24_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.24-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Upon arriving in Barceloneta, Escobar was recognized as a "predilect son" of that municipality by the city\'s mayor, which was followed by a week of festivities in his name.<sup id="cite_ref-p.24_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.24-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Escobar pursued a fight with Brown, but his offers were refused while the Panamanian pugilist promoted a campaign protesting the removal of his recognition as champion, demanding to be reinstated.<sup id="cite_ref-p.26_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.26-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>On August 7, 1935, Escobar defeated <a href="/wiki/Pete_Sanstol" title="Pete Sanstol">Pete Sanstol</a>\xe2\x80\x94who once held, in 1931, the Canadian Boxing Federation and Montreal Athletic Commission (MAC) world bantamweight titles\xe2\x80\x94by unanimous decision in 12 rounds.<sup id="cite_ref-p.26_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.26-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, Escobar still wasn\'t considered the division\'s <a href="/wiki/Undisputed_Champion" class="mw-redirect" title="Undisputed Champion">Undisputed Champion</a> because <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balthazar_Sangchili&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Balthazar Sangchili (page does not exist)">Balthazar Sangchili</a> had defeated Panama Al Brown and was recognized by the IBU. Due to this, Sanstol sent a letter to the NYSAC noting that the fight was of eliminatory nature, even though it was recognized as a titular contest by the NBA and MAC.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Wanting to be recognized as such, Escobar pursued a title unification fight against Tony Marino, who had defeated Sangchili by knockout in his previous fight.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He won the fight by knockout in the tenth round, receiving the "undisputed world champion" \ndistinction.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On November 13, 1935, Escobar became the first Puerto Rican to win a title fight in the first round, defeating "Indian" Quintana by knockout.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>This outcome made it possible to organize a fight against Louis Salica, it was the first time that Escobar fought a title fight in Puerto Rico.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The card was promoted by Arturo Gigante and Gustavo Jim\xc3\xa9nez Sicard\xc3\xb3. Escobar won the fight by unanimous decision, while <a href="/wiki/Jack_Dempsey" title="Jack Dempsey">Jack Dempsey</a> served as referee.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He participated in Mike Jacobs\'s "Carnival of Champions" on September 23, 1937 and lost the title to <a href="/wiki/Harry_Jeffra" title="Harry Jeffra">Harry Jeffra</a> by unanimous decision.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Shortly after, he recovered from this loss and he returned to the gym; he defeated Johnny Defoe in a preparatory contest.<sup id="cite_ref-p.27_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.27-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On February 20, 1938, Escobar faced Jeffra in a rematch organized at the <i>El Escambr\xc3\xb3n</i> baseball park in <a href="/wiki/Puerta_de_Tierra,_Puerto_Rico" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerta de Tierra, Puerto Rico">Puerta de Tierra</a>. He won the contest by unanimous decision, scoring three knockdowns.<sup id="cite_ref-p.28_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.28-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This became an early example of fights that attracted the attention and criticism of boxing analysts, particularly those in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-p.28_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.28-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  On April 2, 1939, Escobar defeated Kayo Morgan before a sold-out crowd of 15,000 at the <i><a href="/wiki/Estadio_Sixto_Escobar" title="Estadio Sixto Escobar">Estadio Sixto Escobar</a></i>, which had been inaugurated three years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-p.28_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.28-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After this fight, he received <a href="/wiki/The_Ring_(magazine)" title="The Ring (magazine)"><i>The Ring</i></a> Bantamweight Championship, which was presented by the magazine\'s editor, <a href="/wiki/Nat_Fleischer" title="Nat Fleischer">Nat Fleischer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p.29_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.29-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Later in 1939, Escobar received a draft notice from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, ordering him to take a physical exam for possible military service. He and his manager presented the issue to the Puerto Rico Boxing Commission, managing to receive a postponement of two months before surrendering his title.<sup id="cite_ref-p.29_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.29-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On April 6, 1940, Escobar fought Sim\xc3\xb3n Ch\xc3\xa1vez for ten rounds in what ended as a draw. Escobar then fought some short non-title fights, of which he lost four, all by points.<sup id="cite_ref-p.29_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.29-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On December 2, 1940, Escobar participated in his last professional fight, losing to a ten-round decision to Harry Jeffra. Throughout his career Escobar avoided being knocked down or out in any fight, all of his losses being by decision. In April 1941, he was drafted to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and participated in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p.29_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.29-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Retirement,_death_and_legacy"><span id="Retirement.2C_death_and_legacy"></span>Retirement, death and legacy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixto_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Retirement, death and legacy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Escobar_Statue.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Escobar_Statue.jpg/220px-Escobar_Statue.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="302" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/82/Escobar_Statue.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="289" data-file-height="397" /></a><figcaption>Statue of Sixto Escobar, located in front of the Sixto Escobar Stadium in Old San Juan, Puerto Rico</figcaption></figure>\n<p>After his military service, Escobar confronted problems to meet the bantamweight\'s limit and retired.<sup id="cite_ref-p._11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He became a spokesman for a liquor brand and relocated to New York temporally, with his family. Subsequently, he returned to Puerto Rico where he worked selling liquor in bars. In May 1947, Escobar and Jim\xc3\xa9nez Sicard\xc3\xb3 co-promoted a fight with the intention of bringing fans back to boxing.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the main event, Diego Sosa was matched against Francisco Col\xc3\xb3n Garc\xc3\xada. Escobar was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a>\'s Boxing Hall of Fame in 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Escobar continued in his job as a liquor salesperson, during which it was common for liquor salesmen to drink a little bit of the product which they sold in front of potential customers.  This contributed to the development of Escobar\'s alcoholic tendencies.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> His alcoholism would later combine with his diabetes and contribute to his death when he was 66 years old. Weeks before his death, Escobar participated in his last ceremony, where Diego Su\xc3\xa1rez, president of <a href="/wiki/V._Suarez_%26_Co." title="V. Suarez &amp; Co.">V. Su\xc3\xa1rez &amp; Company</a>. gave him a recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar,_PR-684,_Barceloneta,_Puerto_Rico.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/220px-Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/330px-Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg/440px-Auditorio_Sixto_Escobar%2C_PR-684%2C_Barceloneta%2C_Puerto_Rico.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3233" data-file-height="2424" /></a><figcaption>Auditorio Sixto Escobar in Barceloneta, Puerto Rico</figcaption></figure>\n<p>After his death, the town of Barceloneta honored his memory by a statue. His remains are interred at Cementerio Municipal Viejo in Barceloneta, according to a memorial written to honor his life. Escobar became the first world boxing champion in history to have a statue in his honor.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On June 9, 2002, Escobar was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Boxing_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Boxing Hall of Fame">International Boxing Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He became the fourth Puerto Rican to receive this distinction and the first to be included in the "Old Timers" section.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In March 2005, a second statue of Estobar was unveiled at the <a href="/wiki/Estadio_Sixto_Escobar" title="Estadio Sixto Escobar">Estadio Sixto Escobar</a> in San Juan.<sup id="cite_ref-p.30_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.30-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Among the celebrities that attended the ceremony were former boxing world champions <a href="/wiki/Julian_Sol%C3%ADs" title="Julian Sol\xc3\xads">Julian Sol\xc3\xads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Escalera" title="Alfredo Escalera">Alfredo Escalera</a>. This statue\'s location served as a remembrance tribute during the 94th anniversary of Escobar\'s birth, when a flower crown was placed at its feet.<sup id="cite_ref-Escobar_vive!_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escobar_vive!-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  During this date, an activity was presented by Puerto Rico\'s House of Boxing in the library of the <i>Pabell\xc3\xb3n de la Fama del Deporte Puertorrique\xc3\xb1o</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Escobar_vive!_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escobar_vive!-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Among those who attended the ceremony  were Sol\xc3\xads, Escalera and <a href="/wiki/Iv%C3%A1n_Calder%C3%B3n_(boxer)" title="Iv\xc3\xa1n Calder\xc3\xb3n (boxer)">Iv\xc3\xa1n Calder\xc3\xb3n</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Escobar_vive!_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escobar_vive!-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On this date, People Inc. presented a documentary covering his life, which was donated to the venue.<sup id="cite_ref-Escobar_vive!_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escobar_vive!-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Several buildings and streets were named after Escobar posthumously, including the <i>Auditorio Sixto Escobar</i>. The house where he lived was later turned into a library and museum bearing his name.<sup id="cite_ref-p.29_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.29-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2019 Sixto Escobar was posthumously inducted to the Puerto Rico Veterans Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Professional_boxing_record">Professional boxing record</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sixto_Escobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Professional boxing record"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<table class="wikitable mw-collapsible">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>66&#160;fights\n</th>\n<th>39 wins\n</th>\n<th>23 losses\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="text-align:left;">By knockout\n</th>\n<td align="center" style="background:#bfd;
