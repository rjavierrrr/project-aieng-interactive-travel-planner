href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a> on a beach in Iceland</figcaption></figure>\n<p><b>Black sand</b> is <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> that is black in color. One type of black sand is a heavy, glossy, partly <a href="/wiki/Magnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a> mixture of usually fine sands containing minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, found as part of a <a href="/wiki/Placer_deposit" title="Placer deposit">placer deposit</a>. Another type of black sand, found on beaches near a <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>, consists of tiny fragments of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>.\n</p><p>While some beaches are predominantly made of black sand, even other color beaches (e.g. gold and white) can often have deposits of black sand, particularly after storms. Larger waves can sort out sand grains leaving deposits of heavy minerals visible on the <a href="/wiki/Erosion_surface" title="Erosion surface">surface of erosion</a> <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpments</a>.\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Placer_deposits">Placer deposits</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_sand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Placer deposits"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_and_black_sands_in_sluice.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Gold_and_black_sands_in_sluice.jpg/220px-Gold_and_black_sands_in_sluice.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" sands and gold in sluicebox, Blue Ribbon Mine, Alaska</figcaption></figure>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnetic_separator_hg.jpg" of black sand by hand</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Black sands are used by miners and prospectors to indicate the presence of a placer formation. <a href="/wiki/Placer_mining" title="Placer mining">Placer mining</a> activities produce a concentrate that is composed mostly of black sand.  Black sand concentrates often contain additional valuables, other than precious metals: <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> and others are often fractionated during <a href="/wiki/Igneous_differentiation" title="Igneous differentiation">igneous</a> processes into a common mineral-suite that becomes black sands after weathering and erosion.\n</p><p>Several <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topaz" title="Topaz">topaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">ruby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> are found in placers and in the course of placer mining, and sands of these gems are found in black sands and concentrates.  Purple or ruby-colored garnet sand often forms a showy surface dressing on ocean beach placers.\n</p><p>An example of a non-volcanic black sand beach is at Langkawi in Malaysia.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Basalt_fragments">Basalt fragments</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_sand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basalt fragments"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Sand_Forming.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Black_Sand_Forming.jpg/220px-Black_Sand_Forming.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Black_Sand_Forming.jpg/330px-Black_Sand_Forming.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Black_Sand_Forming.jpg/440px-Black_Sand_Forming.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a><figcaption>Black sand forming when lava hits ocean. <a href="/wiki/K%C4%ABlauea" title="K\xc4\xablauea">K\xc4\xablauea</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>.</figcaption></figure>\n<p>When <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> contacts water, it cools rapidly and shatters into sand and fragmented debris of various size.  Much of the debris is small enough to be considered sand.  A large lava flow entering an ocean may produce enough basalt fragments to build a new black sand beach almost overnight.  The famous "black sand" beaches of Hawaii, such as <a href="/wiki/Punalu%27u_Beach" class="mw-redirect" title="Punalu&#39;u Beach">Punalu\xca\xbbu Beach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kehena_Beach" title="Kehena Beach">Kehena Beach</a>, were created virtually instantaneously by the violent interaction between hot lava and sea water.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since a black sand beach is made by a lava flow in a one time event, they tend to be rather short lived since sands do not get replenished if currents or storms wash sand into deeper water. For this reason, the state of Hawaii has made it illegal to remove black sand from its beaches. Further, a black sand beach is vulnerable to being inundated by future lava flows, as was the case for Hawai\xca\xbbi\'s <a href="/wiki/Kaim%C5%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaim\xc5\xab">Kaim\xc5\xab</a>, usually known simply as Black Sand Beach, and <a href="/wiki/Kalapana,_Hawaii" title="Kalapana, Hawaii">Kalapana</a> beaches.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An even shorter-lived black sand beach was Kamoamoa.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Unlike with white and <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">green sand</a> beaches, walking barefoot on black sand can result in burns, as the black sand absorbs more <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div
